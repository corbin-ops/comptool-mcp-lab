I. BUSINESS MODEL & CORE STRATEGY
Buy vacant rural land at ~50% of market value. Sell slightly below market with elite drone marketing. Target 7–90 day
sales (~2 weeks average). Primary markets: Mid-US to East Coast. Focus: Rural recreational land.
The Strategic Purpose of Comping
Primary goal: Know the market value so we can offer 50 cents on the dollar.
Backup strategy: Value-add plays. If the seller says NO to the 50% offer on as-is value, we may have identified a
value-add (subdivision potential, etc.). We pitch a DIFFERENT offer based on the value-add — 50% of the improved
value — which might equal the seller's ask. The seller doesn't realize the upside; we unlock it.
Example:
 As-is market value: $100K → Offer $50K → Seller says NO, wants $80K
 Subdivision potential identified: $160K after split
 New pitch: Offer $80K (50% of $160K) = exactly what seller wanted
 We still get 50% margin on the VALUE-ADD play
Why we always look for value-adds: Even if we don't need it for the primary offer, it's the BACKUP strategy when
sellers say no. Most sellers don't know their property can be subdivided. We unlock value they didn't know existed.

III. LAND TYPE IDENTIFICATION
Farmland (Yielding Asset — Worth More)
Actively farmed with visible crop rows, irrigation patterns, tilled soil, distinct field boundaries. This is a YIELDING
ASSET — it produces income. The price of the land PLUS the value of the agricultural production is baked in. Worth
significantly MORE than cleared or pasture land. Always identify and call out farmland — it commands a premium
that other land types do not get. If you see crop rows from satellite, that is farmland. If there is an active farm lease,
factor that into value.
Farmers usually know what land is worth. Harder to get at deep discount because of income potential. Warn the
investor that the seller may be more savvy on pricing.
Pasture Land
Open grass with fencing visible, possibly cattle trails, watering areas, livestock signs. Maintained but not actively
cultivated. Worth less than farmland but more than raw timbered land. Key identifiers: fencing (visible on satellite),
grazed grass areas (not just mowed), sometimes visible livestock or water troughs.
Maintained Cleared Land — THE TRICKY ONE
[!] This distinction is worth thousands of dollars in valuation accuracy.
Green, flat, mowed/maintained but NOT farmed and NOT grazed. No crop rows, no fencing, no livestock. Looks like
farmland from satellite but ISN'T — no yielding asset premium. From satellite it can LOOK exactly like farmland
because it is open and green. When confused, ALWAYS use Google Street View or Google Earth to confirm
actual land use. Satellite alone can be extremely misleading.
Timbered Land
Mature tree cover with value in standing timber. Dense, uniform canopy, established growth. Standing timber can add
$2,000–$15,000+ depending on species, maturity, and acreage. Always mention timber value to buyers as a bonus.
Most wooded land is just 'wooded land' — don't automatically assume timber adds value. Only flag as managed
timber if you see clearly replanted rows of trees (pine plantations, etc.), uniform species and spacing, looks like an
investment/farming operation, ready to harvest.
Clear-Cut Land
Recently logged — debris, irregular patterns, logging roads, stark contrast with surrounding timber. This is a
temporary state. Distinguish from intentionally maintained cleared land — clear-cut is messy, cleared is clean. Takes
5–30% off value. Looks ugly, not ready to build (stumps, debris), not wooded (no privacy/recreation), not cleared (not
clean usable land) — worst of both worlds.
Toggle between map layers (old vs new satellite). Old imagery shows trees, new shows stumps/cleared. Flag it:
'Property appears recently timbered' and discount 5–30% depending on severity.

IV. COMP VERIFICATION & RULES
Source Hierarchy
 Closed (MLS) = Gold standard. Verified arms-length transaction. Use as primary anchors.
 Pending = Discount 5–10% from list price. Hot market = 5%, slow market = 10%.
 REJECT: Off-market and Sold–Public Records. May be family transfers, partial interest sales, tax sales —
not arms-length.
 Never use AI Comp, Zestimate, or any platform estimates.
Never Cross County Lines
Each county is its own market. Pink lines on Land Insights = county boundaries = your comp boundaries. Even if a
comp is physically closer across the county line, don't use it. Buyers search Zillow/Redfin/Realtor by COUNTY —
they filter to one county and never see adjacent county listings. Over time, each county develops its own pricing
patterns. Adjacent counties can have DRASTICALLY different prices for the same land.
Acreage Range
 Always click AUTO (not Manual)
 If not enough comps: below=80%, above=150%
 Always pull wide acreage range to see the PPA curve shape
APN Lookup
Use when listing location seems off or photos are missing. Land Insights aggregates data — pins can be wrong. APN
lookup shows TRUE parcel location, boundaries, flood/wetlands. Always keep original tab open, open NEW tab for
APN search.
Comp Evaluation Depth
 Check description, DOM, full listing history (listed/delisted = red flag)
 Weight comps closer in acreage + proximity more heavily
 Use wider range comps to verify the story
 Open all comps in own tabs, evaluate simultaneously
Comp Quality Awareness
Not all waterfront/golf course/amenity comps are equal. A marshy, wetland-heavy 'waterfront' comp that sold low is
NOT comparable to a clean waterfront. A flood zone riverfront comp is NOT a true reflection of clean riverfront.
Always assess QUALITY of the comp's features vs subject. Pull APN from Zillow/Redfin and check in Land Insights
for soil survey, wetlands, flood zone if needed to verify comp quality before using it in your analysis.
Comp Listing Photos Warning
[!] Photos in MLS listings may show subdivision/HOA amenities (clubhouse, tennis courts, pool, lake, golf
course) that are NOT on the actual parcel.

These are community amenities, not parcel features. Always verify what is on the ACTUAL property using
satellite/APN/Zillow. Don't inflate comp values based on amenity photos.

V. ACREAGE TO PPA RELATIONSHIP — THE EXPONENTIAL
CURVE
This is NOT a linear relationship. It's EXPONENTIAL. The curve drops STEEPLY from small acreage to medium,
then flattens out.
Example Pattern (varies by market):
 1 acre: $15,000/ac
 2 acres: $12,000/ac
 5 acres: $8,000/ac
 10 acres: $5,500/ac
 20 acres: $4,000/ac
 40 acres: $3,200/ac
 80 acres: $2,800/ac
The Inflection Point — WHERE THE MONEY IS
The steepest part of the curve = the inflection point. This is where subdivision makes the most money. Buy at the
lower PPA (larger acreage), subdivide into higher PPA lots.
Steep Curve (Good Subdivide Market)
Huge PPA difference between small and large lots. Example: 5 acres = $10,000/ac, but 40 acres = $3,000/ac.
Subdividing 40 acres into 8×5-acre lots = massive value creation.
Flat/Linear Curve (Bad Subdivide Market)
Small PPA difference between lot sizes. Example: 5 acres = $5,000/ac, 40 acres = $4,500/ac. Subdividing doesn't
create much value — not worth the hassle. Focus on simple flips.
How To Identify Market Type
Pull comps across a WIDE acreage range (2 to 80+ acres). Plot them mentally or visually. Steep/exponential =
subdivide opportunity. Flat/linear = simple flip only.
Regulations can kill a good market. Maryland example: Looks like great exponential curve, BUT strict regulations
(perc tests, complex approvals) add cost, time, and risk. Always factor in regulatory environment.

VI. COMP WEIGHTING — HOW TO THINK ABOUT IT
Core Principle: Not all acres are equal. You're comping USABLE land and CONVENIENCE, not just raw acreage.
Key Differentiation Factors
 Access / road frontage
 Shape of the parcel
 Usable vs unusable portions
 Convenience of movement on the property
Access & Road Frontage
More road frontage = more value. Can access from multiple points, more flexibility for building, easier to subdivide,
more convenient to enter/exit.
Limited frontage (flagpole lots): Narrow access strip (30–50 ft) leading to larger rear area. That 2-acre easement
on a 10-acre lot = really only 8 usable acres. Farther from road = less convenient. Costs money to build driveway
through to the buildable area. DISCOUNT vs same acreage with full road frontage.
Shape — Convenience Factor
Good shapes: Square or rectangular = easy to use, fence, build, subdivide. Bad shapes: Long skinny strips,
irregular shapes with unusable corners, flagpole lots.
How To Comp Flagpole / Limited Access Lots
 Identify the USABLE portion of the property
 Recognize the access strip adds minimal value
 Factor in inconvenience of being far from road + driveway cost
 Example: 10-acre flagpole with 2-acre access strip → might comp closer to a 7-acre lot with good frontage
Primary Anchor Approach
Find the comp MOST similar to your subject — same road type, similar acreage, similar condition, same ACCESS
type. This is your primary pricing anchor. Then adjust: better road = add value, worse road = subtract, better access =
add, flagpole = subtract, cleared vs wooded = adjust, larger acreage = lower PPA, etc.
Comping Lesson — Boon MI (Critical)
Don't limit yourself to immediate nearby comps if they are all large acreage. Look WIDER in the county for
similar-acreage comps to get accurate PPA. Compare land types across comps: if inferior land (pasture, timbered,
dirt road) in a slightly nicer area trades at $X/ac, then farmland (yielding asset) should be equal or MORE.
Specific example: 9.89ac farmland in Boon, MI (Wexford County). Initially valued at $50,000 based on nearby
large-acreage comps (37.5ac at $2,400/ac). Looking wider: W 48th Rd — 10ac timbered/dirt road = $110,000
($11,000/ac). W 46th Rd — 5ac pasture = $50,000 ($10,000/ac). Both INFERIOR to our farmland. Revised to
$80,000 (~$8,090/ac).
Framework: Pick comps nearest in acreage. See what the location looks like — nicer, same, or worse than subject?
Use other comps to construct the full story. If comps are in a nicer area, discount slightly. If subject has advantages

VII. DOM INTERPRETATION
Low DOM (Under 30 Days) — CRITICAL INSIGHT
 Property likely sold UNDER market value — use as FLOOR, market value is likely higher
 This is how YOUR listings should sell (1–2 weeks)
 Use to calibrate disposition pricing: If comp sold in 14 days at $45K, that's a realistic list price → Work
backwards: Offer at 50% ($22.5K)
Normal DOM (30–90 Days)
Standard market transaction. Use at face value, no adjustment needed.
High DOM (90–180+ Days)
Still 100% valid comps — someone actually paid that price. But it took a while, may have been slightly overpriced
initially. Optional: shave 3–5% if 150–200+ days.
Always check price history. DOM shown may be TOTAL days, not days from last price drop. Multiple price drops =
overpriced initially.
Very High DOM (180+ Days)
Something was wrong — price, terms, or property issues. Check if bundle deal or had terms requirements. Use
cautiously, investigate before relying on it.

VIII. STRUCTURES — CALL THEM OUT, DON'T PRICE THEM
A structure could add $2,000 or $20,000 — impossible to know from satellite. A dilapidated house could be a
LIABILITY (demolition costs). ALWAYS call out if a property has a structure visible. Note it as 'has structure —
needs seller verification.' Price the land AS IF no structure exists. Let the investor call the seller to determine actual
value/condition.
Structures on Comps
 Nice metal warehouse/barn: $10K–$60K+ value. Weight as SUPERIOR / CEILING. Mentally subtract
estimated structure value.
 Small house or cabin: Look up on Zillow. Could be $20K–$100K+. Weight heavily as superior.
 Dilapidated structure (junk): LIABILITY. Costs $5K–$10K+ to remove. Mark as SAME or 5% LESS than raw
land.
Don't use comps with structures as direct 1:1 comps. Mentally adjust for structure value.

IX. WATERFRONT VALUATION — CRITICAL PREMIUM
FEATURE
[!] ALWAYS Verify Water Features Yourself
NEVER assume ponds/lakes/creeks exist just because Land Insights shows surface water or wetlands overlay. Could
be swamp, seasonal pond, seasonal creek, or wetlands that look like water but aren't.
MANDATORY PROCESS: 1) Turn OFF all filters. 2) Look at actual satellite imagery with your own eyes. 3) Check
Google Maps to verify water is actually there. 4) Land Insights MISSES small ponds too — always look yourself. 5)
Trust your own eyes, not the filters.
If it even slightly looks like there might be a pond/creek, give BOTH waterfront and non-waterfront evaluations.
Waterfront Hierarchy
 1. Lake — BEST, especially nice lakes. Highest premium, best for recreation (boating, fishing, swimming).
 2. River — Big, flowing rivers worth more than ponds. Depending on lake quality, could be comparable.
 3. Pond — Size matters significantly. Nice, clean, year-round, fishable pond is RARE and commands
significant premium.
 4. Creek — Smallest premium. Watch out for seasonal creeks that may not be flowing.
Waterfront Premium Multiplier
Waterfront commands 2–3x premium over non-waterfront land in the same area. Example: If raw land without water
goes for $8,000–$10,000/ac, pond front with amenities could be $20,000–$25,000/ac or more.
Deeded Water Access (Not Direct Frontage)
Worth ~80% of waterfront premium if real usable easement (50ft deeded lake access = essentially waterfront).
Narrower/less usable easements = closer to 50%. Linear feet of frontage significantly changes value.
Usable Shoreline vs Unusable
Nice beach/gentle slope to water = maximum premium. Steep/swampy/unusable shoreline = reduced premium.
Compare to other waterfront comps to gauge quality.
Water Feature Size — The Acreage Problem
Bigger water = better — that's why lakes beat ponds. BUT if the pond/lake takes up too much of the property's usable
acreage, discount for lost usable land. Same logic as flagpole easements. You're comping based on usable acreage
plus the water premium, not total acreage at full waterfront rates.
Assume Waterfront If Close
If property butts up to waterfront LLC/owner land, is very close to a lake/pond, or has state land between it and water
— always evaluate as waterfront AND give non-waterfront value. Incentivizes asking seller about easements/access.

X. MOBILE HOMES ON LAND
[!] NEVER trust Land Insights structure data.
Example: 113 Double Creek Rd showed '0 sqft structures' and 'Mobile Home: No' but Street View clearly showed a
singlewide. Always verify with Street View (mandatory), Zillow lookup, Google Maps satellite.
Mobile Home Value Ranges
Older mobile homes (1980s): 950 sqft, 2bd/1ba: ~$25,000–$35,000 depending on condition. Factors: year built,
square footage, bedrooms/bathrooms, condition, permanent foundation, utilities connected. Investor needs to verify
condition with seller.
Comping Land with Mobile Homes
 Step 1: Comp the LAND separately as if raw land. Apply waterfront/road/access adjustments.
 Step 2: Add mobile home value separately based on age, size, bed/bath. Note condition needs verification.
 Step 3: Total = Land Value + Mobile Home Value
Example: Land (3.1ac pond front): $80,000 + Mobile home (1980, 950sf, 2/1): $30,000 = Total MV: $110,000

XI. ROAD TYPE HIERARCHY
 1. Paved county road = BEST for most buyers
 2. Nice maintained dirt/gravel road = Good for recreational land
 3. Seasonal/unmaintained dirt road = Discount
 4. Two-track = Significant discount
 5. Highway frontage = DISCOUNT (noise, no privacy = negative)
Why Highway Frontage Is BAD
Constant traffic noise, no privacy, less desirable for recreational/residential use. Buyers pay LESS, not more, for
highway exposure.
Inferring Road Type Without Street View
Look at nearby SOLD comps on same road. Check satellite for road width and condition. Gravel/maintained shows as
lighter, wider path. Two-track shows as faint lines through trees.

[PAGE 18]
XII. LANDLOCKED vs FLAGPOLE vs DEEP EASEMENT
It's not binary — there's a spectrum. Land Insights flags 'Landlocked: Yes' but this is NOT always accurate. Always
provide BOTH values if landlocked is flagged.
The Spectrum
 1. Truly Landlocked (No Access): No legal access at all. Offer 10% of market value. High risk, high hassle.
 2. Landlocked But Right Next To Road: Probably has easement or data error. Verify with seller. If easement
exists = normal pricing. If truly no access = 10%.
 3. Long Easement Through Neighbor's Property: Legal access but LONG driveway/easement. Between
flagpole and landlocked pricing. Consider: How far? Through how many properties? Cost to build driveway?
 4. Flagpole Lot (Short Easement): Narrow access (30–50 ft) to buildable rear area. Inconvenient but
workable. Discount for usable acreage + inconvenience.
Deliverable Format
MARKET VALUE (assuming access): $50,000
Offer Range: $25,000 (50%) - $35,000 (70%)
[!] LANDLOCKED FLAG — If truly landlocked: Offer ~$5,000 (10%)
Verify access situation with seller

XIII. WETLANDS EVALUATION
Core question: Can it perc? If no = 50–80% value killer.
Location of Wetlands Matters
 Back of property, under 20%: Barely discount (~5%)
 Back of property, ~50%: 10–20% discount
 Front/road: 50–80% discount — can't build near road, very inconvenient
 100% wetlands hunting property: Comp against similar wetland sales, discount 30–40%
Small buildable corner: Doesn't save the whole property. Just helps septic placement. Property still priced as
overwhelmingly wetlands.
How To Evaluate
 Check wetlands % in Land Insights
 Look at satellite — WHERE are the wetlands?
 Is there a clear buildable area near the road?
 Compare to other wetland properties in the market
 Note for investor — they may need to verify perc test potential

[PAGE 20]
XIV. SLOPE — WHEN IT MATTERS
 Under 10%: No issue, price normally
 10–20%: Minor discount, 5–10%
 20–35%: Noticeable discount, 15–30%
 35%+: Major discount, 30–50% — building is difficult/expensive
Why slope matters: Harder to build, may need retaining walls/special foundations, septic harder to install, driveway
construction more expensive, less usable flat area.
CRITICAL: Pull up APN for COMPS and check THEIR slope. Compare subject slope to comp slopes. If comps have
similar slope, price similarly. If subject is steeper, discount accordingly.

XV. FLOOD ZONE — DON'T PANIC, LOOK AROUND FIRST
[!] Do NOT panic-discount for flood zone.
FIRST look around — are people building houses in the area in the same flood zone? If yes, flood zone is just a cost
factor (insurance, raised foundation), NOT a value killer.
Edisto Island Lesson (Critical)
Mary Seabrook Rd, Charleston Co, SC: 5.64ac, 79% wetlands, 100% flood zone. Initial valuation was $75,000
(60–70% discount from clean comps). WRONG. The ENTIRE island is in a flood zone and everyone builds there.
Houses were visible on and near the property. Correct approach: recognize flood zone as cost factor, not deal killer.
Look around FIRST before discounting.
Process
 Toggle hazards OFF to see actual property (structures, land type, water features)
 Toggle ON to check flood/wetland zones
 Always check for structures on or near the property
 Different flood zone types matter
 Coastal areas commonly have 100% flood zone but entire communities are built within them

XVI. SUBDIVISION SHAPE RULE — BOWLING ALLEY LOTS
Width-to-length ratio: Golden ratio = 1:2 to 1:4. Max acceptable = 1:5 or 1:6. Skinnier = 'bowling alley lot' =
significant value loss.
Why: Can't build near road because lot is too narrow. Forced to go deep into property — long driveway, utilities run
farther, building site far from road. Wider lots = more accessible from road = more usable = more valuable.
Before proposing ANY subdivision, check if splitting creates bowling alley lots — if yes, NO value-add. Also
consider if the local market supports long/skinny lot sales. Too rural + too skinny = no market.
Lesson (Buckley Rd, Susquehanna Co, PA): 38.37ac already long and narrow. Splitting would create two
unsellable bowling alley lots in a rural area with zero demand for that shape. No subdivision recommended.

XVII. VIEWS / ELEVATION PREMIUM
 Mediocre view: ~5% premium
 Good view: 10–15%
 Superman/panoramic view: 20–30%
Rare to find true great views — still need to build road up, clear trees, make a pad. Don't overweight this — just flag in
Extra Notes if contour lines/elevation show high position with potential views.

[PAGE 24]
XVIII. POWER LINES & UTILITY EASEMENTS — VALUE KILLERS
High Voltage Power Lines (Big Deal Killer)
Signs: Huge metal towers, buzzing/humming sound, wide cleared easement through property. Can't build underneath
or near them (setbacks = couple hundred feet). Takes up usable space, looks ugly, annoying buzzing noise.
Significantly diminishes value. Flag it immediately.
Utility Poles
Not as bad as high voltage lines. Still annoying if right in front/back yard. Note if visible, but less severe.
Gas Line Easements
Cleared strip/line cut through property. No power towers but obvious easement path. Can't build on it. Check satellite
for cleared strips, toggle map layers, check county utility maps if needed.

XIX. TIMBERED LAND & RECENTLY LOGGED
Most wooded land = just 'wooded land.' Don't automatically assume timber adds value. Comp as normal wooded
property.
When Timber Might Matter
Signs of a true timber property: clearly replanted rows of trees (pine plantations, etc.), uniform tree species and
spacing, looks like an investment/farming operation, ready to harvest (mature, tall, consistent). Owners who actively
manage timber usually know what it's worth — it's a yielding asset, harder to get at deep discount.
Comp the property as normal (as-is land value). If it LOOKS like managed timber, call it out. Let investor determine
actual timber value with seller.
Recently Logged
Takes 5–30% off value. Not ready to build, not wooded, not cleared — worst of both worlds. Toggle between map
layers to identify. Flag and discount accordingly.

[PAGE 26]
XX. ACTIVELY FARMED LAND
Covered in Land Type Identification (Section III). Farms are YIELDING ASSETS. Farmers usually know what land is
worth. Harder to get at deep discount. Signs: crop rows, agricultural patterns, farm equipment, active
planting/harvesting. Comp normally but warn investor that seller may be more savvy on pricing.
XXI. MAP LAYERS — ALWAYS TOGGLE
Different map layers have different dates. Satellite imagery could be 1–5+ years old. Things change: houses built,
timber cut, land cleared.
 Toggle between available map layers. Compare old vs new imagery.
 If discrepancy found: flag it for investor. 'Satellite shows [X] but older/newer layer shows [Y] — needs seller
verification.'
 Quick toggle, note anything obvious, move on.
Seasonality doesn't affect comping — land prices don't change by season. BUT winter/snow = harder to sell (can't
visit property, roads not plowed). This affects HOW LONG it takes to sell, not the PRICE.
XXII. WHEN SELLER ALREADY HAS IT LISTED — RED FLAG
[!] Tell the investor immediately if the property is already listed.
Seller is more knowledgeable about pricing, has talked to a realtor, has a price in their head, psychologically
anchored to list price. Much harder to negotiate a deep discount. Note it prominently, include their asking price, still
provide your market value analysis and 50% offer range. Let investor decide how to approach.

[PAGE 27]
XXIII. CASH VS TERMS — MOSTLY UNKNOWN
You can't tell if a sale was cash or owner-financed — this info isn't in public record data. Owner financing = potentially
higher price (buyers pay more for payment flexibility). If a comp seems high AND mentioned owner financing, note:
'This comp may reflect owner financing premium.' Don't obsess over it.
XXIV. COUNTY BOUNDARIES — NEVER CROSS
Covered in Comp Verification (Section IV). Pink lines = county boundaries = your comp boundaries. Each county is its
own market. No exceptions.
XXV. SPARSE MARKETS — USING WHAT YOU HAVE
When sold comps are limited, you still weight SOLDS more heavily, but actives become more important for context.
Reading Active Listings — Save Ratio
Views-to-Saves Ratio: High save ratio = listing is priced well, generating interest. Low save ratio = overpriced or has
issues. Compare save ratios across actives at different price points. Good ratios = realistic market pricing. Bad ratios
= probably overpriced.
Sparse Market Approach
 Widen acreage range to see the FULL story (1ac to 80+ac)

DewClaw — Part 1: Land Type Identification & Boon MI
 Lesson
 FULLY EXPANDED — How to Identify Land Types and Why It Matters for Pricing
WHY LAND TYPE IDENTIFICATION IS CRITICAL
Land type identification is one of the most critical skills for accurate comping. The type of land — farmland, pasture, maintained
cleared, timbered, or clear-cut — directly affects the market value of a property. Getting the land type wrong means getting the price
wrong, sometimes by tens of thousands of dollars. This is not a minor detail or a footnote in your analysis — it is one of the PRIMARY
drivers of value alongside acreage, road type, and location.
Here is why it matters so much: imagine two adjacent 20-acre properties in the same county, on the same road, with the same shape
and no wetlands. One is actively farmed with crop rows visible from satellite. The other is maintained cleared land — green, flat,
mowed, but no agricultural activity. From satellite at a distance, they might look almost identical. But the farmland is worth 30-50%
MORE because it is a yielding asset producing income. If you comp them at the same PPA, you are either massively overpricing the
cleared land or massively underpricing the farmland. That is a $30,000-$50,000 error on a $100,000 property.
This is why the user taught land type identification early in the training using actual satellite screenshots. The user specifically
showed examples where maintained cleared land LOOKED like farmland from satellite but was NOT farmland. The visual similarity is
what makes this so dangerous — you have to know what to look for and you have to verify with Street View or Google Earth when
you are unsure. Never assume. Always verify.
FARMLAND — YIELDING ASSET — 30-50% PREMIUM
What Farmland Is and Why It Commands a Premium
Farmland is land that is actively being farmed — crops are being grown, harvested, and sold for income. The land is a YIELDING
ASSET, meaning it produces revenue for the owner. This is fundamentally different from every other type of vacant land because the
buyer is not just purchasing dirt — they are purchasing dirt PLUS an income stream. That income stream has real monetary value
that gets capitalized into the land price.
The premium for farmland over similar non-farmed land is approximately 30-50%, with 30% being a reasonable baseline for most
situations. The exact premium depends on factors like the quality of the soil, the type of crops being grown, whether there is an
existing farm lease generating income, the local agricultural economy, and the demand for farmland in that specific area. But as a
starting point, when you identify farmland, you should expect the PPA to be roughly 30% higher than comparable pasture or cleared
land in the same area.
This premium explains why farmland comps should NEVER be used as anchor comps for non-farmland properties. If you are
comping a timbered property and you use a farmland comp as your anchor, you will overprice the timbered property by 30-50%
because the farmland comp includes agricultural income value that your timbered property does not have. Farmland comps should
only be used as CEILING references — they tell you the maximum PPA in the area, but your non-farmland subject should be well
below that ceiling.
How to Identify Farmland From Satellite
Farmland has very distinctive visual signatures on satellite imagery that you should learn to recognize instantly. The most obvious
indicator is CROP ROW PATTERNS — parallel lines running across the fields that show active agricultural operations. These rows
are visible from satellite as regular, repeating lines across the field surface. They can run in any direction but are always parallel and
evenly spaced within a field.
Other indicators include: TILLED SOIL which has a distinctive brown/tan color and rough texture that looks different from grass —
you can see the disturbed earth where plowing has occurred. IRRIGATION EQUIPMENT which creates distinctive patterns — center
pivot irrigation creates large circles visible from satellite, linear irrigation creates long rectangular patterns. DISTINCT FIELD
BOUNDARIES where the farmed area has clean, straight edges separating the crop area from surrounding land types.

[PAGE 39]
The key rule: if you see crop rows from satellite, it is farmland. Crop rows are the single most reliable indicator. No other land type
has parallel rows running across the surface. If you see them, you can be confident calling it farmland and applying the 30-50%
premium.
Farmland in Your Comps — How to Handle It
When you see a comp that sold at a suspiciously high PPA compared to other comps in the area, check if it is farmland. This is one
of the most common explanations for outlier high comps. If the comp's satellite shows crop rows or the listing description mentions
agricultural use, farm lease, or crop production, the high PPA makes sense — the buyer paid a premium for the yielding asset.
In your deliverable, when farmland comps appear: note them as ceiling references, explain that the high PPA is due to the farmland
premium (yielding asset), and clearly state that your non-farmland subject should be priced below these comps. This shows the user
you understand WHY the comp is high and that you are not being misled by it.

[PAGE 40]
IDLE FARMLAND — ONLY 10% PREMIUM
What Happens When Farmland Goes Idle
Farmland that has been idle for a couple of years — no active crops being grown — loses MOST of the farmland premium. The full
30-50% premium disappears because the land is no longer producing income. Without active crops, there is no yielding asset, so the
fundamental reason for the premium no longer applies.
However, idle farmland does NOT drop all the way down to regular cleared land value. It retains approximately a 10% premium over
pasture land. The reason is twofold: First, idle farmland still LOOKS nicer and more presentable than rough pasture or timbered land.
Someone previously invested significant time and money in clearing, grading, and maintaining this land for agricultural use. It is flat,
clean, and well-maintained — it has a professional appearance that appeals to buyers even without active farming. Second, idle
farmland has more POTENTIAL to become active farmland again than pasture does. A farmer could lease it and start planting
relatively quickly because the infrastructure (cleared, graded, potentially irrigated) is already in place. That latent potential has some
value in the market.
The satellite will help you distinguish active farmland from idle farmland: active crops show distinct row patterns — you can see the
parallel lines of planted crops. Idle farmland looks like well-maintained cleared land with no agricultural patterns — it is green and
open but has no rows, no tilled soil, no irrigation patterns. This visual difference corresponds to the pricing difference: crop rows
visible = full 30-50% premium. No crop rows but clean/maintained = only 10% premium over pasture.
PASTURE LAND
What Pasture Land Is
Pasture land is open grass that is maintained for grazing livestock — cattle, horses, goats, etc. It is not actively cultivated for crops
(that would be farmland) but it IS being used for a productive purpose (supporting livestock operations). Pasture is worth less than
farmland because grazing generates less income than crop production, but it is worth more than raw timbered land because it is
already cleared and has utility.
How to Identify Pasture From Satellite
The biggest visual giveaway for pasture land on satellite imagery is FENCING. Look for thin lines running along property boundaries
or through the property — these are fence posts and wire that contain livestock. Fencing is often visible on satellite as faint lines,
especially when the sun angle creates shadows from the fence posts.
Other indicators: OPEN GRASS that looks GRAZED rather than mowed — grazing creates uneven patterns because animals eat
selectively (they prefer certain grasses and avoid others), whereas mowing creates uniform, clean-cut patterns. You might also see
WATER TROUGHS (small dark rectangles or circles in open areas), WORN PATHS where animals walk consistently between water
sources and shade areas, and sometimes the LIVESTOCK THEMSELVES on recent satellite imagery (appearing as small dark dots
scattered across the field).
The combination of fencing + open grass + uneven grazing patterns = pasture. If you see all three, you can confidently identify it as
pasture land. If you see open grass but NO fencing and NO grazing patterns, it is more likely maintained cleared land — not pasture.

[PAGE 41]
MAINTAINED CLEARED LAND — THE TRICKY ONE
Why This Is the Most Dangerous Misidentification
Maintained cleared land is green, flat, mowed or maintained, but NOT farmed and NOT grazed. There are no crop rows, no fencing,
no livestock, no agricultural activity of any kind. Someone intentionally cleared this land and keeps it tidy — maybe they mow it
periodically, maybe they maintain it for aesthetics or future use, maybe they just like having an open field. From satellite, it can look
EXACTLY like farmland — open, green, well-maintained. But it is NOT a yielding asset.
This misidentification is the MOST DANGEROUS because the pricing impact is enormous. If you call maintained cleared land
'farmland,' you will apply a 30-50% premium that does not belong, overpricing the property by tens of thousands of dollars. If a buyer
pays that inflated price, they will quickly realize the land is not producing income and they overpaid. Conversely, if you encounter
actual farmland and call it 'just cleared land,' you will underprice it by 30-50%, and the user will miss out on the true value.
During training, actual satellite screenshots were shown where land that looked IDENTICAL to farmland from above was actually just
maintained cleared land with zero agricultural activity. The land was green, open, flat, and well-maintained — but when you zoomed
in or checked Street View, there were no crop rows, no farm equipment, no irrigation, no evidence of farming whatsoever. It was just
a mowed field. The lesson was stark: what looks green and open from satellite is NOT automatically farmland. You MUST verify.
How to Distinguish From Farmland
The key differences on satellite: Farmland has CROP ROWS — parallel lines across the field showing planted crops. Maintained
cleared land has NO rows — it is uniformly green with no pattern. Farmland may have TILLED SOIL areas (brown/tan patches where
plowing occurred). Maintained cleared land is uniformly green with no disturbed earth. Farmland may have IRRIGATION equipment
visible. Maintained cleared land has none.
When you are unsure — and this will happen frequently because the visual similarity is very high — ALWAYS use Google Street
View or Google Earth to confirm. Drop the pegman on the road near the property and look at the land from ground level. From Street
View you can see: are there crop rows? Is there farm equipment? Are there irrigation systems? Does it look like an active farm
operation or just a mowed field? This ground-level view takes 15 seconds and can save you from a $30,000+ pricing error.
TIMBERED LAND — IDENTIFYING PLANTED VS NATURAL
What Timbered Land Is
Timbered land has mature tree cover with potential value in the standing timber itself. The trees have been growing for years or
decades and have accumulated harvestable wood value. Timbered land is generally worth less per acre than cleared, pasture, or
farmland because: 1) the buyer faces clearing costs if they want to build, 2) the land is not generating agricultural income, and 3) the
timber value is a one-time harvest, not an ongoing income stream. However, the standing timber does add a bonus value of
$2,000-$15,000+ depending on species, maturity, and total acreage.
Planted Timber vs Natural Forest — How to Tell From Satellite
There are two distinct types of timbered land visible on satellite, and they look very different. PLANTED TIMBER (commercial timber
operations like Weyerhaeuser, Rayonier, Plum Creek, etc.) has trees arranged in SELECT LINES — very organized, almost in
perfect rows. This is because the timber company planted them in a specific pattern designed for efficient harvesting. Machines need
to move between the rows, so the spacing is deliberate and uniform. All the trees are typically the same SIZE because they were
planted at the same time. And they are often the same SPECIES (usually pine) because the company planted a single commercial
species.
NATURAL FOREST looks completely different on satellite. Trees grow RANDOMLY in a disoriented pattern — no rows, no
organization. Trees are DIFFERENT SIZES because they grew at different times over years or decades. Trees are DIFFERENT
SPECIES mixed together (hardwoods and softwoods intermixed). The canopy looks irregular, patchy, and varied in color/texture
because of the species mix.
Another clue: planted timber is usually owned by a COMPANY NAME or LLC on the parcel data. When you see a parcel labeled
'Weyerhaeuser Forest Holdings' or 'XYZ Timber LLC' and the trees are in organized rows, it is almost certainly a commercial timber

[PAGE 42]
operation. Natural forest is typically owned by individual names.
Distinguishing Mature Hardwood From Scrubby Pine
From satellite you can also assess timber QUALITY to some degree. Mature hardwood appears as TALL, DENSE canopy with
LARGE individual tree crowns — each tree crown takes up significant space and the overall canopy is thick and uniform. The color
tends to be a deep, rich green. Scrubby pine appears as THINNER canopy with SMALLER individual trees — it looks patchier, more
irregular, and less dense. Pine trees have a different color (often lighter or more yellowish-green) and their crowns are typically
narrower and more conical than hardwood crowns.
Mature hardwood has more timber value than scrubby pine. But don't try to estimate an exact dollar amount from satellite — you
cannot know the specific species, board footage, timber market conditions, or harvesting logistics from above. Just note in your
deliverable that standing timber exists with potential harvest value, and put it in Section 9 (Questions to Ask Seller) so the user can
ask about species, maturity, whether a timber cruise has been done, and recent harvesting activity.
CLEAR-CUT LAND VS MAINTAINED CLEARED LAND
Clear-cut land was RECENTLY LOGGED — trees were harvested and removed. On satellite you will see: DEBRIS scattered across
the ground (tops, branches, slash), IRREGULAR PATTERNS where machinery operated, LOGGING ROADS cut through what was
previously solid canopy, and STARK CONTRAST with surrounding standing timber. Stumps are visible. Equipment marks and rutted
soil may be visible. The overall impression is MESSY and ROUGH.
Maintained cleared land was INTENTIONALLY CLEARED and is kept TIDY. It is clean, uniform green, with no debris, no stumps
visible, no logging equipment marks. Someone maintains it — mowing, perhaps grading. The overall impression is CLEAN and
MAINTAINED. The distinction matters because clear-cut land is a temporary state (the forest will regrow) and looks rough, while
maintained cleared land is a permanent state that someone invests in keeping tidy.
CRITICAL FOR COMP VERIFICATION: Always compare listing photos to current satellite. A comp's listing photos might show
standing timber (the property was timbered when it sold), but current satellite might show the land has been clear-cut since the sale
(trees were harvested after the transaction). This means the comp's PPA reflects TIMBERED LAND value — what the buyer paid for
— but the land condition today is different. If your subject is cleared and this comp is now clear-cut, the comp is less relevant than it
appeared. CALL OUT any differences between listing photos and current satellite in your deliverable.

THE BOON MI COMPING LESSON — FULL STORY
The Property
A 9.89-acre farmland property on W Sherman St in Boon, MI (Wexford County). The property had visible crop rows on satellite,
confirming it was actively farmed — a yielding asset. It was near the small town of Boon with some development nearby. The road
appeared to be decent condition.
The Initial (Wrong) Valuation: $50,000
The initial approach was to pull comps from the immediate nearby area. The closest comps found were large-acreage properties:
37.5 acres at $2,400/ac and similar large parcels. Using these as the basis and applying the PPA curve (our 9.89ac should have
higher PPA than 37.5ac), the initial estimate came out to approximately $50,000 (~$5,050/ac). This felt reasonable based on the
nearby data.
The Problem: Only Looking Nearby
The fundamental mistake was ONLY looking at the immediate nearby area where all the comps were large acreage with low PPA.
Those large parcels had low PPA because of the exponential PPA curve (larger lots = lower PPA) and because they were likely
timbered or pasture — not farmland like our subject. By only looking nearby, the analysis missed the broader county-wide picture that
told a completely different story about what smaller, better-quality properties were actually trading at.
The Correction: Looking Wider in the County
The user corrected this by looking WIDER in Wexford County for comps at similar acreage to the subject. This revealed: W 48th Rd
— a 10-acre timbered property on a dirt road that sold for $110,000 ($11,000/ac). W 46th Rd — a 5-acre pasture property that sold
for $50,000 ($10,000/ac). These were FURTHER from the subject property geographically, but they were CLOSER in acreage and
revealed what the market actually pays for smaller parcels in Wexford County.
The User's Logic — How to Think About This
The user's reasoning was precise and logical: both the 10ac timbered ($11K/ac) and the 5ac pasture ($10K/ac) comps were
INFERIOR to our subject in every meaningful way. They were on worse roads (dirt), they were worse land types (timbered and
pasture — neither is a yielding asset), and they were not farmland. Yet they traded at $10,000-$11,000/ac. If inferior properties in a
slightly nicer, more developed area were getting $10-11K/ac, then our SUPERIOR farmland (yielding asset, better land type) should
be at or near that range.
The user then applied appropriate adjustments: the comp areas were slightly nicer/more developed than Boon, so discount slightly
for the less developed area. But the subject has the farmland premium (yielding asset = 30-50% above pasture), which partially or
fully offsets the area discount. The subject also had town adjacency (near Boon = some convenience value). Weighing all factors, the
user valued the property at $80,000 (~$8,090/ac) — a 60% increase from the initial $50,000.
The Lessons That Apply to EVERY Property
1. Don't limit yourself to immediate nearby comps. If the nearby comps are all large acreage with low PPA, you are getting a
distorted picture. Look wider in the county for similar-acreage comps that reveal what the market actually pays at your lot size.
2. Compare land types across comps. If you find comps that are INFERIOR to your subject (worse road, worse land type, not a
yielding asset), their PPA is your FLOOR. Your superior property should be at or above their PPA. If inferior land trades at $10K/ac,
your superior land should be at or above $10K/ac, adjusted for specific area differences.
3. Use the comps to tell the complete story. Every comp should make sense in the narrative. The 37.5ac comp at $2,400/ac
makes sense because it was large acreage (low PPA on the curve) and probably timbered (lower quality). The 10ac comp at $11K/ac
makes sense because it was small acreage (higher PPA on the curve) in a slightly nicer area. Our 9.89ac farmland at $8,090/ac
makes sense because it is small acreage (high PPA) with a farmland premium, discounted slightly for the less developed area
around Boon.

[PAGE 44]
4. Farmland premium is real and significant. The $30,000 difference between the initial $50K and the revised $80K was largely
explained by failing to recognize and properly value the farmland premium. Farmland at 30-50% above comparable pasture/cleared
land is not theoretical — it is what the market pays. Missing this premium means leaving money on the table or making offers that are
too low to win the deal.
5. The user's framework for area quality adjustment. When a comp is in a slightly nicer area than your subject, discount slightly —
maybe 10-15%. When a comp is in a slightly worse area, adjust up slightly. But don't let area differences override land type

DewClaw — Part 2: Wetlands Evaluation Guide
 FULLY EXPANDED — Nuanced Framework for Evaluating Wetland Properties
WHY WETLANDS EVALUATION MATTERS SO MUCH
Wetlands are one of the most significant value factors in rural land — and also one of the most commonly mishandled. The
temptation is to see '50% wetlands' on Land Insights and immediately slash the value by 50%. But that is NOT how the user thinks,
and it is NOT how the market works. A 40-acre property with 50% wetlands could be worth anywhere from 30% below clean land
value to 70% below clean land value — the range is massive, and the difference comes down to WHERE the wetlands are, WHERE
the buildable ground is, and whether you can creatively split the property to maximize value.
The user's core teaching on wetlands can be summarized in one sentence: DON'T BLANKET-DISCOUNT. Instead, evaluate LOT BY
LOT. But that sentence alone doesn't capture the full depth of what was taught. This section expands on every nuance the user
explained about wetlands evaluation, including the discount framework, the perc test logic, the lot-splitting presentation trick, visual
assessment techniques, and live property examples with the user's exact reasoning.
CORE PRINCIPLE: EVALUATE LOT BY LOT, NOT AS ONE WHOLE
When you encounter a property with significant wetlands (30%+), your first instinct should NOT be to apply a single discount
percentage to the entire property. Instead, imagine you are going to SPLIT the property. Even if you ultimately present it as-is,
thinking through the split forces you to ask the right questions: where is the dry ground? Where would someone build? How big is the
buildable area? Could a buyer reasonably use this property for residential purposes, or is it only good for hunting?
This lot-by-lot thinking is what separates a lazy valuation from an accurate one. A lazy valuation sees '50% wetlands' and says '$X
per acre minus 40%.' An accurate valuation sees '50% wetlands, but the front 15 acres near the road are dry and buildable, the back
15 acres are wet — so the front half is worth near-full value with a minor discount, and the back half is a hunting property.' That
accurate valuation might come out to only a 20-25% total discount instead of 40%, which on a $200,000 property is a
$30,000-$40,000 difference.
The user specifically taught this using the Deaver properties in Dillon County, SC. Both properties had approximately 50% wetlands,
but the user's discounts were 20-30% — NOT 40-50% — because the buildable areas were large enough and positioned well
enough that the properties were genuinely usable for residential purposes despite the high wetland percentage.
THE THREE QUESTIONS TO ASK ON EVERY WETLANDS PROPERTY
Question 1: WHERE Is the Buildable Ground?
This is the single most important question. The LOCATION of the buildable ground relative to the road determines how severe the
wetlands discount should be. Buildable ground at the FRONT of the property (near the road) is the best scenario because a buyer
can build conveniently — short driveway, easy utility connections, immediate access. The wetlands in the back are essentially just
'extra land' that the buyer won't use much anyway. This warrants the SMALLEST discount.
Buildable ground in the MIDDLE of the property is a moderate scenario. The buyer can build, but they need to construct a driveway
through the front wetlands or around them to reach the buildable area. This costs money and adds inconvenience. Moderate
discount.
Buildable ground ONLY in the BACK of the property is the worst residential scenario (aside from no buildable ground at all). The
buyer has to go THROUGH or AROUND all the wetlands to reach the building site. Long driveway, expensive utilities, and the front of
the property — the most visible part — looks like swamp. That first impression kills buyer interest. Heavy discount.
NO buildable ground at all (100% wetlands or only tiny high-ground pockets) means the property has zero residential value. It can
only be marketed as a hunting/recreational property to a specialized buyer pool. Maximum discount.
Question 2: HOW BIG Is the Buildable Area?

[PAGE 46]
Not all buildable areas are created equal. A LARGE buildable pocket — say 10+ acres of dry, flat, usable ground — is genuinely
valuable even if it is in the back of the property. A buyer can work with a large buildable area. They have room for a house, a yard,
outbuildings, and space to work. A SMALL buildable pocket — just a tiny high-ground corner where you can barely fit a septic system
— is worth much less because it is cramped, limited, and feels like you are building on the one tiny island in a swamp.
The user specifically taught this on the 40ac Deaver property. The right lot (~20ac) had wetlands at the front but a HUGE buildable
pocket in the back. The user discounted only 30% — more than the left lot (20% discount, dry at front) but less than a typical
heavy-wetlands discount — specifically BECAUSE the buildable pocket was large. If that same lot had only a tiny buildable corner in
the back instead of a large open area, the discount would have been 40-50%+. The size of the buildable area directly drives the
discount percentage.
Question 3: Can You Split Dry From Wet?
This is the creative value-add question. If the wetlands are concentrated on one side of the property and the dry ground is
concentrated on the other, you might be able to create two lots: one clean lot (the dry portion) and one hunting lot (the wet portion).
This can dramatically increase total value because the clean lot commands near-full market value while the hunting lot adds bonus
value on top. Even though the total wetlands haven't changed, the per-lot presentation is much more appealing to buyers.
If the wetlands are scattered throughout the property and you CANNOT cleanly split dry from wet, you are stuck evaluating the
property as a whole. In this case, the discount ranges from 10-40% depending on where you can build and how big the buildable
areas are.

WETLAND DISCOUNT FRAMEWORK — FULL DETAIL
Under 20% Wetlands in the Back — ~5% Discount
This is essentially a non-issue for pricing. The reasoning: nobody builds in the far back corner of their property. When a buyer visits a
25-acre property and sees 20 acres of clean, usable land at the front and middle, with a small wet area in the far back corner, their
reaction is 'great property with a little wet spot in the back that I'll never use anyway.' The wetlands don't interfere with any realistic
use of the property — the buyer can build near the road, have a big yard, and never even visit the wet corner.
The ~5% discount is more of a formality than a real value impact. It acknowledges that technically the buyer is getting slightly less
usable land than a fully dry property of the same acreage. But in practice, the buyer doesn't care about that back corner, and neither
does the market. Properties with under 20% wetlands in the back sell at essentially the same prices as fully dry properties.
~50% Wetlands with Dry/Buildable Ground at the FRONT — ~20% Discount
This is the most favorable scenario for a property with significant wetlands. The reasoning: the buyer drives up to the property and
sees usable land RIGHT AT THE FRONT, near the road, where building is most convenient. They can envision their house, their
driveway, their yard — all at the front where it makes sense. The wetlands are BEHIND them, in the back half, where they would not
have built regardless of whether it was wet or dry.
The 20% discount reflects the reality that the buyer is only getting about half the usable acreage of a fully dry property. They are
paying for 40 acres but can only practically use 20 acres of it. However, the 20 acres they CAN use are the 20 acres that MATTER —
the front portion near the road. So the discount is relatively modest despite the high wetland percentage. The location of the buildable
ground makes all the difference.
Practical example: on the 40ac Deaver property, the LEFT lot (~20ac) had dry buildable ground at the front near the road. The user
applied approximately a 20% discount to this lot. Even though there were wetlands elsewhere on the lot, the critical front area was
clean and the lot presented well to potential buyers.
~50% Wetlands with Big Buildable Pocket in the BACK — ~30% Discount
This scenario has the buildable ground in a LESS convenient location — the back instead of the front. The reasoning for the higher
discount compared to front-buildable: the buyer has to go THROUGH or AROUND the wetlands to reach the buildable area. This
means a longer driveway (more cost), running utilities farther from the road (more cost), and the building site is far from convenient
access. The front of the property — the part visitors see first — might look wet, which creates a negative first impression.
However, the discount is LESS than you might expect for a half-wetlands property because the buildable pocket is LARGE. A large
open area in the back — say 8-10 acres of dry, flat ground — is genuinely usable. It is not just a tiny corner where you can barely fit a
house. A buyer with vision can see the potential: build an access road through or around the wetlands, and you have a beautiful,
private building site with a big open area surrounded by natural wetlands (which some buyers actually find appealing for privacy and
wildlife viewing).
The user specifically taught this on the 40ac Deaver property. The RIGHT lot (~20ac) had wetlands at the front but a HUGE buildable
area in the back. The user discounted approximately 30%. The key word the user used was 'huge' — the size of the buildable pocket
was the specific reason the discount was only 30% instead of 40-50%.
~50% Wetlands Scattered Throughout — 10-40% Discount
This is the hardest scenario to evaluate because you cannot cleanly separate dry from wet. The wetlands are mixed throughout the
property — patches of wet ground intermixed with patches of dry ground, with no clear line between them. You cannot lot-split to
create one clean lot and one wet lot because every resulting lot would have wetlands scattered through it.
The discount range is wide (10-40%) because it entirely depends on one critical question: is there a contiguous buildable area large
enough for a house plus a septic system? If yes — if there is a real, usable, connected patch of dry ground somewhere on the
property even though wetlands surround it — discount on the lower end (10-20%). The buyer has a buildable spot; it is just
surrounded by inconvenience. If the buildable ground is fragmented into tiny, disconnected pockets that are each too small to
practically use, discount on the higher end (30-40%). The property becomes much harder to use for any residential purpose.

[PAGE 48]
In the scattered scenario, use common sense. Look at the satellite imagery with hazards off. Can you visually identify a CLEAR
building site? Or does it look like a patchwork with no obvious place to build? Your eyes will tell you more than the wetland
percentage number.
Wetlands in ONE Corner of the Front — ~15-20% Discount
This is an important distinction from wetlands across the ENTIRE front. If the wetlands occupy just one corner of the front area — say
the front-left quadrant — the MAJORITY of the frontage is still buildable. The middle and right side of the front are still clean and
accessible. The buyer can build in the center or on the dry side of the front, and the wet corner is just an inconvenience in one area.
This came up on the 40ac Deaver property where the left/front lot had wetlands in the front-left area with additional wet areas in the
back. The user noted that the middle and right side were still buildable — the wetlands limited one specific area but didn't block the
entire front. The discount was approximately 15-20%, reflecting the partial limitation.
Front/Road Access Completely Blocked by Wetlands — 50-80% Discount
This is the worst-case scenario for a residential property. If wetlands cover the ENTIRE front of the property along the road, the buyer
cannot build anywhere near the road. The most convenient and valuable part of the property — the road-accessible front — is
unusable. The buyer is forced to build deep into the property, which means: an extremely long driveway (expensive), running utilities
a long distance from the road (expensive), the building site is far from emergency access (safety concern), and the front of the
property — what everyone sees — is swamp (terrible first impression).
Very few buyers are willing to accept this level of inconvenience and expense for a building site. The buyer pool shrinks dramatically,
and those who are willing expect a significant price reduction. Hence the 50-80% discount range. The exact discount depends on
how deep the wetlands extend (just a band along the road vs most of the property) and whether there is any reasonable path through
or around them to reach dry ground in the back.
100% Wetlands — Hunting/Recreational Only — 30-40% Off Wetland Comp Sales
If a property is 100% wetlands with no buildable area at all, it has ZERO residential value. You cannot build a house. You cannot
install a septic system. The land has no residential utility whatsoever. The only market is hunting/recreational buyers — people who
specifically want swamp land for duck hunting, deer hunting, fishing, or just recreational ownership of wild land.
For these properties, you should NOT comp against clean residential land sales. Instead, try to find other wetland property sales in
the area — properties that sold specifically as hunting/recreational land. Those are your comparable sales because the buyers were
in the same buyer pool (recreational, not residential). If you find wetland comp sales, discount 30-40% from those to be conservative.
If no wetland comps exist, you are making the market — apply a heavy discount to clean land comps and price conservatively.
Minimum lot size for a marketable pure wetlands hunting property: approximately 20 ACRES at the absolute minimum, preferably
20+ acres. Below 20 acres of pure swamp, there is very little hunting buyer interest — it is simply not enough land to hunt on
meaningfully. A 5-acre swamp lot has essentially no market. A 20-acre swamp lot might attract a duck hunter. A 50-acre swamp lot
has a real hunting market.

THE PERC TEST — THE #1 QUESTION FOR WETLANDS PROPERTIES
What a Perc Test Is and Why It Matters
A percolation test (perc test) determines whether the soil on a property can absorb wastewater from a septic system. The test
involves digging holes, filling them with water, and measuring how quickly the water drains. If the soil drains properly (passes perc),
you can install a standard septic system at normal cost — typically $5,000-$15,000 depending on the area and system type. If the
soil does NOT drain properly (fails perc), you need an engineered or mound septic system.
An engineered/mound septic system costs $10,000 to $40,000+ depending on the area, the severity of the drainage problems, and
the type of system required. This is a MASSIVE additional cost for the end buyer — potentially $20,000-$30,000 more than a
standard septic. This kills value not just because of the dollar amount, but because of the UNCERTAINTY and HASSLE. The buyer
doesn't know exactly how much it will cost until engineers evaluate the site. They don't know if it will even work. The whole process
takes longer and involves more experts. Many buyers simply walk away from properties that need engineered septic because the
uncertainty is too high.
When to Actually Worry About Perc
Here is the nuance: perc is NOT a concern on every wetlands property. If the dry portion of the property is clearly large enough to
perc and build on — like a solid 10+ acres of dry, flat ground — perc is not even worth mentioning as an issue. There is OBVIOUSLY
buildable ground with adequate drainage. The perc test would pass easily on the dry portion.
Perc only becomes a REAL concern when you are questioning whether there is ANY buildable spot at all. This typically means 80%+
wetlands properties where you are looking at a small high-ground corner and wondering: is this corner big enough to pass perc? Is
the soil here adequate or is it just marginally less wet than the surrounding swamp? On these properties, the perc test is the
difference between 'buildable with heavy discount' and 'completely unbuildable hunting property.' It is the #1 question that determines
the property's use case and therefore its value category.
Also important: Land Insights wetlands data is NOT always 100% accurate. A property might show 30% wetlands on the overlay but
look completely dry and normal on satellite. This is DECEIVING because the wetlands could still be present in the SOIL
CONDITIONS — poor drainage, high water table, clay layers that prevent absorption — even though the surface looks dry. The
reverse can also happen: a property might show low wetlands but actually have wet areas that the data missed. Don't blindly trust the
percentage — use it as a starting point and then verify with your eyes on satellite, toggling hazards off to see the actual land
condition.
The Description Red Flag — 'Talk to Realtor About Perc Test'
This is one of the most important things the user taught about perc tests, and it relates to COMP EVALUATION, not just subject
property analysis. When you are reading a comp's listing description on Zillow or Redfin, pay close attention to any language about
perc tests or soil testing.
If the description says 'PERC TEST PASSED' or 'SEPTIC APPROVED' — that is a strong POSITIVE. The property is confirmed
buildable. The seller and listing agent are advertising this because it removes buyer uncertainty and adds value. A comp with a
passed perc test is a stronger, more valuable comp, and its PPA reflects the reduced risk.
If the description says 'TALK TO REALTOR ABOUT PERC TEST' or 'CONTACT SELLER ABOUT SOIL CONDITIONS' — that is
almost certainly a RED FLAG indicating something is wrong. Think about it logically: if the property PASSED the perc test, why would
they NOT put that in the description? A passed perc test is a selling point that every agent would want to advertise. The fact that they
instead want you to CALL about it means they know the truth would scare buyers away if written down. They want the opportunity to
convince you VERBALLY over the phone — to spin it, downplay it, or frame it positively in a way that text alone cannot accomplish.
When you see this language in a comp description, it explains a low PPA — the property likely has perc issues and buyers
discounted it accordingly. Flag it in your deliverable and note that this comp's low PPA is likely due to soil/perc concerns based on
the description language.

LOT-SPLITTING WETLANDS PROPERTIES — THE PRESENTATION TRICK
Why Lot-Splitting Changes Buyer Perception
This is one of the most powerful insights the user taught about wetlands, and it is worth understanding deeply. Imagine a 40-acre
property with 50% wetlands listed on the market as one whole parcel. The listing says '40 acres, 50% wetlands.' A buyer looks at this
listing and thinks: 'Half this property is swamp. That's a problem property. I don't want to deal with that.' The realtor looks at it and
thinks: 'This is going to be hard to sell. Half of it is unusable.' The satellite shows a property that is half blue (wetland overlay) and half
green. The overall impression is NEGATIVE.
Now imagine you take that SAME 40-acre property and split it into two 20-acre lots. Lot A is the dry portion — maybe it has 10-15%
wetlands along one edge. Lot B is the wet portion — 80%+ wetlands, marketed as a hunting property. Lot A's listing says '20 acres,
mostly cleared, minor wetlands along back edge.' A buyer looks at this and thinks: 'Nice 20-acre lot with a small wet area in the back
that I won't use. Looks good.' The satellite shows a property that is mostly green with a small blue strip along one edge. The overall
impression is POSITIVE.
The TOTAL WETLANDS have not changed. You did not remove a single square foot of swamp. The same amount of wet ground
exists. But the PER-LOT PRESENTATION is dramatically different. Instead of one scary '50% wetlands' property that makes buyers
hesitate and realtors cringe, you have one attractive 'mostly clean with minor back wetlands' property that sells relatively quickly
PLUS one hunting property that appeals to a specific buyer pool who actually WANTS swamp land.
The user specifically made this point: 'As-is, that property with a bunch of wetlands did not look good at all. Compared to if we lot-split
it — yes, they're still going to have the same amount of wetlands, but on a per property basis, it looks a lot more appealing.'
Marketing is everything in land sales. The same physical reality, presented differently, generates dramatically different buyer
responses.
How to Split: Clean Lot + Hunting Lot
When the wetlands are concentrated on one side of the property and the dry ground is concentrated on the other, the optimal split is:
one 'clean' lot containing the majority of the dry/buildable land, and one 'hunting' lot containing the majority of the wetlands. The clean
lot should include enough dry ground to be genuinely attractive for residential building — ideally with road frontage, buildable area
near the road, and minimal wetlands (just a small percentage along back or side edges).
The hunting lot is marketed directly to recreational buyers — hunters, wildlife enthusiasts, people who want a camp property for
weekends. Price it at hunting property values, which are significantly lower than residential. The minimum size for a marketable
hunting lot is approximately 20 acres — below that, there isn't enough land for meaningful hunting activity and the buyer pool shrinks
to nearly zero.
What If the Split Creates an 80%+ Wetlands Back Lot?
If lot-splitting creates a back lot that is 80%+ wetlands, that lot is essentially a hunting property with no residential value. You cannot
build a house on it. It should be priced at hunting property values — comp against similar wetland/hunting sales in the area,
discounted 30-40% from those. Don't try to market it as residential — the buyer pool for 'buy 20 acres of swamp and try to build a
house' is essentially zero. But the buyer pool for 'buy 20 acres of hunting land with great duck habitat' is real, especially in areas with
outdoor recreation culture.
Spreading Wetlands Evenly vs Concentrating — Which Is Better?
Generally better to create one clean and one wet lot rather than two mediocre lots that BOTH have significant wetlands problems.
The math: one clean lot at near-full value ($X) plus one hunting lot at hunting value ($Y) typically generates more total revenue than
two lots that are both discounted 30-40% because they both have '25% wetlands scattered throughout.'
The reason: buyers see '25% wetlands scattered throughout' and think 'I don't know where I can build on this — the wetlands are
everywhere.' That uncertainty scares them. But buyers see 'clean lot, minor wetlands in back' and think 'I know exactly where I'll build
— at the front where it's dry.' Certainty sells. Uncertainty discounts.

VISUAL ASSESSMENT — WHAT TO LOOK FOR ON SATELLITE
Always Toggle Hazards OFF — See With Your Own Eyes
The wetland overlay on Land Insights (the blue shading) can hide important features underneath — buildable areas, structures,
ponds, roads, cleared spots. You MUST toggle hazards OFF and look at the actual satellite imagery with your own eyes. What you
see with hazards off tells you more than the overlay percentage. The overlay is a starting point; your visual assessment confirms or
adjusts the story.
Identifying Marshy/Severe Wetlands vs Mild/Seasonal Wetlands
Severe wetlands are visible on satellite even without the overlay. They look DARK, BUSHY, and almost swampy. The vegetation is
thick, dark-colored (dark green to brownish-green), and UNEVEN — it clearly does NOT look like normal grass, maintained land, or
regular forest. The surface may appear bumpy or irregular. Standing water might be visible as dark patches. The overall impression
is: this looks like a SWAMP. You can visibly tell the difference between normal land and marsh.
Mild or seasonal wetlands can look completely NORMAL and DRY on satellite. The surface appears like regular grass or forest —
there is nothing visually alarming. This is DECEIVING because the wetlands are in the SOIL CONDITIONS — poor drainage, high
water table, clay layers — rather than on the surface. The land looks fine from above but the soil cannot support a standard septic
system. This is why you cannot assume a property is fine just because the satellite looks dry — the wetland designation might still be
accurate based on soil testing and hydrology data that you cannot see from above.
When the satellite looks dry but Land Insights flags significant wetlands, the property could go either way. The wetlands might be
mild/seasonal (the land is usable most of the year and only wet during heavy rain), or they might be real but invisible from the surface
(the soil is the problem, not the surface). You cannot determine this from satellite alone — that is why perc tests exist. Note the
discrepancy in your deliverable and flag it for the user.
Creeks and Wetland Bands
When a creek runs through a property, it almost always creates a WETLAND BAND along both sides. The soil adjacent to the creek
stays wet from the water flow, creating a strip of wetlands following the creek's path. This band might not be flagged by Land Insights
but you should assume it exists.
Be CONSERVATIVE with creeks — assume there are wetlands along the creek even if the overlay doesn't show them. The creek
itself could be a minor water feature value-add (creek is the lowest tier in the waterfront hierarchy — small premium). But the
wetlands band it creates could REDUCE buildable area or even cut the property in half if the creek runs through the middle. Evaluate
both: the creek premium (small positive) versus the wetlands band (negative). The net impact depends on how wide the wetlands
band is and whether it disrupts the primary buildable areas near the road.

COMPING WETLANDS PROPERTIES
Finding Wetland Comps — The Best Approach
The best approach for comping a wetlands property is to find OTHER properties with wetlands that have sold in the same county.
These are the most comparable because the buyers who purchased those properties already factored in the wetlands issues — they
knew about the wet areas, the limitations, the potential perc problems, and they paid a price that reflected all of that. Comparing your
subject to these wetland comps gives you the most accurate picture of what the market pays for impaired land in this specific area.
If you can find wetland comps AND clean comps in the same area, you get the best of both worlds. The difference between what
clean land sold for and what wetland land sold for tells you the MARKET'S ACTUAL wetlands discount in this specific county. That
might be 15%, it might be 30%, it might be 50% — the point is the market tells you rather than you guessing. If the market says
wetlands in this area cost 20% off clean land, and your subject has similar wetlands, apply that 20% and feel confident.
When No Wetland Comps Exist — Making Your Own Market
If there are no wetland property sales in the county, you are making your own market. Comp against clean land sales and apply your
discount percentages based on the framework in this guide: under 20% back = ~5%, front buildable = ~20%, big back pocket =
~30%, etc. Be CONSERVATIVE — better to price slightly under market than to overprice a wetlands property that sits for months.
But don't discount so aggressively that you turn a potential deal into a non-deal. That would be leaving money on the table.
Wetlands Properties Sit Longer on Market
Even aggressively priced wetlands properties may take longer to sell than clean land. This is inherent to the product — wetlands
scare some buyers and some realtors, and the buyer pool is smaller. Factor this into your list price by pricing slightly more
aggressively than you would for clean land. The properties WILL sell, especially with elite drone marketing focused on the buildable
portions, but expect higher DOM than comparable clean properties in the same area.
MARKETING WETLANDS PROPERTIES — DRONE FOOTAGE STRATEGY
Focus on the buildable portions. When creating drone footage for a wetlands property, emphasize where a buyer WOULD build:
the dry areas near the road, the cleared buildable spots, the nice features of the usable portions. Show where the driveway would go,
where the house could sit, what the views look like from the building site. If you show the wetlands prominently in the drone footage,
it scares buyers away — they see swamp and move on.
The realtor factor. Some realtors will push buyers away from wetlands properties, especially those that lack a perc test. They don't
want to waste their time on a property that might not be buildable. Having a perc test done (or strong evidence the dry portion can
perc) removes that objection and keeps realtors engaged. Without a perc test, realtors might not even show the property to their
clients.
Lot-split marketing. If you have split a wetlands property, the clean lot markets normally — like any other clean rural lot. The hunting
lot markets to a completely different buyer pool: hunters, outdoor enthusiasts, recreational land buyers. Don't try to sell a swamp lot
to someone looking for a homesite. Target the right buyer for each lot type.

DewClaw — Part 3: Subdivision & Value-Add Guide
 FULLY EXPANDED — Creative Subdivision Strategies for Maximum Value
WHY SUBDIVISION IS THE #1 VALUE-ADD STRATEGY
The DewClaw business model has two layers. Layer 1 is the simple flip: buy at 50% of as-is market value, sell slightly below market
with elite drone marketing, target 7-90 day sales. Layer 2 is the value-add: if the seller rejects the 50% as-is offer, pitch subdivision —
offer 50% of the IMPROVED (subdivided) value, which is higher than the as-is value. This often lands right where the seller wanted
because the improved value lifts the 50% offer into an acceptable range.
Subdivision is where the real money hides. The PPA curve is exponential — smaller lots have HIGHER PPA, larger lots have
LOWER PPA. When you buy a large parcel at the lower PPA and sell smaller lots at the higher PPA, the spread is your profit
multiplier. A 75-acre property at $4,000/ac ($300,000) split into ten 7.5-acre lots at $8,000/ac ($60,000 each) is worth $600,000 total
— double the as-is value. That spread is what makes subdivision plays so powerful.
But the user's core criticism was clear: Claude was way too conservative on subdivision. On the 75.91ac Dillon County property with
3,193ft of road frontage, only 2-3 lots were suggested when 5-10+ was the obvious play. That conservative thinking left hundreds of
thousands of dollars on the table. The lesson: get CREATIVE. Look for the hidden value. More lots = more money, as long as
demand exists for those lot sizes in the area.
STEP 1: ALWAYS LOOK UP COUNTY SUBDIVISION ORDINANCE —
NON-NEGOTIABLE
Why This Is the First Step Every Single Time
Before presenting ANY subdivision scenario, you MUST look up the county's subdivision ordinance. This is not optional and it is not
something to skip because you are in a hurry. If you pitch a subdivision plan that violates the county's rules — like suggesting 12 lots
in a county that only allows 5 before triggering major subdivision review — the user wastes time and effort pursuing an impossible
plan. That destroys trust and credibility.
Google '[County Name] [State] minor subdivision ordinance' or '[County Name] subdivision ordinance.' You are looking for specific
answers to these questions: How many lots can you create before triggering MAJOR subdivision review? This threshold varies by
county but is typically 5-10 lots. What is the MINIMUM lot size allowed? Some counties require minimum 2 acres, others 5 acres,
others have no minimum. What are the ROAD FRONTAGE requirements per lot? Many counties require minimum frontage (50ft,
100ft, etc.) for each new lot. What are the FLAGPOLE LOT rules? Minimum width for the access strip, maximum depth, any special
requirements.
Minor vs Major Subdivision
MINOR subdivision (typically 5-10 lots depending on county) is the preferred path. It involves simpler approval processes, usually just
a plat review by the planning office. Timeline: approximately 1-3 months from application to approval. Less red tape, fewer
requirements, faster execution. This is what the DewClaw model targets.
MAJOR subdivision (above the minor threshold) requires significantly more approvals — planning commission review, potentially
public hearings, environmental review, possibly road construction requirements, stormwater management plans, and longer
timelines. This is more complex and expensive. HOWEVER — if the upside is significant, ALWAYS present major subdivision as an
option. Don't withhold the numbers just because it is major. The user will decide if the upside justifies the complexity. Always flag 'this
would be a major subdivision' prominently so the user knows what they are getting into.
Exempt Plats — The Easiest Path
Some states have exempt plat provisions that bypass subdivision approval entirely. South Carolina, for example, has a 5-acre
exempt rule: if every resulting lot is 5+ acres AND no new public infrastructure (roads, water, sewer) is required, the subdivision is
EXEMPT from formal approval. You just need a survey and a recorded plat. This is the fastest, cheapest, simplest path to

[PAGE 56]
subdivision.
Always check if the state has an exempt plat provision. If it does, and your lot sizes qualify, note it prominently in your deliverable:
'SC 5-acre exempt plat rule applies — lots at 5ac+ require no subdivision approval, just survey and plat recording.' This dramatically
reduces the user's execution risk and timeline.
If You Cannot Find the Ordinance Online
Sometimes the county's subdivision ordinance is not available online — smaller counties especially may not have digitized their
regulations. If you cannot find it after reasonable searching: note 'Could not find [County] subdivision ordinance online — verify with
county planning office before proceeding' and STILL present your subdivision scenarios. Don't hold back the analysis. The user can
call the county planning office to verify the rules. But the scenarios themselves — the lot configurations, the potential values, the
demand analysis — are still valuable for the user's decision-making even without confirmed rules.

STEP 2: CHECK DEMAND — WHAT LOT SIZES ARE ACTUALLY SELLING?
This Is the Most Important Factor for Lot Size Selection
The user emphasized this multiple times during training: the MOST IMPORTANT thing when considering subdivision is looking at
what lot sizes are actually selling in the surrounding area. You need to look around on the map and answer these questions: What
size lots exist in the area with HOUSES on them? This tells you what buyers are actually purchasing and using. Are similar-sized
vacant lots SELLING in the MLS comps? This confirms active market demand at that lot size. How quickly are those lots selling
(DOM)? Low DOM = strong demand. High DOM = weak demand at that price/size.
The biggest subdivision mistake — the one the user specifically called out — is splitting into lots that are TOO SMALL for the area. If
every lot in the surrounding area is 10+ acres with nice homes, and you create 2-acre lots, those lots might sit on the market for
months or years because nobody in that rural area wants 2 acres. The market is telling you '10 acres is what people want here' and
you ignored it. Conversely, if you are near a city and surrounding lots are 2-5 acres with active development, then 5-acre lots make
perfect sense and smaller might work too.
Lot Size Preferences
The user's preferred lot size range for the DewClaw business model is 5-10 ACRES for rural recreational properties. This is the
sweet spot — large enough for hunting, building a cabin, having privacy and space. Small enough to be affordable for most buyers
and to generate meaningful PPA uplift from the subdivision.
For properties closer to cities where development pressure exists and smaller lots are common, 2-5 ACRES can work well. The
demand for smaller lots increases as you get closer to urban areas because land is more expensive and buyers accept smaller
parcels for convenience.
The ABSOLUTE MINIMUM lot size the user prefers is 2 ACRES. Below 2 acres, you are getting into entitlement play territory — this
is outside the core DewClaw rural recreational model. However, if you see strong demand for 1-acre lots in a specific area (lots of
1-acre lots with houses, active sales at good PPAs), mention it as an option. The user is open to hearing about it even if it is outside
the normal model, but default to 5-10 acres for your recommended scenario.
How to Present Lot Size Recommendations — Three Game Plans
For every subdivision play, present THREE scenarios and a RECOMMENDED option:
Option A — Fewer, bigger lots: The conservative approach. Fewer lots at larger sizes (closer to what is selling in the area). Safer
demand, simpler execution, probably stays within minor subdivision threshold. Lower total revenue but higher certainty of quick
sales.
Option B — More, smaller lots: The aggressive approach. Maximum lot count at smaller sizes that still have market support. Higher
total revenue potential but requires more market demand and might push into major subdivision territory. More lots to sell = longer
total timeline.
Recommended — Your best judgment: The scenario you believe best balances lot count, market demand, execution complexity,
county rules, and total profitability. ALWAYS explain WHY you are recommending it. Example: 'I recommend the 6-lot split at ~12.5ac
each because 10-15ac lots are selling with houses in the area, it stays under the minor subdivision limit of 10 lots in this county, and
the total value of ~$675,000 represents significant upside over the as-is value of ~$450,000.'

STEP 3: CREATIVE LOT CONFIGURATIONS — THINK LIKE A DEVELOPER
Road Frontage Is the Key to Lot Count
Road frontage determines how many lots can have DIRECT road access. More frontage = more lots with their own road-facing
entrance. The calculation is simple: total road frontage divided by minimum frontage per lot (from county ordinance) = maximum lot
count from frontage alone. Example: 1,000ft of road frontage with a county minimum of 100ft per lot = up to 10 lots with direct road
access.
Two sides of road frontage is a HUGE advantage. If a property has frontage on TWO different roads, you can create lots fronting
each road, effectively doubling your lot count potential. The user emphasized this: the more road frontage you have, the more lots
you can make, and the more money you can make. Always check all four sides of a property for road frontage — not just the obvious
front road.
Flagpole Lots — The Hidden Value Play Most People Miss
This was one of the most important concepts the user taught during the entire training session. Before this lesson, the instinct was to
look at a property with limited road frontage and say 'not enough frontage to subdivide.' The user completely changed that thinking.
A FLAGPOLE LOT is a lot with a narrow access strip (the 'flagpole handle') connecting the road to a wider buildable area deeper in
the property (the 'flag'). The access strip is typically 30-60ft wide (depending on county ordinance) — just wide enough for a
driveway. The buildable area in the back can be as large as the available acreage allows.
Example: a 40-acre property with only 200ft of total road frontage. The old thinking: 'Only 200ft, can't subdivide — maybe 2 lots at
best.' The NEW thinking: create 4 flagpole lots with 50ft access strips each, each lot being approximately 10 acres extending deep
into the property. Four 10-acre lots at a higher PPA than one 40-acre lot = significant total value increase. The access strips are
narrow but functional — buyers can drive to their building site.
The user specifically said: these are the things that are 'hidden in plain sight' that most people — including Claude initially —
completely miss. Properties with limited frontage but significant depth are flagpole subdivision opportunities. When you see a deep
property with limited frontage, your FIRST thought should be 'flagpole lots,' not 'can't subdivide.' If you can identify these
opportunities, you are finding value that the vast majority of people cannot see, and that is exactly where the money is made.
Always check the county ordinance for minimum flagpole access width before presenting. Some counties require 30ft, some 50ft,
some 60ft. The width requirement determines how many flagpole lots you can create from the available frontage.
Building a Road Through the Property
When a property has enough acreage and demand for many lots but not enough road frontage for all of them, building a PRIVATE
ROAD through the property creates interior lots. This is a higher-complexity play but can unlock massive value on large properties.
Road type: the user always does DIRT or GRAVEL roads. Paved roads are too expensive for most deals. However, if the county
ordinance REQUIRES a paved road for subdivision AND the upside is big enough, the user is open to hearing the numbers and
deciding. Always check what the county requires.
Road width: wide enough for two cars to pass each other going in opposite directions. Check county ordinances for exact
requirements — typically around 20-30 feet. This is the same width as a standard road.
Interior lot discount: lots fronting the new private gravel road typically sell for about 5-10% LESS than lots fronting the existing public
paved road. This mirrors the general road hierarchy (gravel < paved). Not all lots in the subdivision are worth the same PPA — factor
this into your per-lot value estimates.
Cost estimation: simultaneously open another tab and look up 'gravel road cost per linear foot [county or state].' Use the Land
Insights ruler/measuring tool to measure the approximate road distance through the property. Present the cost estimate RIGHT
NEXT TO the subdivision scenario. Format example: '8-lot split: Total value ~$500K | Road cost estimate: ~$20,000 (800ft gravel at
$25/ft) | Minor subdivision.' Do NOT subtract costs from the total value — just list them alongside so the user can evaluate the full
picture.
Lot Lines Don't Have to Be Perfect

[PAGE 59]
Lots do NOT have to be equal sizes. A split creating one 20ac lot and three 8ac lots is perfectly acceptable if that is what maximizes
value and matches market demand. The terrain, shape, and features of the property should guide the lot configuration, not a desire
for symmetry.
Lot lines can be DIAGONAL if needed to maximize usable lots or follow natural features. They don't have to be perpendicular or
parallel to the road. However, don't create WEIRD shapes that would confuse or turn off buyers. Each lot should still look like a
usable, reasonable piece of property. The shape golden ratio for each lot is 1:2 to 1:4 width-to-depth. Maximum acceptable is 1:5 or
1:6. Skinnier than that creates 'bowling alley lots' that lose significant value.
Split along NATURAL FEATURES when it makes sense. If half the property is farmland and half is timbered, split along that natural
dividing line to create one farmland lot and one timbered/recreational lot. Different land types appeal to different buyer pools, and
separating them lets you market each lot to its ideal buyer.

STEP 4: THE PPA CURVE AND SUBDIVISION VALUE
The fundamental concept: the PPA curve is EXPONENTIAL. Smaller lots have HIGHER PPA and larger lots have LOWER PPA. This
is where subdivision money is made — you buy at the large-lot PPA and sell at the small-lot PPA. The STEEPER the curve (bigger
difference between large and small lot PPAs), the more profitable the subdivision.
ALWAYS verify PPA at the target lot size with ACTUAL COMPS. Don't just assume smaller = higher PPA based on theory. Pull
comps at the lot size you are proposing and verify what the market actually pays. The curve theory is directionally correct (it always
goes that direction) but the actual dollar amounts come from real sold comps at that specific lot size in that specific county.
If the PPA curve is relatively FLAT in a county (small lots and large lots trade at similar PPA), subdivision upside is limited. But
creating more sellable units still has SOME value — two $50K lots might sell faster than one $100K parcel because each individual
lot is more affordable for buyers. Present the numbers even if the upside is modest.
CRITICAL ERROR from the 75.91ac Dillon property: Claude initially said smaller lots would have LOWER PPA than larger lots — the
exact OPPOSITE of how the exponential curve works. Smaller lots ALWAYS have higher PPA. This was a fundamental error that
would have massively undervalued the subdivision play. Never get the curve direction backwards.
STEP 5: WHEN NOT TO SUBDIVIDE
Bowling alley lots: If splitting creates lots skinnier than 1:5 or 1:6 width-to-depth ratio, the resulting lots lose so much value from the
bad shape that subdivision isn't worth it. This was taught on the Buckley Rd property (38.37ac, Susquehanna County, PA) — already
a long narrow strip, splitting would create unsellable bowling alley lots in a rural area with zero demand for that shape.
No demand for smaller lots: If all surrounding lots are 40+ acres with no smaller lots selling anywhere in the area, creating 5-acre
lots might not work. Selling one larger parcel quickly is better than sitting on 8 small lots nobody wants. This is the biggest subdivision
mistake: splitting too small for the area because you got caught up in 'more lots = more money' without checking if there is actually
demand for those smaller lots.
Wetlands screw up every resulting lot: If wetlands are scattered throughout and every resulting lot would have significant wetlands
problems, it might be cleaner to sell as one parcel. One property with 30% wetlands in the back is easier to market than four lots that
all have scattered wetlands.
Not enough profit margin: The user's general threshold: a $300K purchase price property where subdivision only yields $400K is
barely worth the effort. Below that spread, the simple flip is faster, easier, and less risky. But STILL present the numbers even if
marginal — the user can decide. Don't withhold the subdivision analysis.
STEP 6: PRESENTING COSTS — ALWAYS SEPARATE
Do NOT subtract costs from the total subdivision value. Present costs SEPARATELY right next to each scenario so the user can
evaluate the full picture. Costs to estimate and present: Road construction (if building a new road) — look up gravel road cost per

NEVER PITCH SUBDIVISION TO THE SELLER
The user was emphatic about this: NEVER tell the seller about your subdivision plans. Keep the strategy to yourself. There is no

DewClaw — Part 4: Flood Zone & Slope Evaluation
 FULLY EXPANDED — Nuanced Framework for Flood Zones and Terrain
FLOOD ZONE — THE 'LOOK AROUND FIRST' RULE
Why Panic-Discounting Is Wrong
The instinct when you see '100% flood zone' on Land Insights is to panic and apply a massive discount — 50%, 60%, even 70%.
This is WRONG in many situations. The Edisto Island property (Mary Seabrook Rd, Charleston County, SC — 5.64ac, 79%
wetlands, 100% flood zone) proved this emphatically. The initial valuation applied a 60-70% discount and came to approximately
$75,000. The problem: the ENTIRE ISLAND is in a flood zone. Every single property, every house, every building on Edisto Island
sits in a flood zone. People build there anyway because they have no other option — if you want to live on Edisto Island, you build in
a flood zone.
When everyone in the area is building in the flood zone, the flood zone is NOT a value killer — it is a COST FACTOR. The buyer
expects to pay flood insurance (which can be significant — hundreds to thousands per year) and may need a raised foundation
(additional construction cost). These are real costs that reduce the property's net value slightly. But they are manageable costs that
thousands of people in flood zone areas accept every day. The market in these areas already has the flood zone cost baked into
property prices. Your comps from flood zone areas already reflect this reality.
The critical error on Edisto Island was double-discounting: applying a flood zone discount ON TOP of comps that were already in the
same flood zone. Those comps already priced in the flood zone conditions. Adding another 30-50% discount on top was penalizing
the property twice for the same condition.
The #1 Rule: Check If People Are Building
Before discounting for flood zone, ALWAYS look around the area first. Toggle hazards on to see the flood zone boundaries, then look
at the satellite imagery around your property. Are there HOUSES nearby in the same flood zone? If yes — people are building
despite the flood zone designation. The market has accepted the flood risk and is pricing it as a cost factor, not a deal-breaker. Your
discount should be modest: approximately 10-15%, reflecting the additional costs (insurance, raised foundation) the buyer will face.
If there are NO houses nearby in the flood zone — nobody is building there — that is a very different signal. The market is telling you
this area is not suitable for development. The flood risk may be too severe, the insurance costs too high, or the practical challenges
of building too great. In this case, discount more heavily: 25-40% depending on severity. More often than not, flood zones do NOT
have many houses in them. But SOMETIMES they do — coastal areas, islands, established riverfronts — and those 'sometimes' are
the situations where panic-discounting costs you money.
Partial Flood Zone — Same Logic as Wetlands
If a property is partially in a flood zone (like 30% flood zone), the discount depends on WHERE the flood zone falls on the property.
This is EXACTLY the same framework as wetlands evaluation:
Flood zone in the FRONT/road area = bigger problem. The most convenient buildable area is affected. The buyer would need to build
to flood-resistant standards in the most expensive location (near the road where construction costs are already highest due to utility
connections). Larger discount.
Flood zone in the BACK = minor issue. The back of the property is rarely the primary building site. The flood zone doesn't interfere
with convenient construction near the road. The buyer might not even notice the flood zone in the back corner. Smaller discount —
same logic as wetlands in the back (~5-10%).
Flood zone covering the middle or cutting through the property = moderate issue. It may not block the front entirely but creates
complications for site planning. Moderate discount based on how much it impacts buildability.
Comping Flood Zone Properties

[PAGE 63]
Try to find other properties WITH flood zones that have sold in the area. These are the best comps because the buyers already
factored in the flood zone conditions. Compare what flood zone properties sold for versus clean properties to understand the
MARKET'S ACTUAL flood zone discount in that specific area. That market-determined discount is more accurate than any
percentage you could guess.
If you are comping in an area where EVERYTHING is in a flood zone (like Edisto Island, coastal areas, or riverfronts), then ALL your
comps are already flood zone comps. In this case, do NOT apply an additional flood zone discount on top — the flood zone is already
baked into every comp's price. Just comp normally using those flood zone comps as your data set.
Different Flood Zone Types
There are different flood zone designations (Zone A, Zone AE, Zone X, Zone V, etc.) that indicate different levels of flood risk and
insurance requirements. Some properties can be in multiple flood zone types simultaneously. While each type has specific technical
definitions regarding flood probability and insurance costs, for COMPING PURPOSES the main question remains the same: are
people building in it or not? The market behavior tells you more about the practical impact than the zone designation letter. Note the
zone type in your deliverable for reference, but don't get bogged down researching the technical meaning of each designation.

THREE different flood zone types. No purchase price or date available.

SLOPE EVALUATION — LOCATION AND SEVERITY FRAMEWORK
Basic Slope Discount Guidelines
Under 10% average slope = no issue, no discount. This is essentially flat or gently rolling terrain that creates no building challenges.
10-20% average slope = 5-10% discount. Moderate slope that increases construction costs slightly (may need some grading,
potentially a walk-out basement design) but is very buildable. 20-35% average slope = 15-30% discount. Significant slope that
noticeably increases construction costs (retaining walls, special foundations, grading, potentially difficult driveway). 35%+ average
slope = 30-50% discount. Severe slope that creates major building challenges — very expensive to construct, limited building
options, potentially dangerous during construction.
These are STARTING POINTS, not absolute rules. Always compare your subject's slope to the COMP slopes. If every property in a
mountain area has 20% average slope, the market has already priced in that terrain. Your additional discount from those mountain
comps should be small or zero. If your subject is significantly steeper than the comps in the area, THAT difference justifies additional
discount.
Slope Location — Same Framework as Wetlands and Flood Zones
WHERE the slope is on the property matters enormously — the same framework applies as for wetlands and flood zones. The
front/road area is the most critical because that is where access and convenient building happen.
Steep slope in the FRONT of the property: This is the most severe problem — potentially 50-70% discount. If the front is super
steep, the fundamental question is: how do you even ACCESS the property? Can you build a driveway through a 40% grade?
Probably not safely or affordably. If you cannot drive to the buildable area, the property is essentially inaccessible for residential use.
Even if there is beautiful flat ground in the back with amazing views, the buyer CANNOT GET THERE. This is fundamentally different
from wetlands in the front — wetlands are flat but wet. A steep front slope is a PHYSICAL BARRIER that may be impossible to
overcome regardless of budget.
Flat front with steep slope in the BACK: Only 5-20% discount, depending on how steep the back is. You can build near the road
where it is flat — short driveway, easy utility connections, convenient access. The steep back is just unused land, similar to wetlands
in the back. Most buyers aren't going to build in the far back corner anyway. The back slope might actually be a POSITIVE if it
creates elevated views — hilltop or ridgeline properties have view premiums (5-30%). But the slope discount and view premium
partially offset each other.
Average slope vs max slope: Care more about the AVERAGE slope, especially at the front where building would occur. A property
with 5% average but 40% max in one back corner is mostly fine — the high slope is isolated in a location nobody would use. A
property with 25% average slope throughout the entire property is a much bigger problem because every potential building site is
steep.
Slope and Septic — The Rocky Soil Connection
Steep slopes create TWO problems for septic systems that directly impact property value. First, rocky exposed soil on slopes often
does NOT drain well enough for the septic leach field. The soil is thin, rocky, and lacks the depth of organic material needed for
proper wastewater absorption. This means the property may FAIL a perc test — and as covered in the Wetlands section, failing perc
means needing an engineered/mound septic at $10,000-$40,000+ additional cost, which kills value.
Second, physically INSTALLING a septic system on the side of a hill or mountain is difficult, expensive, and sometimes physically
impossible. The excavation is harder, the system needs special engineering to handle the grade, and the leach field layout is
constrained by the terrain. How do you build a level septic tank pad on a 30% slope? It requires significant earthwork.
The combination of poor drainage soil + difficult installation makes steep slope + septic a double problem. The same property that
has a 20% slope discount might face an ADDITIONAL perc/septic concern if the soil on the slope cannot support conventional septic.
Flag this in your deliverable when you see significant slope — note in Section 9 (Questions to Ask Seller) that perc test status should
be verified given the terrain.
Slope and Subdivision
You CAN still subdivide a hilly property — slope does not automatically kill the value-add play. But slope DOES affect the value of
each resulting lot. If you create 5-acre lots on sloped terrain, each of those lots sells for less than equivalent 5-acre lots on flat terrain

[PAGE 65]
because the buyer faces higher construction costs. Factor the slope discount into your per-lot PPA estimates, not just the overall
property value.
Slope can also affect WHERE you can place lot lines. Steep ravines, ridgelines, and cliff faces may create natural boundaries that
influence lot configuration. Work with the terrain rather than fighting it — place lot lines along natural features where possible.
Slope as a Positive — Views and Elevation
In some situations, slope creates the desirable feature that gives a property premium value. A property on a hilltop or mountaintop
with panoramic views can command significant premiums: mediocre/average view = ~5% premium. Good view with scenic value =
10-15% premium. Superman/panoramic unobstructed view = 20-30% premium.
However, it is RARE to find truly great views that justify a large premium because you typically still need to: build a road up the slope
(expensive and sometimes impossible on steep terrain), clear trees at the top so you can actually SEE the view (clearing costs), and
create a building pad on the summit (grading costs on steep terrain). The view premium and the slope discount partially OFFSET
each other. A mountaintop property with a 30% slope discount but a 25% view premium nets out to only about 5% discount — but a
mountaintop property with a 30% slope discount and only a 5% 'meh' view premium nets out to 25% discount. Evaluate both factors
and present the net effect.
If contour lines or elevation data in Land Insights suggest the property sits in a high position with potential overlook views, flag it in
Extra Notes. But don't overweight views — they are speculative from satellite (you don't know what the actual view looks like until you
visit) and the costs to access them are real.
QUICK REFERENCE — FLOOD ZONE & SLOPE DISCOUNTS
Flood Zone: People building nearby = 10-15% discount (cost factor). No development in flood zone = 25-40% discount. Flood zone
in front = bigger discount. Flood zone in back = minor. Entire area is flood zone with development = minimal additional discount
(already priced in).
Slope: Under 10% = none. 10-20% = 5-10%. 20-35% = 15-30%. 35%+ = 30-50%. Steep FRONT = 50-70% (can't access). Flat front
/ steep back = 5-20%. Slope + rocky soil = perc concern. Slope + views = view premium partially offsets discount.

DewClaw — Part 5: HOA & Proximity Valuation
 FULLY EXPANDED — HOA Framework, Lake/City/Highway Proximity Impact
HOA LOTS — COMPREHENSIVE FRAMEWORK
First Reaction: Negative — Because Restrictions Kill Buyer Pool
When you see that a property or comp is in an HOA, the first reaction should be NEGATIVE. The reason is simple and fundamental:
people who buy vacant land want FREEDOM. They want to build whatever they want, put a trailer on it if they choose, have no rules
about what their property looks like, and answer to nobody about how they use their land. That is the entire appeal of buying vacant
rural land — independence and control.
An HOA directly conflicts with this appeal. HOA restrictions tell the buyer: you cannot put a mobile home here, you must build a
minimum square footage house, you must maintain your property to certain standards, you must pay annual fees, and you must
follow architectural guidelines. Each of these restrictions ELIMINATES potential buyers. The person who wanted to put a trailer on 5
acres? Gone. The person who wanted to build a small cabin? Maybe gone if minimum square footage is too high. The result: a
smaller buyer pool, longer time on market, and lower price.
The user confirmed this directly: 'HOA lots always sit way longer on the market, 100%.' The longer market time is a direct
consequence of the smaller buyer pool. Fewer interested buyers = fewer offers = more time sitting = price pressure. This is why HOA
is inherently a negative for vacant land values.
HOA Subdivision With FEW Houses Built = Major Red Flag
This is one of the most important signals to watch for. If you see an HOA subdivision — a planned community with lots laid out, roads
built, maybe even amenities — but very FEW houses have actually been built, that should tell you something critical: the HOA is
probably VERY STRICT. People are choosing NOT to build there because the restrictions are too onerous.
Think about it: if the subdivision was reasonably priced, in a decent location, with reasonable rules, people would be building. The
fact that lots are sitting empty in a developed subdivision with infrastructure means buyers are looking at the HOA rules, seeing the
restrictions, and deciding to buy somewhere else with more freedom. This is the market voting with its feet, and the vote is: 'these
restrictions are too harsh.'
When you see this signal — an HOA subdivision with few houses — discount the lots more heavily than a typical HOA discount. The
market has already told you these lots are harder to sell.
HOA Subdivision With LOTS of Houses = Area Quality Can Add Value
The opposite signal is equally important. If the HOA subdivision has LOTS of houses, is well maintained, has nice properties with
manicured lawns, and looks like an attractive, thriving community — the area quality itself can add value even though it is HOA.
People have accepted the restrictions because the AREA is genuinely desirable. The HOA is working as intended — it is maintaining
property values, keeping the neighborhood attractive, and creating a community that people want to live in.
This doesn't fully OFFSET the HOA discount — there are still restrictions that limit the buyer pool. But a well-developed, attractive
HOA subdivision is a much better situation than a dead subdivision with empty lots. The area quality, maintained roads, and
community feel have real value that partially compensates for the restrictions.
Comping HOA Lots — Find Same-Subdivision Comps
The best approach for comping an HOA lot is to find other lot sales WITHIN THE SAME HOA SUBDIVISION. These are the
gold-standard comparables because the buyers already factored in those SPECIFIC restrictions, those SPECIFIC fees, and that
SPECIFIC area quality. A comp from the same HOA subdivision tells you exactly what the market pays for lots under those exact
conditions.
Same-subdivision comps also reveal HOA STRICTNESS indirectly: if lots are selling at decent prices with reasonable DOM (30-90
days), the HOA isn't killing the market. If lots are sitting for 6+ months or selling at deep discounts, the HOA is a significant problem.

[PAGE 67]
If you CANNOT find comps within the same subdivision, look for comps in other HOA subdivisions in the county — but be cautious
because different HOAs have different restrictions. An HOA with 'no mobile homes' is very different from an HOA with 'minimum
2,000sf home with architectural review.' Using one HOA's comps for a different HOA's lots introduces uncertainty.
Using HOA Comps for Non-HOA Subjects — Flag With Hazard Emoji
If your SUBJECT property is NOT in an HOA but you are using a COMP that IS in an HOA with amenities (community lake, pool, golf
course, maintained roads), be very careful. That comp has BOTH positives (amenities, maintained area, community feel) AND
negatives (restrictions, fees, limited buyer pool) baked into its sale price. It is nearly impossible to separate these factors.
Did the comp sell high because of the lake access? Or did it sell lower than it would have WITHOUT the HOA restrictions? You
genuinely don't know. The amenities and restrictions are intertwined in the price. For this reason: flag the comp with the HAZARD
EMOJI immediately so the user sees it. Don't weight it heavily in your valuation. Note it as a reference point with a caveat: 'This comp
is in an HOA with [amenities] — PPA reflects both amenity premium and HOA restriction discount, difficult to separate.' If the comp
stands out and has a realtor's contact info, suggest calling that realtor to learn more about listing history and offers received.
HOA Restrictions and Value Impact
Specific restrictions and their impact: 'No mobile homes' = HURTS value because a lot of vacant land buyers want to put trailers or
mobile homes on property. This eliminates a significant portion of the buyer pool. 'Minimum square footage' (e.g., must build 1,800+
sf) = HURTS value because it raises the minimum investment required to use the lot, eliminating budget-conscious builders.
'Architectural review' = HURTS value because it adds time, cost, and uncertainty to the building process. The user confirmed: 'A lot of
people want to actually end up doing stuff like putting trailers and mobile homes on property. If HOA says they can't, that kills value.'
HOA Fees
HOA fees range from $500/year to $5,000/year or more. Higher fees usually mean more amenities and maintenance (nicer area,
maintained roads, community facilities) but also higher ongoing costs for the buyer. Note the fee amount in your deliverable if you
find it in the listing description. Higher fees can go either way — they might indicate a nicer area (positive) or just more overhead with
no real benefit (negative). The context matters.
Never Create an HOA for Subdivision
The user was emphatic: never, ever, EVER suggest creating an HOA when proposing subdivision plays. The DewClaw business
model targets rural recreational buyers who want freedom. Creating an HOA would directly conflict with the target buyer pool and
make the lots harder to sell. Keep subdivisions simple — just lots, no HOA, no restrictions beyond what the county requires.

PROXIMITY TO LAKES — DETAILED VALUATION FRAMEWORK
Direct Lakefront = Maximum Premium (2-3x Non-Waterfront)
Properties with direct lake frontage — the property boundary literally touches the lake water — command the highest water-related
premium. In many markets, lakefront lots sell for 2-3x what comparable non-waterfront lots sell for. The reason: the buyer can walk
out their door, across their yard, and be at the lake. They can build a dock, launch a kayak, fish from their property. This direct,
private access to water is extremely valuable and extremely limited in supply (there are only so many lots that touch the lake).
Lake quality matters significantly within this tier. A popular recreational lake (boating, swimming, fishing, public access, community
activity) = highest premium. A nice private lake (attractive, clean, good fishing but limited public access) = high premium but
potentially smaller buyer pool. A small private pond = moderate premium. A reservoir = lower premium because reservoirs are
typically less aesthetically appealing and may have restricted recreational use.
Across the Road From a Lake — Noticeable Premium, Not Lakefront
A property directly across the road from a lake has a noticeable premium over properties farther away, but it is NOT worth as much
as direct lakefront. The reason: you can literally SEE the lake from your property — potentially from your front porch. That visual
connection has real value. However, you cannot ACCESS the lake directly from your property. You have to cross a road to get to the
water, and the road itself creates separation — traffic, noise, and a physical barrier between you and the lake.
The drop from direct lakefront to across-the-road is meaningful — perhaps 40-60% of the lakefront premium disappears. You still get
the view and the proximity, but you lose the private access and the direct connection that makes lakefront so valuable.
Near a Lake (1/10 to 1/4 Mile) — Proximity Premium Zone
The lake premium starts to appear at approximately 1/10 to 15/100 of a mile from the lake in typical rural areas. At this distance, you
may be able to see the lake from higher ground on the property, and you are close enough to walk or drive to the lake easily. The
premium is relatively small compared to direct frontage — more of a location amenity than a water feature premium.
In more desirable areas — near popular beaches, cities, or heavily-used recreational lakes — the premium zone extends FURTHER.
Up to 1/4 mile or even a mile from the lake, properties command a premium because the AREA is desirable and the lake is the
anchor attraction. The key question: can you TELL you're near a lake? If the lake defines the community (lake in the road names,
lake community signs, businesses catering to lake visitors), the proximity premium extends across the whole community.
Community Lake in Subdivision — ONLY Waterfront Lots Get Real Premium
This is a critical distinction the user emphasized: in a subdivision with a community lake, ONLY the lots directly ON the water get the
real lake premium. There is a HUGE drop-off from lakefront lots to non-lakefront lots within the same subdivision. The non-lakefront
lots benefit from area quality and the community amenity (they can use the lake if the HOA allows it), but they are nowhere near the
premium of direct frontage.
This matters enormously for comping. If you see a lakefront lot in a subdivision that sold for $50K/ac, you CANNOT apply that PPA to
a non-lakefront lot in the same subdivision. The non-lakefront lot might be $10-15K/ac — a fraction of the lakefront price. Always
check whether a comp is actually ON the water or just NEAR the water. The difference can be 2-3x.

[PAGE 69]
PROXIMITY TO CITIES — DETAILED VALUATION FRAMEWORK
General Rule: Closer = More Expensive (But Every Market Is Different)
The fundamental principle: closer to a city = higher PPA. This is driven by convenience (closer to jobs, shopping, services,
entertainment), development pressure (builders and developers want land near growing cities), and infrastructure (closer to roads,
utilities, internet, cell service). You will see this clearly and consistently in comp data — properties closer to town trade at higher
PPAs than properties deep in rural areas of the same county.
However, the user emphasized that every market is different. There is no universal formula like '5 miles from city = X% premium, 20
miles = Y% discount.' The actual impact depends on the specific city, the local economy, the road network, and a dozen other
factors. Let the COMPS tell you the premium — pull comps at various distances from town and the PPA gradient will show itself.
The Sweet Spot: 10-15 Minutes Outside the City Edge
The user identified a specific sweet spot: approximately 30 minutes from the CENTER of the city, or about 10-15 minutes from the
OUTER EDGE of the city. This is the 'rural recreational ring' — close enough to the city for convenience (you can get to a grocery
store, restaurant, or hospital in 10-15 minutes) but far enough to feel RURAL (open space, privacy, larger lots, natural surroundings).
This ring is where the DewClaw business model has the strongest demand. Buyers in this zone want the best of both worlds: rural
living with urban convenience. They are willing to pay a premium over truly isolated rural land because of the proximity to services,
but they don't want to pay city prices. This creates a PPA sweet spot that is higher than deep rural but lower than suburban/urban
fringe.
City Size Matters — A LOT
Proximity to a large city (500K+ population metro area) commands significantly more premium than proximity to a small town (10K
population). The math is straightforward: more people = more demand = higher prices. A property 10 miles from Charlotte, NC (metro
population ~2.7 million) is worth DRAMATICALLY more than a property 10 miles from a town of 5,000 people. The buyer pool within
driving distance of Charlotte is hundreds of times larger.
Always note what the property is close TO, not just how close it is. '15 minutes from Walterboro' (pop ~5,000) is very different from
'15 minutes from Charleston' (metro pop ~800,000). Both are '15 minutes from a city' but the value implications are completely
different.
Small Towns (500-2,000 Population) — Adds Value Over Truly Isolated
A small town right next to the property ADDS value compared to truly isolated rural land. Even a tiny town with a gas station, a
general store, and a post office provides CONVENIENCE that pure isolation does not. The buyer can get gas, buy groceries, mail a
package without driving 30+ miles. That convenience has real value — not as much as being near a real city, but meaningfully better
than being in the middle of absolute nowhere.
Small towns also indicate SOME level of community and infrastructure. There are probably maintained roads, possibly county water,
perhaps a volunteer fire department. These basic services increase the property's appeal and value compared to a location where
none of that exists.
Rural Recreational — Distance From City Can Be POSITIVE
For the DewClaw target buyer (hunting land, cabin property, weekend getaway), being farther from a city is not always negative.
Many buyers SPECIFICALLY WANT isolation and privacy. They are looking for a property where they can escape the city, enjoy
nature, hunt, fish, and not see another person for days. For these buyers, proximity to a city is actually a NEGATIVE because it
brings traffic, noise, development pressure, and other people.
The market supports both buyer pools: closer to city = development/homebuilding buyers (higher PPA). Farther from city =
recreational/hunting buyers (lower PPA but real, consistent demand). Both are valid markets with active buyers. The key is
recognizing which buyer pool a specific property appeals to and pricing accordingly.

PROXIMITY TO HIGHWAYS & INTERSTATES — EXPANDED
Directly ON a Major Highway = NEGATIVE for Residential (10-30% Discount)
If the property's frontage is directly on a major highway where traffic moves at 50-65 mph, this is a NEGATIVE for residential value.
The discount ranges from 10-30% depending on severity. The user explained the reasons clearly: 1) Nobody wants to literally be
speeding off a highway to get to their property — it is dangerous, unpleasant, and stressful to decelerate from 65mph into a driveway.
2) You may need to work with the county to build a special offshoot/deceleration lane, which adds cost and bureaucratic hassle. 3)
Highway noise destroys the rural, peaceful atmosphere that rural land buyers want. The constant sound of trucks and cars at high
speed is unpleasant. 4) Zero privacy — every person driving by at 65mph can see your property, your house, your yard.
Try to find other highway-frontage comps to see what the market actually discounts. The 10-30% range is a guideline but the real
comp data will give you the exact discount for that specific market.
Exception: Commercial Zoning on Highway = POSITIVE
If the property is COMMERCIALLY ZONED and fronts a highway, the highway frontage FLIPS from negative to POSITIVE. Traffic
visibility and easy access are exactly what commercial properties need — a gas station, a retail shop, a warehouse, a restaurant all
BENEFIT from being on a busy highway. This completely changes the comp approach. Highway-front commercial land commands a
PREMIUM, not a discount. If you encounter this, note the commercial zoning prominently and comp against commercial land sales,
not residential.
Nearby Highway (Not Directly On It) = Mostly Neutral
If the property is near a highway or interstate but not directly fronting it — say, 1/2 mile away with a buffer of trees or other properties
between — the impact is mostly neutral. Good regional access is a mild positive (easy to get to the property from the highway), but it
is not a significant value driver for rural recreational land. Note it in Extra Notes as a minor access benefit.

DewClaw — Part 6: Comping Mastery
 FULLY EXPANDED — Process, Investigation, Weighting & Common Mistakes
THE COMPING MINDSET — HOW THE USER THINKS
Comping is not a mathematical formula. It is not 'take 5 PPAs, average them, done.' Comping is building a STORY — a narrative
about what a property is worth based on what similar properties have sold for, adjusted for specific differences. The user builds this
story by evaluating each comp TRAIT BY TRAIT against the subject property: is this comp's road better or worse? Is the land type
similar or different? Does it have wetlands that mine doesn't? Is it closer to town? Each difference translates to a specific percentage
adjustment, and those adjustments stack to determine whether the comp is above or below the subject's value.
The user evaluates 5-20 comps to build the full story. Some comps are ANCHORS — nearly identical to the subject in key traits.
These drive the price. Some are CEILING comps — superior to the subject, setting the maximum. Some are FLOOR comps —
inferior, setting the minimum. The subject's value falls between the floor and ceiling, weighted toward the anchors. Every comp
should MAKE SENSE in the story. If a PPA seems too high or too low, dig deeper until you understand why. 'Price doesn't stray too
far from the tree without a reason.'
STEP 1: KNOW YOUR PROPERTY COMPLETELY — BEFORE TOUCHING
COMPS
Before looking at a single comp, you need to fully characterize your subject property. What are its defining traits? Land type:
completely wooded/timbered, cleared, maintained, pasture, farmland, or a mix? If a mix, where is each type (front cleared, back
timbered, etc.)? Road type: paved county road, maintained gravel, rough gravel, nice dirt, rough dirt, seasonal two-track? What is the
quality — smooth and well-maintained or rutted and rough? Shape: nice rectangle, square, irregular, long and narrow?
Width-to-depth ratio? Wetlands: what percentage and WHERE — front, back, scattered, one corner? Slope: average and maximum.
Where are the steep areas — front blocking access or back not affecting buildability? Water features: pond, creek, lake nearby or on
property? Are they verified or just suspected from the overlay? Structures: any buildings, mobile homes, barns, sheds visible on
satellite? Road frontage: how many feet? On how many roads?
This detailed characterization is what you are COMPING AGAINST. Every comp gets compared to THESE traits. If you don't fully
know your own property, you cannot accurately assess whether a comp is similar, better, or worse. Take the time to know your
subject inside and out before looking at the first comp.
STEP 2: ALWAYS READ THE DESCRIPTION — THE #1 CRITICISM
Why This Is Non-Negotiable
The user identified 'not reading listing descriptions' as the SINGLE BIGGEST weakness in Claude's comping. The description on
Zillow/Redfin is where critical information lives that directly affects how you weight the comp. Without reading the description, you are
making comp weighting decisions with incomplete information — and incomplete information leads to wrong prices.
What Descriptions Reveal
HOA details: 'Located in XYZ Estates HOA, $350/year fees, no mobile homes, minimum 1,500sf home.' This completely changes
how you weight the comp — it is in an HOA with restrictions that limit the buyer pool. Flag it with hazard emoji.
Perc test status: 'Perc test passed!' = confirmed buildable, value-add. 'Talk to realtor about perc test' or 'Contact seller about soil
conditions' = RED FLAG. If it passed, they would advertise it. They want you to call because the truth would scare buyers if written
down.
Road details: 'Paved county road access' vs 'accessed via seasonal dirt road, may be impassable in winter.' Enormous difference in
value. The description tells you road quality that you might not be able to determine from satellite.

[PAGE 72]
Land features: 'Recently cleared and maintained,' 'mature hardwood timber,' 'active cattle pasture with fencing,' 'organic farm with
established irrigation.' Each of these tells you the land type and condition in the seller's own words.
Seller motivation: 'Must sell!' 'Estate sale,' 'Relocating,' 'Price reduced!' These clues suggest the seller is motivated, which might
explain a lower sale price that doesn't reflect true market value.
Issues disclosed: 'Property is in flood zone X,' 'easement along east boundary,' 'wetlands in southeastern portion.' These explain
why a PPA might be low.
Reading the description takes 30 SECONDS. That 30 seconds can completely change how you weight the comp. A comp at
$4,000/ac with a description revealing wetlands and a failed perc test tells a completely different story than a comp at $4,000/ac with
a description saying 'beautiful cleared lot, paved road, perc test passed.' Same PPA, completely different relevance to your subject.

[PAGE 73]
STEP 3: ALWAYS CHECK THE PHOTOS — THE #2 CRITICISM
The user identified 'not checking listing photos' as the second biggest weakness. Listing photos show what the property looked like
AT THE TIME OF SALE — this is what the buyer paid for. Current satellite might show something DIFFERENT because the property
condition may have changed since the sale.
Examples of changes that photos catch: listing photos show standing mature timber (the property was timbered when sold — PPA
reflects timbered land value). Current satellite shows the land has been CLEAR-CUT (trees harvested after the sale). If your subject
is cleared, this timbered-land-priced comp is less relevant than it appeared. Listing photos show a cleared field with farm equipment
visible. Current satellite shows overgrowth and abandonment. The comp's PPA reflects an ACTIVE farm — a yielding asset premium
that may no longer exist.
Photos also reveal: road condition (paved vs dirt visible in access photos), land quality (nice maintained vs rough brush), structures
(barns, sheds, mobile homes that might hide under tree canopy on satellite), wet areas or standing water, and the overall 'feel' of the
property. Compare EVERY anchor comp's photos to the current satellite imagery. If ANYTHING looks different, CALL IT OUT in your
deliverable.
STEP 4: INVESTIGATE WHEN PPAs DON'T MAKE SENSE — THE #3 CRITICISM
The Investigation Checklist — In Order
When a comp's PPA seems too high or too low compared to other comps in the area, do NOT accept it and move on. Investigate.
Run through this checklist:
1. Street View on Land Insights: Drop the pegman on the road near the comp and look at the property from ground level. What
does the road look like? Can you see the land condition? Any structures? Any red flags — trash, run-down buildings, marshy areas?
Also use this moment to assess the AREA QUALITY: are surrounding properties nice or run-down?
2. Listing photos on Zillow/Redfin (simultaneously): What does the land actually look like in the listing photos? Marshy? Terrible
access? Super sloped? In a terrible area? Compare to what you see on satellite.
3. Read the description: This is often where you find the answer to 'why did this sell at this PPA?' Wetlands mentioned? Failed perc
test hints? HOA restrictions? Unusual easements? Seller desperation?
4. Toggle flood/wetland overlays on Zillow/Redfin map: Quick 10-second check. If the comp shows flood zone or wetlands on the
Zillow map that you didn't notice before, that could explain the low PPA.
5. Open a NEW Land Insights tab with the comp's APN: This is the most thorough check. Grab the APN from the bottom of the
Zillow/Redfin listing. DUPLICATE your Land Insights tab (NEVER navigate away from your subject property — you might not be able
to get back). Enter the APN with the correct county in the new tab. Check: Insights tab for wetlands %, flood zone %, slope data, road
frontage. Toggle hazards on and off to see overlays vs actual satellite. Check Street View if available.
6. Check DOM and price history: Did it sit for 200+ days? Were there multiple price drops? A comp that sold after months of price
reductions was overpriced initially — the final sale price is closer to market. Listed/delisted patterns = red flag, something may be
wrong with the property.
The goal of all this investigation: figure out WHY the PPA is what it is. Once you know the reason, you know how to weight the comp.
Low PPA because of wetlands and bad road? = floor comp. High PPA because of cleared farmland and paved road? = ceiling comp.
Same traits as your subject? = anchor comp.
The APN Verification Workflow — Detailed Steps
This is the specific process the user taught for deep-diving a comp's quality in Land Insights:
Step 1: On the Zillow/Redfin listing, scroll all the way to the bottom of the page and find the APN (Assessment/Parcel Number). Copy
it.
Step 2: Go to your Land Insights tab where your SUBJECT PROPERTY is loaded. DUPLICATE the tab. This is critical — never
navigate away from your subject property tab. Reasons: (a) the comp's APN might not work in Land Insights and you would lose your
subject if you navigated away, (b) you need your subject property tab intact for continued comp searching after you finish verifying

[PAGE 74]
this comp.
Step 3: In the new duplicate tab, use the search to enter the APN with the correct county name. Wait for it to load.
Step 4: Once the comp loads in Land Insights, check EVERYTHING: Go to the Insights tab — read wetlands %, flood zone %, slope
data, road frontage numbers. Toggle hazards ON to see the flood/wetland overlays on the map. Toggle hazards OFF to see the
actual satellite imagery underneath. Check Street View if available by dropping the pegman.
Step 5: Compare what you see on Land Insights to what the Zillow/Redfin listing showed. Do the photos match the current satellite?
Is the description consistent with what LI shows? If anything is DIFFERENT — listing photos show timbered land but current satellite
shows it has been clear-cut — that is critical information that affects how you weight the comp.
Step 6: When you are done evaluating this comp, CLOSE that duplicate tab and go back to your original subject property tab. Keep
your browser clean. Move on to the next comp that needs investigation.

BUILDING THE STORY — COMP TIERS AND WEIGHTING
The Three Tiers
ANCHORS (Similar comps): Match your subject most closely in the traits that matter most: acreage, land type, road type, location,
shape, and condition. These are the comps that DRIVE your price. If you find 3 anchor comps all at $6,000-$7,000/ac, that is your
range. Weight these the heaviest — they are the foundation of your valuation story.
CEILING (Better comps): Superior to your subject in meaningful ways — better road, cleaner land, closer to town, more features
(well/septic, cleared, farmland). Your subject should price BELOW these. They tell you the absolute maximum. Use them to confirm
that your anchor-based price isn't too high — if your anchors say $6,500/ac and your ceiling is $7,800/ac, that makes sense. If your
anchors say $6,500/ac but your ceiling is only $5,000/ac, something is wrong with your anchor selection.
FLOOR (Worse comps): Inferior to your subject — more wetlands, worse road, timbered vs your cleared, worse shape, worse
location. Your subject should price ABOVE these. They tell you the absolute minimum. Use them to confirm your anchor price isn't
too low — if your anchors say $6,500/ac and your floor is $3,000/ac, the spread makes sense if the floor comps have significant
issues.
How to Make the Final Adjustment From Anchor PPA
Once you have identified your anchor comp(s), adjust the anchor PPA for specific differences between the anchor and your subject.
Each adjustment should be a specific, logical percentage tied to a specific trait difference:
Road type: paved to gravel = ~10-15% discount. Gravel to dirt = ~5-20% depending on dirt road quality (nice maintained dirt = 5%,
rough seasonal dirt = 15-20%). Land type: farmland premium = 30-50% over pasture. Cleared vs timbered = timbered is lower
(clearing cost for buyer). Pasture vs maintained cleared = roughly similar. Wetlands: back <20% = ~5%. ~50% front buildable =
~20%. Big back pocket = ~30%. (See Part 2 for full framework). Slope: under 10% = none. 10-20% = 5-10%. (See Part 4 for full
framework). Shape: nice rectangle vs bowling alley = significant. Well/septic installed = 15-20% premium on the comp. Proximity:
closer to town = higher PPA. Near lake = premium. Near highway = 10-30% discount for residential.
Stack these adjustments: 'Anchor comp at $7,800/ac (cleared, paved road). My subject is on a gravel road (-12%) and has a creek
with 5% wetlands (-3%). Adjusted PPA: $7,800 × 0.85 = ~$6,630/ac.' This is logical, specific, and defensible.
ACTIVE LISTINGS — VIEW-TO-SAVE RATIO
Active listings that haven't sold are NOT anchor comps. They show what someone is ASKING, not what the market is PAYING.
Typically there is about a 20% drop from active list price to actual sold price on average — could be more, could be less. Active
listings are primarily useful as CEILING references.
However, one powerful tool for evaluating active listings is the VIEW-TO-SAVE RATIO on Zillow. This metric shows how many
people viewed the listing versus how many saved/favorited it. The benchmark: for every 10 views, you want to see at least 1 save. If
the ratio meets or exceeds this threshold (1:10 or better), the property is probably priced close to correctly — buyers are interested
enough to save it for later consideration. If the ratio is terrible (hundreds of views, 1-2 saves), the property is overpriced — buyers are
looking but nobody is interested enough to take it seriously.
Active listings with GOOD view-to-save ratios are closer to market reality and make better ceiling references. Active listings with BAD
ratios are overpriced and serve only as the highest possible ceiling. Don't completely ignore active listings — they are still data. But
weight them appropriately based on their traction.
PENDING COMPS — HOW TO WEIGHT THEM
Pending comps are sales that are IN PROGRESS but not yet finalized. They are the most CURRENT data point (more recent than
most sold comps) but not 100% reliable because deals can fall through, terms can change, and the final closing price might differ
from the pending list price. The user's approach: discount pending comps 5-10% from the pending list price. Weight pending comps
at approximately 80-85% of what you would weight an actual sold comp. Use them as strong supporting data, but don't make a
pending comp your sole anchor.

SPECIFIC COMPING SCENARIOS — NUANCED ANSWERS
Suspiciously Low PPA — Investigation Checklist Applied
When you see a comp with a PPA that seems too low, run the full investigation checklist. The user's order: 1) Street View to see the
deal visually. 2) Listing photos simultaneously. 3) Read the description — this is where you most often find the answer. Look for:
wetlands mentions, perc test language (especially the red flag 'talk to realtor about perc test'), bad road descriptions, HOA
restrictions, disclosed issues. 4) Zillow/Redfin map overlays. 5) APN lookup in a new LI tab if still confused. Common explanations for
low PPA: wetlands, failed or questionable perc, bad road, timbered, bad shape, landlocked, in a poor area, HOA restrictions, flood
zone, steep slope.
Two Comps Tell Completely Different Stories
When one comp says $4,000/ac and another says $8,000/ac at similar acreage, there is ALWAYS a reason. Price doesn't stray far
from the tree without explanation. DIG DEEPER into BOTH comps. Check descriptions, photos, wetlands, road type, land condition
on both. One of them has traits that explain the gap. The $4,000 comp probably has issues (timbered, bad road, wetlands, bad
shape, bad area). The $8,000 comp probably has advantages (cleared, paved road, nice layout, closer to town). Find the reason,
then determine which one matches YOUR subject's quality. Weight that one as your anchor.
The Ada Ln example proved this: the 23ac comp at $4,130/ac was likely inferior quality (not investigated). The 22.3ac Tampico Way
comp at $7,847/ac was cleared/maintained with a paved road — quality-matched to the subject. The correct anchor was Tampico,
not the 23ac comp. The difference: $50,000 in the final valuation. Finding the RIGHT comp matters more than finding the MOST
comps.
Comps With Structures
When a comp has a structure on it (barn, shed, house, mobile home), flag it with the HAZARD EMOJI. Comp the land as if the
structure doesn't exist — use it as a ceiling reference only because the structure inflated the sale price above raw land value. Don't
try to estimate the structure's value and subtract it — too imprecise and not your job. The user will investigate the structure
separately. Just note: 'Comp includes a [structure type] — using as ceiling reference, raw land PPA is lower than this comp's total
PPA.'
Sparse Comp Markets
When comps are extremely sparse (only 1-2 in the whole county), you are making your own market. Use process of elimination: find
whatever floor and ceiling data exists. Place your subject on the PPA trendline between available data points using the exponential
curve principle (smaller lots = higher PPA, larger = lower). If you have a 15ac property and only a 10ac comp ($8K/ac) and a 20ac
comp ($5K/ac), interpolate: 15ac falls between, probably around $6,000-$6,500/ac. Be conservative when data is sparse and note in
the deliverable that comp data is limited and confidence is lower. Never cross county lines — each county is its own market.
Tax Sale Properties
If the owner bought the property at a tax sale for almost nothing (like the Kershaw Camden property at $1,000), this does NOT
change market value. Tax sale price = distressed transaction. Market value = what a willing buyer pays a willing seller in an open
market. The property is worth what the comps say regardless of what the owner paid. The low cost basis IS a strong seller motivation
signal — any offer feels like pure profit — but it is NOT a valuation factor.
Commercial Zoning in Residential Area
If commercially zoned but sold at normal residential prices in a residential area, the market treated it as residential. Comp as
residential. If surrounded by commercial properties and businesses, it is a completely different comp pool — commercial goes for
much more than residential. Note the zoning in your deliverable and let the user evaluate. Commercial lots typically take longer to
sell.
Adjacent to Timber Company

If the property is IN THE MIDDLE of a large timber operation (surrounded on all sides by timberland), discount 10-30% depending on
how isolated and industrial it feels. Smaller parcels feel more trapped. If the timber company is just ONE neighbor among many
(residential properties nearby too), it doesn't meaningfully affect value.
Near State Park / National Forest
Adds roughly 10% premium if butting up to or very close to public recreational land. Hunters especially value adjacent public land
because it effectively expands their hunting area. Note in Extra Notes.
Listed/Delisted History
If a comp was previously listed and failed to sell, flag it. Buyer may have backed out, could be something wrong with the property.
Don't dismiss the comp entirely but don't weight it as heavily. Note it for the user's team to investigate.

THE THREE BIGGEST MISTAKES — AND HOW TO FIX THEM
Mistake #1: Not reading listing descriptions. FIX: For every comp you use as an anchor, open it on Zillow/Redfin and read the
FULL description. Extract HOA info, perc test status, land features, restrictions, red flags. Takes 30 seconds. Changes everything.
Mistake #2: Not checking listing photos. FIX: Look at every anchor comp's photos. Compare them to current satellite. Note any
changes — land condition may have changed since the sale. Photos show what the BUYER paid for.
Mistake #3: Not digging deeper when PPAs don't make sense. FIX: When a comp seems off, run the investigation checklist
(Street View, photos, description, map overlays, APN lookup in new LI tab). Find the REASON for the PPA. If you can't explain a
PPA, don't anchor to it.
AREA QUALITY ASSESSMENT — HOW TO READ AN AREA
The user identified three main factors for quickly assessing area quality from the map and Street View:
1. How rural/isolated is it? The more rural and isolated, the lower the PPA generally. Extremely isolated with nothing around for
miles is a yellow flag — limited demand, limited services, limited buyer pool.
2. What kind of houses are visible? From satellite, can you see bigger houses with maintained yards, or run-down mobile homes
with cluttered properties? The house quality surrounding the subject property is a strong indicator of area desirability and PPA level.
3. Street View confirmation: Drop pegman and LOOK. If you see trash, unmaintained properties, broken-down vehicles, run-down
mobile homes with sagging roofs = poor area, lower PPA. If you see well-maintained homes, clean roads, manicured yards, nice
fencing = nice area, higher PPA. You CANNOT determine area quality from GIS data alone — you MUST go to Street View. While
you are already checking Street View for road type, also assess the surrounding area quality. Takes 10 extra seconds.
MULTIPLE PARCELS & PACKAGE DEALS
If the owner has ANY other properties — even just one — note it in the deliverable and put it in Section 9 (Questions to Ask Seller) so
the user can ask on the call. Adjacent parcels owned by the same person: note the adjacency but comp each parcel individually.
Combined potential is interesting but doesn't change individual values unless combining.
MARKET HEALTH INDICATORS
More solds than actives + low DOM (30-90 days) = hot. Lots of actives, few solds, high DOM (6+ months) = cold. But the user doesn't
care much about hot vs cold: 'I could sell a piece of property in any market if I get it for the right price.' Just note conditions briefly.
Lots of actives with few solds means sellers are overpricing — sold comps reflect TRUE market, active listings reflect wishes.
OUTLIER COMPS — NEVER DISREGARD, ALWAYS INVESTIGATE
If 3 comps say $5K/ac and 1 says $10K/ac, never throw out the outlier. Investigate it — pull APN, check LI, read Zillow description
and photos. Outliers almost always have a reason: well/septic, farmland, closer to town, lake proximity. Listing photos sometimes
reveal things NOT in the description — call those out. If the reason makes sense, use as ceiling. If genuinely unexplainable, be
aware but don't weight heavily.

LIVE COMPING EXAMPLE 1: WOODED GRAVEL ROAD SUBJECT
Subject property: Completely wooded, off a gravel road. No wetlands, no flood zone, reasonable shape. The goal: use comps to
determine what this specific property is worth by comparing it trait-by-trait to sold properties in the area.
Comp 1 — Gravel Road, Natural Cleared Spot in the Middle
This comp is off a gravel road (same as our subject) and has a natural cleared spot about one-quarter to one-half of the way into the
property. The cleared spot was NOT man-made — it just naturally exists, perhaps where trees fell or where soil conditions didn't
support tree growth. To actually USE this cleared spot for building, you would need to BUILD A ROAD through the trees from the
road frontage to reach it. That road construction costs money — potentially thousands of dollars depending on distance and terrain.
The user's evaluation: this comp is 'pretty damn close' in PPA to our subject. The gravel road matches (same road type = no road
adjustment needed). The land is mostly wooded (same = no land type adjustment needed). The cleared spot adds ONLY about 5%
value. WHY only 5%? Because the cleared spot is in the MIDDLE of the property, not at the front. To use it, you need to invest in
building an access road. That investment cost offsets most of the value the cleared spot would otherwise provide.
Now here is the critical comparison: if that SAME cleared spot was at the FRONT of the property, right near the road where a
driveway would naturally go, it would add 20-25% value. Why? Because at the front, the cleared spot IS the building site. No road to
build, no trees to clear, no extra cost — the buyer can immediately start building on a ready-made pad right near the road. The
convenience is enormous. The cost savings of not needing to clear trees and not needing to build an interior access road are
significant.
The math on this nuance: front cleared spot = 20-25% premium. Middle cleared spot = 5% premium. That is a 4-5x difference in
value for the SAME physical feature, driven entirely by LOCATION on the property. This principle applies universally: WHERE a
feature is determines the vast majority of its value, not whether the feature exists. A pond at the front of a property (visible,
accessible, aesthetic) is worth more than the same pond hidden in the back behind trees. A flat buildable area at the front is worth
more than the same area in the middle requiring road access.
Comp 2 — Dirt Road, Completely Wooded (Identical Except Road)
This comp is the EXACT SAME as our subject property in every meaningful way — completely wooded, similar acreage, similar
shape — except it is on a DIRT road instead of our gravel road. The user immediately identified this as a FLOOR comp: it is worth
5-20% LESS than our subject, with the exact discount depending on the quality of the dirt road. A nice, well-maintained dirt road
might only warrant a 5% discount. A rough, seasonal, barely-passable dirt road warrants 15-20%.
This type of comp is incredibly powerful for calibration. When you find a comp that differs from your subject by ONLY ONE
VARIABLE — in this case, road type — the price difference between the two properties IS the market value of that variable. If this
dirt-road comp sold for $4,500/ac and our gravel-road subject should be priced at $5,000/ac, the difference ($500/ac or ~10%) is the
market's valuation of gravel vs dirt road in this area. You can use this calibration point for every other comp in the area: any property
on a dirt road gets approximately 10% less than an equivalent gravel-road property.
The user's teaching here was about building CONFIDENCE in your adjustments. Instead of guessing '10% for dirt vs gravel' from a
textbook, you can PROVE it from the comp data. One-variable comps give you that proof.
Comp 3 — Paved Road, Wetlands in Back, Completely Wooded
This comp introduces two differences from our subject: BETTER road (paved vs our gravel = adds value) and WETLANDS in the
back (~5% discount because back wetlands barely matter). The user evaluated the net effect: paved road adds value (maybe
10-15% over gravel), back wetlands subtract a tiny amount (~5%). Net result: this comp is a BIT ABOVE our subject — it is the LOW
CEILING.

[PAGE 81]
Now the story is taking shape. We know our subject is: ABOVE Comp 2 (floor — dirt road, 5-20% below us). NEAR Comp 1 (anchor
— gravel road with middle clearing, nearly identical PPA). BELOW Comp 3 (low ceiling — paved road but minor wetlands). The
subject's price is bracketed between the floor and the low ceiling, anchored near the most similar comp.
Comp 4 — Paved Road, Well and Septic Already Installed
This is the HIGH CEILING. Paved road (better than our gravel) PLUS well and septic already installed. The user valued the well and
septic premium at approximately 15-20%. WHY is it worth that much? Because having a well and septic already in place REMOVES
two of the biggest costs and uncertainties a buyer faces when purchasing raw land:
WELL: Drilling a well costs $5,000-$15,000+ depending on depth, geology, and location. There is also UNCERTAINTY — what if the
well doesn't produce adequate water? What if you hit rock and need to drill deeper? What if the water quality is poor? Having a well
already drilled and producing eliminates all of this cost and risk for the buyer.
SEPTIC: Installing a standard septic system costs $5,000-$15,000+. But first you need a PERC TEST, which introduces uncertainty
— what if it fails? Then you need an engineered/mound system at $10,000-$40,000+. Having septic already installed means the perc
test already passed, the system is already in, and the buyer faces ZERO uncertainty about buildability.
Combined, the buyer saves $10,000-$30,000+ in hard costs plus eliminates the single biggest risk factor in rural land purchase (can I
build here?). That removal of cost and uncertainty is worth the 15-20% premium. Our subject property, without well or septic, is
DEFINITELY below this ceiling comp.
The Complete Story for Example 1
FLOOR: Comp 2 (dirt road, identical otherwise) = 5-20% below subject. ANCHOR: Comp 1 (gravel road, middle clearing) = nearly
identical PPA. LOW CEILING: Comp 3 (paved road, back wetlands) = slightly above subject. HIGH CEILING: Comp 4 (paved road +
well/septic) = definitely above subject. Subject price: near the anchor PPA, confirmed by being above the floor and below both
ceilings. Every comp makes sense in the story. Every price difference is explainable by specific trait differences.

Comp 1 — Half Wetlands (Front Left Corner + Back), Pasture, Gravel Road

If the wetlands were across the ENTIRE front — blocking all road-accessible building sites — the discount would be catastrophic
(50-80%). But because they are only in one corner, the impact is much more manageable. The buyer still has plenty of buildable

buyers) and gravel road (vs our paved). But it is BETTER in two ways: more road frontage (creates subdivision potential and multiple
access points, which adds inherent value even without actually subdividing) and closer to town (convenience premium for the buyer).
The user's analysis: as-is, this comp is still 10-20% LESS than our subject. The negatives (wooded + gravel) OUTWEIGH the

However, the user made an important additional note: the extra road frontage creates SUBDIVISION POTENTIAL that should be
flagged separately from the as-is valuation. If county regulations allow subdividing on a gravel road, the additional frontage could
unlock significant value-add. This illustrates an important principle: always evaluate properties on TWO levels — what it is worth
AS-IS (for the flip) and what it COULD be worth WITH VALUE-ADD (for the subdivision backup strategy).
Comp 3 — Half Pasture Front / Half Wooded Back, Nice Dirt Road
This comp is CLOSE to our subject but slightly inferior. The front is pasture — matching our subject's land type in the most important
location (the front near the road). The buyer drives up and sees open pasture, just like our subject. But the back half is wooded —
less usable, harder to access, and visually less appealing than full pasture. The user discounted approximately 10% for the wooded
back: 'You're not really going to use the back as much, but it's still nice to have cleared in the front.'
The road is a nice quality dirt road — better than rough dirt but worse than our paved. The user discounted another 5-10% for the
road difference, noting that the discount is on the lower end (5%) because the dirt road is well-maintained and nice quality. If it were a
rough, seasonal dirt road, the discount would be larger (15-20%).
Combined: ~10% for wooded back + ~5-10% for dirt road = this comp is about 15-20% below our subject. But it is CLOSE — close
enough to serve as a CEILING reference from below. Our subject is slightly above this comp, confirmed by our better road and fully
pasture land.
If a Farmland Comp Appears at High PPA
The user added this important contextual note: if you also see a farmland comp in the area trading at a significantly higher PPA than
everything else, don't be confused or misled by it. Farmland is a YIELDING ASSET — it generates income from crop production.
That income stream is baked into the price, which is why farmland trades 30-50% above comparable pasture or cleared land.
Your PASTURE subject sits BELOW farmland in value but ABOVE the wooded/wetlands/gravel floor comps. The farmland comp
helps you understand the COMPLETE RANGE of the market — from the worst properties at the bottom (timbered, wetlands, dirt road

= floor) to the best (farmland, paved road, yielding asset = highest ceiling). Your subject's price falls in the middle of this range,
positioned by its specific traits: better than the inferior comps, not as valuable as the farmland.

farmland premium. It is not an outlier to investigate — it is the market correctly pricing a fundamentally different (and better) product.
Use it as a ceiling that explains the top of the market, and place your subject appropriately below it.

Q1-2: Already Listed Properties
Does being listed on MLS change comping? No — still comp through sold comps. BUT check the active listing's VIEW-TO-SAVE

market — realtors tell sellers to list high for negotiation room. $200K listing with $150K in comps = seller leaving room, real value is

Q3-4: Farmland Premium
How much more? 30-50% more than pasture (lean 30%). Yielding asset = income stream baked into price. Idle farmland? Only
10% more than pasture — looks nice, has potential, but no income. Crop rows visible = full premium. No rows = idle, 10% only.
Q5-6: Power Lines vs Utility Lines
High voltage through middle: 25-30% discount. Huge towers, buzzing noise, can't build under/near. If farther back on large
property: 10-20%. On edge/border: Back/side = 5%. Narrow property where edge is close = 10-30%. UTILITY lines (no noise):
Front = 10%, back/side = 5% or nothing. Main issue is building restriction and visual, NOT noise.
Q7-8: Timber Identification
Planted timber: Trees in organized SELECT LINES/rows, usually under LLC/company name, all same size. Natural forest:
Random, disoriented, mixed sizes/species. Mature hardwood: Tall, dense, large uniform crowns. Scrubby pine: Thinner, irregular,
smaller. Don't estimate dollar value — note 'timber has potential harvest value' and put in Section 9.
Q9-10: Mobile Homes
Value separately? Always value LAND as vacant, but 100% note the mobile home. Put in Section 9 for seller questions.
Age/condition matter? Absolutely — 1980s singlewide in bad shape ≈ liability. 2015 doublewide in good shape = $25-35K+
value-add. Can't assess from satellite — seller must provide specs.
Q11-12: Pending & Active Comps
Pending: Always discount 5-10% from list. Weight at 80-85% of sold comps. Most current data but not finalized. Active: Mostly
ceiling reference. ~20% drop to actual sold typical. Check view-to-save ratio: good ratio = closer to market. Bad ratio = overpriced,
highest ceiling only.
Q13-14: Area Quality
Top 3 signals: 1) How rural/isolated — more rural = lower PPA generally. 2) House quality on satellite — nice houses vs run-down
mobile homes. 3) Street View confirmation — trash, unmaintained properties = poor area; maintained homes, clean roads = nice
area. MUST go to Street View — can't tell from GIS data alone.
Q15-16: Multiple Parcels & Package Deals
Flag every time? Yes — any other properties, even just one, note it and put in Section 9. Adjacent parcels same owner? Note
adjacency but comp individually. Combined potential is interesting but doesn't change individual values.
Q17-18: Market Health
Hot: More solds than actives + low DOM (30-90 days). Cold: Lots of actives, few solds, high DOM (6+ months). User doesn't care
much — business model works in any market with aggressive pricing and elite marketing. Lots of actives with few solds = sellers are
overpricing; sold comps = TRUE market value.
Q19: Comp Weighting — Same Acreage/Worse Area vs Different Acreage/Same Area

No universal answer. Both are useful data. Draw TWO trendlines in your head: 'what does each acreage trade at in this area?' (PPA

Q20: Outlier Comps
NEVER 100% disregard. Pull APN, check LI, read Zillow description and photos. Outliers almost always have a reason: well/septic,

THE CORE EFFICIENCY RULE: EVERYTHING SIMULTANEOUSLY

What 'Simultaneously' Actually Means in Practice
When a Land Insights link arrives, the following should all be happening in parallel, not in sequence:
Process 1 — Land Insights Subject Property: Open the link, grab Summary data (acreage, APN, owner name, mailing address,

THE COMP INVESTIGATION PROCESS — DONE IN PARALLEL
Scanning All Comps — Whittle Down Quickly
When you zoom out on Land Insights and see comp dots, you might see 8, 10, 15, or even 20 comps visible. You do NOT need to
investigate all of them in depth. The process is a FUNNEL — start wide and narrow down quickly:
First pass (2 seconds): Scan all PPA labels. Immediately identify the range — what is the highest PPA? What is the lowest? What
is the cluster in the middle? Any obvious outliers? This gives you the full spectrum in a glance.
Second pass (5 seconds): Identify which comps are TRASH — way too large (100+ acres when subject is 10 acres), clearly in a
different area/market, obviously irrelevant land type. Mentally dismiss these. Don't click on them. Don't investigate them. They add
nothing to the story.
Third pass (10 seconds): Click into the remaining RELEVANT comps — similar acreage, similar area, PPAs that make sense for
this market. These are your potential anchors, ceilings, and floors. Open them for investigation.
Fourth pass (30-60 seconds): For the relevant comps, read descriptions on Zillow/Redfin, check photos, verify land type and road
type. This is where you confirm whether each comp is actually comparable to your subject. Comps that looked relevant from the PPA
label might turn out to be irrelevant after reading the description (HOA, structures, farmland premium, wetlands issues). Dismiss

Final assessment (15-30 seconds): You should now have 3-5 solid comps categorized as anchors, ceilings, and floors. Build the

The entire comp investigation process — from first scan to final price — should take about 60-90 seconds. Combined with the 30-60
seconds for subject property data gathering, the total should be approximately 2 minutes.
Reading Descriptions — More Accurate Than Photos
The user specifically emphasized that DESCRIPTIONS are more accurate than photos. The reason: listing photos could be OLD —
taken months or even years before the sale, showing conditions that no longer exist. The property might have been timbered since
the photos were taken, or cleared, or had a structure demolished. But the description is typically updated by the listing agent and
reflects the current or recent condition of the property more accurately.
This means when you investigate a comp, read the DESCRIPTION FIRST. If the description tells you everything you need to know
(land type, road condition, features, issues, HOA status), you may not even need to spend time on the photos. Photos are
SECONDARY CONFIRMATION — useful for verifying what the description says, catching things the description didn't mention, and
visually comparing the comp to your subject. But the description is the primary source of truth.
When to Pull the APN for a Comp
You do NOT need to pull the APN and open a duplicate Land Insights tab for every single comp. That would be too slow. Only pull
the APN when: 1) A comp's PPA doesn't make sense after reading the description and checking photos — something is off and you

the full story — you need LI data to fill in the gaps. 3) You suspect a comp might have wetlands, flood zone, or slope issues that
explain a low PPA but aren't mentioned in the listing. For comps where the description clearly explains the PPA and the photos

STREET VIEW — WHEN TO USE AND WHEN TO SKIP
Street View is a CONFIRMATION tool, not a discovery tool. You use it to VERIFY things you already suspect or need to confirm —
road type, area quality, access points, structures. If you can already confirm these from the satellite imagery on Land Insights, you do
NOT need Street View.
When to SKIP Street View
If you can clearly see from satellite: the road is obviously paved (smooth, wide, marked), the area has nice houses with maintained
yards (visible from above), the property has a clear access point (driveway visible), and the land condition is obvious (clearly cleared,
or clearly timbered, or obviously farmland with crop rows) — then Street View adds nothing. Skip it and save 10-15 seconds.
When to USE Street View
If any of the following are UNCLEAR from satellite: Is the road paved, gravel, or dirt? (Roads can look similar from above.) What is
the area quality? (Can't tell house condition from directly above.) Is there a visible driveway or access point to the property? Are there
structures hidden under tree canopy that satellite can't see? Is there standing water, marshy areas, or other conditions not visible
from above? Is there a fence indicating pasture use?
When you do use Street View, spend a MAXIMUM of 10-15 seconds. Quick glance: road surface (paved/gravel/dirt), area quality
(nice/average/poor), property access (driveway visible?), anything notable. Then move on. Don't explore the entire road. Don't drive
around the neighborhood. Quick glance, confirm what you need, close.
Street View as Standard SOP for Property Access
The user did clarify that you should ALWAYS look at the access to the property — meaning the road and how you would enter the
property. This is a standard operating procedure item. When you are doing Street View (for confirmation), always check: what does

narrow, overgrown)? Is there a gate, fence, or any barrier? These access details affect value and should be noted in Extra Notes.

VALUE-ADD — ALWAYS INCLUDE, NO EXCEPTIONS
The user initially said to skip value-add when the upside is marginal, then REVERSED this instruction and said to ALWAYS include it.
The reasoning is critical to understand:
In most cases, the user targets 50% of market value for the as-is flip. But sometimes the seller pushes back and wants 60% or 70%.
In a normal flip-only analysis, the user cannot go above 50% because there is not enough margin. But if there is a value-add
(subdivision) that makes the improved value significantly higher than the as-is value, the user CAN come up to 60% or 70% of the
as-is value because the subdivision play provides additional margin.
Example: property worth $100,000 as-is. Seller wants $70,000 (70%). In a normal flip at 50%, the user can only offer $50,000 — too
low for the seller. But if subdivision makes the property worth $130,000, the user can offer $70,000 (54% of improved value) — the
seller is happy, and the user still makes significant profit from the subdivision upside. The value-add is what makes the deal WORK at
the seller's price.
This is why 99% of people only look at normal flips and miss deals. They can't come up to 70% because they don't see the
subdivision play. The user CAN come up because the value-add provides the extra margin. 'Always, always actually add the
value-adds' — no exceptions, even if the normal flip works fine. The value-add gives the user OPTIONS and FLEXIBILITY in
negotiation that they would not have otherwise.
