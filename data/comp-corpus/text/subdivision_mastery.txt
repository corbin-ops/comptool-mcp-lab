1. The Subdivision Decision Framework
Every property that crosses the desk gets evaluated through this 6-step decision tree before subdivision is
recommended. The steps must be followed in order — each one is a gate. If a property fails at any step, it
either becomes a simple flip or gets passed on entirely. Do not skip steps. Do not jump to lot design before
confirming demand. The framework exists to prevent costly mistakes.
Step 1 — Shape Check
Before anything else, look at the parcel shape on Land Insights. Is the property wide enough relative to its
depth to support splitting without creating bowling alley lots? The golden width-to-length ratio is 1:2 to 1:4.
Maximum acceptable is 1:5 or 1:6. Anything skinnier than that means splitting will only make the lots less
desirable, not more.
If the property is narrow all the way through — like a long rectangle where the short side is on the road —
stop here. Any split just creates skinnier bowling alleys. This is a flip, not a subdivision. Move on.
Key principle: You cannot fix a bad width-to-length ratio by subdividing. You only make it worse.
Flagpole lots work because the body of the lot is fat and usable — the narrow strip is just access.
A lot that is skinny all the way through has no fix.
Step 2 — Road Access Check
What roads touch the property? What type are they? Check the road color coding on Land Insights: green
= private/service road, yellow = residential/unclassified, orange = city/county road, red =
highways/interstates. The road type matters because county regulations may restrict which road classes
you can subdivide off of.
Check whether the county allows new subdivided lots to front onto the available road types. If the only
road is a county road and the county does not allow new subdivisions off county roads, you either need an
internal road or cannot subdivide. This single restriction can reduce the value of a subdivision plan by
20-50% or more.
If the only access is a single narrow flagpole strip — one access point = one lot, period. All the acreage
and cleared land in the world does not matter if the road access cannot support multiple lots.
Also check: If a property has 3 narrow access strips at 50 feet each but the county requires 60-foot
minimum for flagpole lots, that is a dead end. Flag it with the warning emoji so the owner knows the
subdivision play is blocked by county requirements.
Step 3 — Area Demand Check
Zoom out county-wide on Land Insights. This is not a tight radius around the subject property — it is the
entire county. Build a mental map of the county's personality: where are the towns? Where does density
cluster? Where does it thin out? Where does the subject property fall on that spectrum?
Look for these positive demand signals: lots of smaller parcels (2-5 acres) nearby with structures on them,
proximity to a town, a lake nearby attracting buyers, visible new construction, general population density.

[PAGE 4]
These tell you people are actively choosing to buy small lots here, so if you create more small lots, they
will sell.
Watch for these caution signals: way out in the sticks, very few houses around, only big parcels with
sparse development, no town or attraction nearby. This is rural recreational territory. It may be fine for a
buy-and-flip, but subdivision into small lots is risky because there may not be enough demand to absorb
them quickly. In these areas, big lots might be the only thing that moves because anyone who wanted a
small lot would just live closer to town.
Build rings outward from the subject property: what is happening immediately around it? One mile out?
Five miles out? County-wide? This should take 10-20 seconds to scan and understand. The goal is to
determine whether the market will absorb smaller lots in the timeframe we need (30-90 days).
Additional demand signals to look for: Is it near a lake (attracts buyers)? Is there a town or city nearby?
Are there houses and structures around, or is it desolate? Is it a nice area or a trashy area? Check Street
View and Google Maps — are the houses maintained? Is there a community feel? Even if the numbers
say subdivision works, if the area looks rough, buyers will not come.
Step 4 — PPA Trendline Check
Pull comps across the full acreage spectrum in the county. You want to know what 2-acre lots sell for,
what 5-acre lots sell for, what 10 sells for, what 20 sells for, what 50 sells for. Each of those is a data point
on the PPA curve. (Full details on the PPA Trendline Framework are in Section 2.)
A steep curve means subdivision is lucrative — there is a big spread between large-parcel PPA and
small-lot PPA. A flat or linear curve means there is no real subdivision premium. You are just creating
more work for no payoff. If the curve is flat, this is a flip, not a subdivision.
Step 5 — Design the Split
Now — and only now — do you start thinking about how to draw the lot lines. The priority order is:
1. Easy road frontage lots first — cookie-cutter lots along the road with the most frontage.
2. Maximize waterfront touches — wrap as many lots as possible around any pond, lake, or significant
water feature.
3. Prioritize cleared land — buyers want to see where they can build. Cleared lots sell faster and at a
premium.
4. Flagpole cleanup lots last — use irregular/flagpole lots to capture remaining acreage that does not
fit neatly into the primary layout.
Match the target lot size to what the surrounding development pattern shows. If the area has mostly 2-4
acre lots with houses, target 2-4 acres. If the area is more rural with 8-12 acre parcels, do a conservative
2-3 way split. Do not force a lot size the area does not support.
Different lot sizes within the same subdivision are completely normal and expected. Some lots will be
waterfront, some cleared, some wooded, some flagpole. They do not need to be the same size. Price
them accordingly when comping.

[PAGE 5]
Step 6 — Final Gut Check: Sell Timeline
We want to sell within 30 days ideally, 90 days maximum. Subdivision only makes sense if the lots will
actually move in that timeframe. Creating 12 small lots in an area with no demand means sitting on
inventory, which kills the business model. That is why the area demand check (Step 3) comes before the
subdivision design (Step 5) — no point calculating lot yield if the market cannot absorb it.
The sell timeline is the governor. Do not maximize lot count — maximize lot count WITHIN what
the market will absorb quickly. Over-subdividing in a low-demand area is worse than a
conservative split because you are stuck holding inventory.

2. The PPA Trendline Framework
The PPA (Price Per Acre) Trendline is the single most important analytical tool for property valuation and
subdivision analysis. It is not optional — it must be built mentally on every property evaluation. The
trendline tells the story of the market and where the subject property fits within it.
2.1 — How the Curve Works
The PPA trendline is NOT linear — it is exponential on the low end and flattens to roughly linear on the
high end. This is the fundamental shape of all rural land markets:
Small lots (1-5 acres): PPA is HIGH and rises STEEPLY as acreage decreases. A 2-acre lot might sell at
$15,000/ac while a 5-acre lot might sell at $8,000/ac. That drop is fast and dramatic. Small changes in
acreage cause huge swings in PPA. This is the volatile, exponential part of the curve.
Medium lots (5-20 acres): PPA is moderate and declining more gradually. The curve is still falling but not
as steeply. This is the transition zone.
Large parcels (20+ acres): PPA is LOW and relatively STABLE. A 20-acre parcel might be $3,500/ac and
a 40-acre might be $2,800/ac. The curve has flattened out — PPA is not jumping wildly anymore, it is
stabilizing. This is the linear, predictable part of the curve.
2.2 — Why 50% Below / 100% Above
The comp range is set at 50% below the subject acreage and 100% above (expanding to 50%/200% or
even 100%/300% if needed). This asymmetry exists precisely because of the curve shape:
On the low end (smaller acreage comps): PPA rises exponentially. Small acreage differences cause huge
PPA swings. These comps are harder to trust and riskier to lean on. That is why we only go 50% below —
we are being conservative on the steep, volatile part of the curve.
On the high end (larger acreage comps): PPA flattens out and becomes more linear. The numbers are
stable and predictable. A 30-acre comp and a 50-acre comp might have very similar PPAs. That is why we
go 100% above (or more) — the flat part of the curve is trustworthy, and weighting toward higher-acreage
comps gives a conservative valuation.
Always weight toward the higher-acreage comps (lower PPA) for a conservative valuation. The
flat part of the trendline is where the data is reliable. The steep part is where mistakes get made.
2.3 — Building the Trendline on Every Eval
On every property evaluation — whether subdivision is the play or not — build the mental trendline. Pull
comps at multiple acreage points: 2 acres, 5 acres, 10 acres, 20 acres, and beyond. Each comp is a data
point. Connect them mentally to see the curve shape.
Even when the comps do not perfectly fill every acreage point, use whatever exists in between to establish
ceilings and floors. If you have a 2-acre comp at $15,000/ac and an 8-acre comp at $5,000/ac but nothing
at 5 acres, you can still interpolate — but be cautious on the steep part of the curve because the

[PAGE 7]
exponential shape makes the interpolation less reliable. Any comp in between, even if it is not a perfect
match, gives you a ceiling or floor to narrow the range.
Build SEPARATE curves per comp type when relevant: wooded vs. cleared, waterfront vs. non-waterfront.
Each land type has its own PPA behavior. Do not mix a waterfront 5-acre comp with a landlocked wooded
5-acre comp — they are on different curves.
2.4 — What the Curve Shape Tells You
Steep curve (big spread between large-parcel PPA and small-lot PPA): GOOD subdivision market. The
value created by splitting a large parcel into small lots is significant. This is where the subdivision money is
made — you are moving product from the flat part of the curve up to the steep part.
Flat/linear curve (small-lot PPA and large-parcel PPA are similar): BAD subdivision market. There is no
real premium for small lots. Focus on simple flips.
Metro/lake counties tend to have steeper curves. Deep rural counties tend to have flatter curves. But this
is a generalization — there are rural counties with steep curves. The comps themselves tell you the truth;
let the data speak rather than assumptions.
2.5 — Handling Outliers
Outliers above the trendline are common. They are often off-market sales, bidding wars, or buyers who
overpaid. When you see a comp that sold significantly above the trendline:
Check the listing history — was there a bidding war? Did it go above asking price? That may signal
strong demand, or just one motivated buyer.
Check if it was off-market (Sold-Public Records). Off-market sales are unreliable and should not be
used as comps per standing rules.
Note it, but do not let it pull the valuation up. The trendline built from ceilings and floors across multiple
comps is what matters — not one property that went crazy.
The trendline should get progressively tighter as you identify close ceilings and floors near your subject's
acreage. You are whittling the range down, not chasing outliers.
2.6 — The Subdivision Math
Once the trendline is built, the subdivision math becomes straightforward:
1. The parent parcel sits at [X] PPA on the flat part of the curve (e.g., $3,000/ac for a 24-acre parcel).
2. If split into [Y]-acre lots, the trendline says those sell at [Z] PPA (e.g., $8,000/ac for 5-acre lots).
3. The spread between X and Z is the value created by subdivision.
4. Reality-check: subtract road costs, survey costs, permitting, and the fact that not every lot will hit
top-of-curve PPA.
5. If the net yield still significantly exceeds the as-is value, subdivision is the play.

3. Reading Area Demand
Reading area demand is a zoom-out exercise that must happen before any subdivision design work. It
determines whether subdivision is viable, what lot sizes the market will absorb, and how quickly the lots
will sell. This is not optional — skipping this step has historically led to properties sitting on the market until
they expire worthless.
3.1 — The County-Wide Scan
Zoom out to the county level on Land Insights. Build a mental map of the county's development pattern:
Where are the towns and population centers?
Where are the density clusters — areas with lots of small parcels and structures?
Where does it transition to rural — larger parcels, fewer structures?
Where is the deep rural edge — big tracts, sparse development, no towns nearby?
The subject property falls somewhere on this spectrum. Its position tells you what lot sizes the market will
absorb at that location. Near a town with small-lot clusters? Small lots will sell. In the rural transition zone?
Conservative split. Deep rural? Big lots only — or just flip as-is.
3.2 — Positive Demand Signals
Lots of smaller parcels (2-5 acres) nearby with structures/houses on them
Proximity to a town, city, or population center
A lake or recreation area nearby attracting buyers
Visible new construction activity in the area
General population density — houses, roads, infrastructure
A mix of lot sizes showing market activity across the spectrum
When these signals are present, people are actively choosing to buy small lots here. Creating more small
lots will meet existing demand.
3.3 — Caution Signals
Way out in the sticks — very few houses, mostly large tracts
No town, lake, or attraction nearby
Only big parcels with sparse development — the market favors large acreage
The area looks rough or poorly maintained on Street View
No recent small-lot sales activity in comps
When these signals dominate, the market is telling you that buyers in this area want big tracts. Creating
small lots creates LESS demand, not more. The farther out from civilization, the bigger the lots need to be.
People who want small lots would just live closer to town.

[PAGE 9]
3.4 — Matching Lot Size to Market
The target lot size comes from what is already selling around the subject property. Not what existed 20
years ago, but what people are buying and building on now. If the surrounding development pattern is 2-4
acre lots with houses, target 2-4 acres. If it is 8-12 acre parcels, do a conservative 2-3 way split. Match the
market.
Preferred minimum lot size for subdivision: 2 acres. Once you start selling smaller lots, it takes longer to
sell all of them and the process gets complicated. For most rural subdivision plays, 2-5 acre target lots are
the sweet spot when the market supports it.
The demand check can tell you to walk away from the deal entirely — not just 'do not subdivide'
but 'do not buy this at all' — because if the market does not support the size you are offering, the
property is going to sit. Smaller parcels in areas dominated by big tracts are a risk not just for
subdivision but for the flip itself.

4. Lot Line Design Principles
4.1 — Priority Order for Drawing Lines
When designing a subdivision layout, always work in this order:
Priority 1: Easy road frontage lots. Start with the road that gives the cleanest, most cookie-cutter lots. If
there is a residential road running through or along the property, that is usually the best starting point.
Divide the road frontage into lots of the target acreage.
Priority 2: Maximize waterfront touches. If there is a pond, lake, or significant water feature, wrap as
many lots as possible around it. Waterfront premium is significant — do not leave it on the table. For larger
ponds, multiple waterfront lots work well and buyers can build docks. For very small ponds, one lot with
the pond as its feature may be better than splitting it across multiple lots where nobody gets to enjoy it.
Priority 3: Prioritize cleared land. Buyers want to see where they can build. Lots with cleared areas sell
faster and command higher PPA. When drawing lines, try to include cleared ground in as many lots as
possible. If a lot can have access through cleared land rather than only through timber, that is preferred.
Priority 4: Flagpole cleanup lots. The remaining acreage that does not fit neatly into the primary layout
gets captured with irregular or flagpole lots. These are the outliers, and having a few is normal in every
subdivision. The flagpole strip is the sacrifice zone — the lot body should still be a usable, reasonably
shaped parcel.
4.2 — Lot Shape Rules
Golden width-to-length ratio: 1:2 to 1:4. This gives lots that feel spacious and usable — you can have a
house, yard, outbuildings, and driveway without everything being in a single line.
Maximum acceptable ratio: 1:5 to 1:6. Beyond this, the lot starts feeling like a bowling alley.
Anything skinnier: significant value loss. Before proposing a subdivision, always check if splitting creates
bowling alley lots. If yes, do not subdivide — there is no value-add, only value destruction.
The 'sideways flagpole' hack: When a flagpole lot would otherwise be too deep, run the flagpole strip
horizontally instead of vertically. This keeps the buildable lot body at a usable width-to-length ratio while
still providing road access through the horizontal strip.
4.3 — Different Lot Sizes Are Normal
Lots within a single subdivision do not need to be the same size. Some will be waterfront, some cleared,
some wooded, some flagpole. Different sizes are expected. When lots along a natural feature boundary
(like farmland vs. timber), the lots on each side can absolutely be different sizes.
When the lots are roughly similar in size, you can calculate the average lot size (parent acreage / lot
count) and comp that average to estimate total subdivision yield. When there is a huge disparity in lot
sizes — say 2-acre waterfront lots AND 15-acre timber lots — you must comp each group separately.
Averaging them would badly skew the numbers because of the exponential PPA curve.

[PAGE 11]
4.4 — Estimating Lot Count Quickly
The threshold for pulling subdivision comps is low: if you can see even 2-4 possible splits, that is enough
to warrant checking. The more road frontage, the more obvious the opportunity.
For a quick mental estimate: look at the road frontage and the total acreage. Road frontage tells you how
many lots can touch the road. Total acreage tells you how deep you can go. Both together give you a
rough lot count.
If a property has frontage on two roads, count potential lots from EACH road separately and add them up.
More road frontage = more lots = better.
Try multiple layout variations mentally. You have the advantage of being able to draw visual lines in your
head and test different configurations simultaneously. Use the measuring tool on Land Insights to
sanity-check dimensions.
Timing: aim for 2 minutes per evaluation. If subdivision comps genuinely need 3-4 minutes, take the time.
Do not sacrifice accuracy for speed. But do not waste time either.

5.2 — The Residential Road Access Hack
When a county restricts new lot creation off certain road types, there is sometimes a workaround: a lot that
technically fronts on an allowed road (like a county road) can still have its physical driveway access come
off a different road (like a residential road). The legal frontage is on the county road, but the practical entry
is through the residential road and the cleared area. This gives buyers the best of both worlds.
This is not always possible and depends on county regulations, but it is worth exploring when the
alternative is losing half the subdivision value.
5.3 — Narrow Strips Touching the Road
When you see a narrow strip running from a parcel body down to the road on Land Insights, it is almost
certainly legal access — if it touches the road, it more than likely has legal access. Flag it and note it, but
do not overthink it. The key check is whether the strip width meets the county's minimum frontage
requirement for flagpole lots.

6. Building a Road Through the Middle
6.1 — When to Consider an Internal Road
An internal road makes sense when all of the following are true:
The county has a steep PPA trendline — big spread between large-parcel and small-lot PPA.
There is demand for small lots — surrounding development shows people building on 2-5 acre
parcels.
The property is deep enough that road-frontage-only subdivision wastes significant interior acreage.
The road cost pencils out against the additional lot yield — even at the high cost estimate, the extra
lots generate more revenue than the road costs.
The mental math: 'If a road costs $50,000-$100,000 but I get 5-8 additional lots at significantly higher
PPA, the revenue from those additional lots far exceeds the road cost.'
A square lot illustrates the concept simply: without a road through the middle, you might get 4 lots off the
existing frontage. With a road down the center, you double the frontage and get 8 lots. Each lot is smaller,
which means higher PPA. As long as the market supports the smaller lot size, this is a clear win.
6.2 — Road Cost Estimation
When evaluating whether an internal road is worth building, provide a cost range with three tiers:
Low end: Flat, cleared terrain. Minimal grading. Short road length. Simple gravel construction.
Middle (most likely): Some grading required, moderate length, basic gravel with drainage.
High end: Steep terrain requiring significant grading, longer road, culverts for water crossings, possibly
paving.
If the topography is steep and grading gets expensive, it may make more sense to just subdivide off the
existing road frontage rather than building an internal road. The road is only worth building when the
additional lot yield clearly outweighs the construction cost at the mid-to-high estimate.
6.3 — Wetlands, Creeks, and Rivers
Wetlands: You can build a road through wetlands. Culverts are typically $5,000-$10,000 each, which is
not a dealbreaker. The real question is not 'can I build through it' but 'does the road improve the lot layout
enough to justify it?' If the lots on either side of the road are still mostly wetlands, it is probably not worth it.
If the road unlocks clean buildable lots that just happen to have wetlands in between, go for it.
True marsh: This is different from mapped wetlands. Actual marshy, boggy ground is not something you
should be putting a road through or subdividing. Going around it would be too expensive and
time-consuming.
Small creeks: Culvert — no big deal. The water flows through the tube, you build over it. Just a line item
cost.

[PAGE 14]
Rivers: Completely different conversation. Bridge territory, which is serious money and complexity. Flag it
but do not assume it is part of a subdivision plan.

7. Flagpole Lots
7.1 — What They Are
A flagpole lot has a narrow strip (the 'pole') connecting to the road for legal access, with the main body of
the lot (the 'flag') set back from the road. The narrow strip is just for access — the buildable area is in the
body of the lot.
Flagpole lots are normal and expected in any subdivision. They are how you capture acreage that does
not have direct road frontage. The key is that the body of the lot must be usable — wide enough, flat
enough, and accessible enough to build on.
7.2 — County Frontage Minimums
Counties have minimum frontage requirements for flagpole lots — typically 30-60+ feet. If the flagpole strip
is narrower than the county minimum, the lot cannot be created. This is a hard stop.
If a property has multiple narrow access strips that are each below the county minimum, this is a
dead end for subdivision. Flag with the warning emoji so the owner knows immediately.
7.3 — When Flagpole Access Kills Subdivision
If the entire property is accessed through a single narrow flagpole strip — one access point — then the
property functions as one lot regardless of how much acreage is in the back. You cannot create multiple
subdivided lots when there is only one way in. All the cleared land, the great shape in the back, the
beautiful views — none of it matters if you cannot give each new lot its own legal access.

8. When NOT to Subdivide
Approximately 85-90% of properties that come across the desk will NOT be subdividable. The default
assumption should always be 'this is a flip' — subdivision potential is the exception, not the rule. Only
upgrade to 'subdivision opportunity' when shape, road frontage, acreage, and area demand all line up
together.
8.1 — Walk-Away Signal: Narrow Shape
If the property is narrow all the way through — long rectangle with the short side on the road — any split
just creates skinnier bowling alleys. Nobody wants a lot where they can only access the property in a
single line. Everything — house, shed, garden — would have to be arranged single-file. This is
undesirable regardless of acreage.
There is a reason flagpole lots are flagpole lots: the body is thick and usable, only the access strip is
narrow. If the ENTIRE lot is narrow, there is no fix. Do not subdivide.
8.2 — Walk-Away Signal: No Market Demand (The Dangerous Trap)
This is the most dangerous scenario because it looks like a good deal on paper. The property
has a decent shape. It has road frontage. You COULD physically split it. But the area is telling
you nobody is buying small lots here.
This is where real money gets lost. If the surrounding area only has big tracts, no small-lot construction, no
town nearby, and only large-acreage sales in the comps — then creating small lots creates LESS
demand, not more. The property may already be a harder sell at its current size because it is small for the
market.
Even the parent parcel at its current acreage might barely have demand in these deep rural markets.
Splitting it just makes things worse. This is how properties sit on the market until they expire worthless.
The test: If the market does not support the size you are offering, and splitting would create even
less demand, then either flip as-is or pass on the deal entirely. Do not force subdivision where
the market is saying no.
8.3 — Walk-Away Signal: Single Access Point
If the property is accessed through one narrow flagpole strip with a large buildable area in the back, it
functions as one lot. You cannot run multiple flagpole strips through a single narrow neck — they would
either be too skinny to meet county minimums or eat up all the usable frontage on the strip. One access
point = one lot.

9. Comping the Subdivision
9.1 — The Dual-Comp Approach
When subdivision is the play, always pull comps at TWO acreage levels:
As-is comps: What is the parent parcel worth at its current acreage? This is the baseline — the value if
subdivision does not happen.
Subdivision comps: What do the target lot sizes sell for? This is the ceiling — the value if subdivision is
executed successfully.
The spread between these two numbers is the value created by subdivision. Present both to the owner so
they can see the opportunity.
9.2 — Average Lot Size Method
When the subdivided lots are roughly similar in size:
Parent acreage / lot count = average lot size. Comp that average lot size to get the PPA. Multiply PPA by
parent acreage to get total subdivision yield.
This works when the lots cluster in a similar range — say all between 4-7 acres. The exponential curve
does not vary dramatically across this small range, so averaging is acceptable.
9.3 — Group Comping Method
When the lots have a huge size disparity — say 2-acre waterfront lots AND 15-acre timber lots — you
must comp each group separately. The PPA curve is exponential. A 2-acre lot might be $15,000/ac while
a 15-acre lot is $3,000/ac. Averaging $9,000/ac across all lots would be drastically inaccurate — it would
overvalue the big lots and undervalue the small ones. Comp each group at its own acreage point on the
trendline, then sum the groups for total yield.

[PAGE 18]
10. Pricing Output: Market Value & List Price
Every valuation delivers TWO numbers:
1. Actual Market Value: The best estimate of what the property is truly worth based on comps, trendline
position, and property characteristics. This is the clean number based on the data.
2. List Price (5% Discount): Market value discounted by approximately 5% to provide a margin of error
and ensure the property is priced to move. This is the price the property would be listed at for sale.
The acquisition target is approximately 50% of the list price. The owner uses the market value to avoid
missing deals by a few thousand dollars, and the list price to set the acquisition target.
Always give SPECIFIC prices, not just ranges. A range is acceptable when uncertain, but always
include a specific recommended value. The owner needs concrete numbers to make offers.

11. Real-World Property Examples
The following examples were walked through visually on Land Insights during training. Each example
includes the property details, what the land looked like, the decision made, and the reasoning behind it.
These are the reference cases for pattern recognition going forward.
Example 1: Pamela Adams — Full Subdivision (With Residential Road)
Location: Near Eddins Rd / Zoar Rd area | Acreage: Large parcel (150+ acres) | County: SC
What the land looked like: A massive parcel with Eddins Road (yellow/residential) running through the
middle, Zoar Road (orange/county) along the eastern boundary, and Abrams Creek along the western
edge. The property had a mix of cleared fields and timber. A significant pond/lake sat in the interior of the
property. Multiple existing parcels (James E McLain, Michael E Valdaliso) were carved out within the
property boundaries.
The subdivision plan (Version 1 — with residential road access): Red lot lines were drawn creating
approximately 15-20 lots. The residential road (Eddins Rd) served as the primary subdivision spine. Lots
along this road were clean, cookie-cutter, roughly equal sizes. Multiple lots were wrapped around the pond
to capture waterfront premium. Cleared areas were prioritized. Lots along Zoar Rd were slightly more
irregular, with flagpole lots in the upper right to capture remaining acreage. Different sized lots throughout
— completely normal.
Key design decisions: Started with the residential road because it was easier and more cookie-cutter.
Maximized waterfront touches on the pond. Prioritized cleared land. Flagpole lots used as cleanup. The
residential road did the heavy lifting for access and created the most lots.
Example 2: Pamela Adams — Constrained Subdivision (No Residential
Road Access)
Same property, different scenario: county does not allow subdivision off the residential road.
The subdivision plan (Version 2 — Zoar Rd only): Dramatically fewer lots. A large dead zone in the
northwest corner of the property could not be reached from Zoar Rd, creating a single large remainder
parcel. Lots were larger and more irregular. The residential road was used as a physical access hack —
lots fronted on Zoar Rd for legal purposes but driveways could access through the cleared area near the
residential road.
The sideways flagpole hack was used on the upper right — the flagpole strip ran horizontally instead of
vertically to prevent the lot body from becoming too deep. Waterfront priority was maintained even with the
constraint.
Value impact: Estimated 20-50% loss in total subdivision value compared to Version 1. Fewer lots, bigger
lots (lower PPA), dead zone acreage sold at the lower large-parcel PPA. This demonstrates why
identifying road type restrictions is critical — it can be the difference between a home run and a mediocre
deal.

Lesson: The road type restriction reduced the deal value by 20-50%. When flagging subdivision
potential, always identify the road types and note any potential restrictions. This is not a
footnote — it is a deal-defining factor.
Example 3: Louis Gauthier — Conservative Split (3 Lots)
Location: 844 Chase Rd, Meshoppen, PA | Acreage: 24.05 | County: Susquehanna County, PA
Link: app.landinsights.co/data?parcel=164399721
What the land looked like: A 24-acre parcel with road frontage on Chase Rd (yellow/residential) on the
north side and State Route 3004 (orange/county) on the south side. The property had a mix of cleared
areas and heavy timber. A creek/river ran along the eastern edge near Elk Lake Rd. The surrounding area
was very rural — large tracts everywhere, Commonwealth of Pennsylvania state land nearby, sparse
development. No cluster of small-lot activity.
The subdivision plan: Three lots, roughly 8 acres each average. The top lot used Chase Rd frontage. The
bottom lot used State Route 3004 frontage and picked up the creek/river frontage for a waterfront
premium. The middle lot accessed via Chase Rd. The lots were not equal sizes — the layout worked with
the natural features.
Why only 3 lots: The area demanded it. Zooming out to the county level showed big rural tracts
dominating. No small-lot development clusters. No town or lake nearby. The market supports bigger lots.
A conservative 2-3 way split still moves up the PPA curve (three 8-acre lots at higher PPA than one
24-acre parcel) but does not overshoot the demand.
Lesson: Match the split to the market. Do not maximize lot count in an area that cannot absorb
small lots. Conservative splits still make money without the risk of sitting on unsold inventory.

Example 6: John E Karabin — Could Split But SHOULD NOT (The
Dangerous Trap)
Location: 1084 McGavin Rd, Meshoppen, PA | Acreage: 4.61 | County: Susquehanna County, PA
Link: app.landinsights.co/data?parcel=117216538
What the land looked like: A 4.61-acre parcel on McGavin Rd with an irregular but workable shape — not
a bowling alley. The property had road frontage on McGavin Rd (yellow/residential). It was surrounded by
open fields and large tracts. A creek/river ran along the north side. Very few structures in the immediate
area. Last purchased for $20,000 thirteen years ago.
Why this is the most dangerous example: The shape works. The road frontage is there. You COULD
physically split this into two roughly 2-acre lots. Everything about the property says 'yes, subdivide.' But
the AREA says no. Zooming out to the county level shows massive open fields, huge parcels, barely any
structures, deep rural. Nobody is buying 2-acre lots out here.
The critical insight: Even the 4.61-acre parcel itself barely has demand in this market because it is small
for the area. The surrounding parcels are all much bigger. Splitting it into two 2-acre lots would create
parcels that are even MORE out of step with what buyers want. You would be creating less demand, not
more. The property would sit on the market until it expires worthless.
Real-world consequence: Properties like this have been purchased in the past and they sat on
the market until they expired worthless. This is not theoretical. This is money lost. When the
market does not support the size you are offering, and splitting makes it worse, walk away
from the subdivision play — and potentially the deal itself.
Lesson: 'Can I subdivide?' and 'Should I subdivide?' are completely different questions.
Physical ability means nothing without market demand. The area demand check can tell you to
walk away from the deal entirely.

Example 8: Mapledale LLC — Road Through the Middle (Two Adjacent
Parcels)
Location: King William County, VA | Acreage: 358.51 + 353.75 (712+ total) | Owner: Mapledale LLC
Links: app.landinsights.co/data?parcel=140889297 and app.landinsights.co/data?parcel=140898050
What the land looked like: Two massive adjacent parcels owned by the same LLC totaling 712+ acres in
King William County, VA. The Jacks Creek Rd property (353.75 acres, address: 1626 Jacks Creek Rd)
already had a road running through it, slightly to the right, which could be centered for better lot
distribution. The other parcel (358.51 acres) was along Route 621 with some steep topography in the
lower portion. Multiple roads surrounded the properties: Routes 621, 629, 632, 678, and Green Level Rd.
The area context: King William County showed a mix of development. Comp data visible on the map
showed an extremely steep PPA trendline: 2.23 acres selling at $132,290/ac PPA while 6 acres was at
$14,170/ac PPA and 4 acres at $12,500/ac PPA. This is a massive spread indicating a very strong
subdivision market.
Why build a road through the middle: At 350+ acres each, these properties are far too large to sell at a
reasonable PPA as-is. The trendline is extremely steep, meaning small lots command enormously higher
PPAs. A road through the middle of the 621 parcel would double the effective frontage and allow
subdivision into smaller lots (2-5 acres) that match the area demand. Even with road construction costs
($50k-$100k+), the lot yield from smaller parcels at that PPA curve would far exceed the road investment.
The topography factor: Steep terrain on the lower portion of the 621 parcel adds grading cost. If the
grading gets too expensive, the fallback is to just subdivide off existing road frontage — which still works
because there is significant frontage available. The road through the middle is the maximum-yield play;
subdividing off existing frontage is the conservative fallback.
Lesson: When you have massive acreage, an extremely steep PPA trendline, and surrounding
demand for small lots, building an internal road is a clear value-add play. Always evaluate road

cost against lot yield. If topography makes the road too expensive, fall back to existing-frontage
subdivision.
