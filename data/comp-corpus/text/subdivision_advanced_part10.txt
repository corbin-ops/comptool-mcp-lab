HIDDEN VALUE IN SUBDIVISION — THE CORE CONCEPT
The most profitable subdivision deals are the ones that other people MISS. Most investors look at a property and see one thing — a
single parcel to flip. They calculate the as-is value, make an offer at 50%, and move on. They completely miss the fact that the
property could be subdivided into 5, 8, or even 10+ lots that are each worth significantly more per acre than the whole parcel. That
oversight is where DewClaw makes the real money.
The user specifically emphasized that these opportunities 'hide in plain sight.' A property with three narrow access points from a road
looks unremarkable to most people — they see weird entry points and don't think anything of it. The user sees three flagpole lot
opportunities that could triple the property's total value. A large property with enough road frontage looks like a normal big lot to most
people. The user sees 8 individual lots that each sell at a higher PPA than the whole parcel. This advanced subdivision thinking is
what separates the DewClaw approach from 99% of other land flippers who only do simple flips.
FLAGPOLE LOTS FROM HISTORICAL LOT SPLITS — HIDDEN IN PLAIN SIGHT
One of the most important hidden value plays the user taught is recognizing flagpole lot opportunities that were CREATED by
previous owners without anyone realizing it. Here is how this happens:
A property owner once had a large parcel — say 60 acres. Over the years, they sold off smaller lots from the edges of their property.
Maybe they sold a 5-acre lot on the left side to a neighbor. Then a 3-acre lot on the right side to another buyer. Then a 7-acre lot off
the back. Each time they sold a piece, the remaining property's shape changed. What was once a clean rectangle became an
irregular shape with narrow access strips where the sold lots used to connect to the road.
Now the remaining 45 acres has three narrow entry points — maybe 50-60 feet wide each — where the sold-off lots created gaps
along the road frontage. To most people looking at this property on a map, these narrow strips look like odd remnants of the original
parcel. Nobody thinks twice about them. They are just weird shape features.
But the user sees SUBDIVISION OPPORTUNITIES. Each of those narrow access strips IS a potential flagpole lot access point.
Three 50-foot-wide access strips = three flagpole lots, each extending from the road back into the larger buildable area of the
property. Instead of selling one 45-acre parcel at the large-lot PPA, you sell three ~15-acre lots at a significantly higher per-acre
price. The total revenue could be 50-100%+ more than selling the whole parcel.
The user was very clear: 'These are the things that are hidden in plain sight, but people don't even see that, just like you don't see it.
So now you're starting to connect the dots — oh wow, if I can identify it, then imagine how many people don't, and how much money
we could capitalize on.' This is the competitive advantage. Identifying these plays is how DewClaw finds deals that nobody else sees.
How to Spot Historical Lot Splits on the Map
When looking at a property on Land Insights, look for these visual clues that suggest previous lot splits created flagpole opportunities:
Narrow strips of the parent parcel touching the road: If the property has one or more thin extensions reaching out to the road,
separated by other parcels on either side, those thin extensions are likely remnants from when the owner sold off the lots on either
side. Each thin extension is a potential flagpole access strip.
Multiple small access points instead of one wide frontage: If the property touches the road in multiple places with gaps in
between (where other people's parcels front the road), each of those access points is a flagpole entry. Count them — each one could
be a separate lot.
Neighboring parcels that were clearly carved out of a larger parent: Look at the surrounding parcels. If they look like they were
pieces of a once-larger property (similar orientations, irregular shapes that fit together like puzzle pieces), the remaining property
likely has flagpole access strips from the carve-outs.

ROAD-BUILDING MATH — THE COST-TO-REVENUE FORMULA
The Correct Formula
When evaluating whether a road-building subdivision play makes financial sense, the formula is:
TOTAL INVESTMENT COST = Acquisition price + Road construction + Utilities (water, sewer, electricity if needed) + Any
other development costs
TOTAL REVENUE = Sum of all individual lot sale prices after subdivision
The total investment cost should be approximately 50-60% of the total revenue. This means for every dollar you spend on
acquisition and development, you should get approximately $1.67-$2.00 back in lot sales. That is a healthy spread that makes the
deal worth the time, effort, complexity, and risk of doing a subdivision with road construction.
On BIGGER properties where the absolute dollar amounts are larger, the acceptable ratio can stretch to 65-70%. The reason: even
at 65-70% cost-to-revenue, the DOLLAR SPREAD on a large deal can be very significant. If total investment is $350,000 and total
revenue is $500,000 at 70% ratio, the profit is $150,000. That is worth doing even though the percentage margin is thinner than the
ideal 50-60% range. It is all about how much spread (profit in dollars) actually exists.
How to Calculate and Present This
When you identify a subdivision play that involves road construction or utility installation, calculate and present the math explicitly in
the deliverable:
Step 1: Determine the as-is value of the whole property and calculate your offer price (50% of as-is). This is the ACQUISITION
COST.
Step 2: Estimate the road construction cost. Look up gravel road cost per linear foot in the county/state. Measure the road distance
using the Land Insights ruler tool. Multiply linear feet × cost per foot = estimated road cost.
Step 3: Note any utility costs if applicable (water, sewer, electric hookups). These vary significantly by location — note them as items
to investigate rather than precise estimates unless you can find local cost data.
Step 4: Pull comps at EACH specific lot size you are proposing. If the subdivision creates four 10-acre lots and two 7-acre lots, pull
comps for both 10ac and 7ac sizes separately because each size has its own PPA.
Step 5: Calculate TOTAL REVENUE by multiplying each lot's estimated PPA by its acreage, then summing all lots.
Step 6: Calculate the cost-to-revenue ratio: (Acquisition + Road + Utilities) ÷ Total Revenue × 100 = X%. Present this percentage.
Step 7: Present it clearly: 'Acquisition at 50%: $150K + Road: $20K + Utilities: $5K = $175K total investment. Total revenue from 6
lots: $340K. Cost-to-revenue ratio: 51%. Spread: $165K.'
If the ratio is 50-60% = strong deal, recommend it. If 60-70% = workable on bigger properties, present it with a note about the thinner
margin. If above 70% = the spread probably isn't worth the complexity, but still present the numbers and let the user decide.
Roads Can Curve Around Features
A subdivision road does NOT have to be a straight line from one end of the property to the other. Roads can CURVE around features
that would be expensive or impractical to cross: wetlands (building a road through wetlands requires expensive fill and permitting),
steep slopes (road grade has limits — too steep and vehicles can't safely use it), timber stands (might be cheaper to go around
mature timber than to clear a path through it), creeks and streams (crossing requires a bridge or culvert).
When a road needs to cross a wet area or small stream, a CULVERT can be installed. A culvert is a pipe or tunnel under the road
that allows water to flow through while the road passes over it. Culverts are a standard construction element for rural roads — they
cost more than just grading a dirt road on dry ground, but they are a proven, routine solution. The cost of culverts should be factored
into your road cost estimate.
When estimating road cost for a curved road: measure the ACTUAL path the road would take (including curves) using the Land
Insights ruler tool, not just the straight-line distance. A road that curves around a wetland area might be 30-50% longer than a straight
shot through it, but the additional road cost is likely much less than the cost of filling wetlands and getting environmental permits.

LOT VALUE DIFFERENTIATION WITHIN A SUBDIVISION
Not every lot in a subdivision is worth the same amount. When you create multiple lots from a parent parcel, each lot should be
valued based on its SPECIFIC traits — just like comping any individual property. The key factors that create value differences
between lots in the same subdivision:
Road Type Matters Per Lot
If the parent property has one side on a PAVED road and another side bordering a DIRT road, the lots fronting the paved road are
worth MORE than the lots fronting the dirt road. This is exactly the same as comping any normal property — road type affects value.
When designing the lot configuration, put the PREMIUM lots on the paved road side (these will sell faster and at higher PPA) and the
DISCOUNTED lots on the dirt road side (these will take slightly longer and sell at lower PPA).
Interior lots fronting a NEW private road (the road you built through the property) typically sell for 5-10% LESS than lots fronting the
existing public road. This mirrors the general road hierarchy — a new gravel private road is not as desirable as an established public
road.
Flagpole Lots vs Standard Lots
Flagpole lots — lots with a narrow access strip leading to a wider buildable area in the back — are worth LESS than standard lots
with full road frontage. How much less depends on the DEPTH of the flagpole:
Shallow flagpole (short access strip, buildable area is close to the road): SLIGHTLY less than a standard lot — maybe 5-10%
discount. The access strip is short, so getting to the buildable area is quick and convenient. The inconvenience is minimal.
Deep flagpole (long access strip, buildable area is far from the road): Essentially the same value as a lot on a dirt road because
the buyer needs a LONG driveway through the access strip to reach their building site. It is functionally the same experience as
driving down a long dirt road to reach a property. The user specifically said: 'Normally, when a flagpole lot is super deep, you actually
need a road going back into it. And normally, if it's not developed and it's just vacant land, it's normally just a dirt road to go into there,
so that's essentially the exact same thing as a normal lot on a dirt road.'
This means: when designing a subdivision with flagpole lots, the shallower the flagpole, the more value it retains. If you can create
flagpole lots where the access strip is short and the buildable area is relatively close to the road, the value penalty is small. If the
flagpole forces a very long access road, the lot drops to dirt-road-lot pricing.
Land Type Differences Per Lot
If the parent property has different land types in different areas — cleared farmland on one side and timber on the other, pasture at
the front and wetlands in the back — split along those natural boundaries to create lots with DISTINCT land types that appeal to
DIFFERENT buyer pools. A cleared farmland lot appeals to agricultural buyers and homebuilders. A timbered lot appeals to
recreational/hunting buyers. A wetlands lot appeals to duck hunters (if 20+ acres). Each lot type has its own PPA and its own buyer
pool.
Pull comps at each lot size AND for each land type. The cleared 10ac lot on the paved road might be worth $8,000/ac while the
timbered 10ac lot on the dirt road side might be worth $4,000/ac. These differences should be reflected in your per-lot valuations.
Wetlands Per Lot After Splitting
As covered extensively in Part 2 (Wetlands), lot-splitting a wetlands property creates cleaner-looking individual lots even though the
total wetlands haven't changed. A lot with 10% wetlands in the back looks much better than a whole parcel with 50% wetlands
throughout. When calculating per-lot values, assess the wetlands impact on EACH lot individually: the clean lot gets near-full value
with minor discount, the wetlands lot gets priced as hunting/recreational land.

PULLING COMPS AT EACH TARGET LOT SIZE — PRECISION MATTERS
When presenting a subdivision value-add scenario, you need comps at EACH SPECIFIC LOT SIZE you are proposing. This is critical
because the PPA curve is exponential — every acreage point on the curve has a different PPA. You cannot use a 20-acre comp's
PPA to estimate the value of an 8-acre lot. The 8-acre lot has a significantly higher PPA because it is on a different point on the
curve.
If your subdivision creates lots of different sizes — for example, four 10-acre lots along the road and two 7-acre flagpole lots — you
need TWO sets of comps: one set showing what 10-acre lots trade at in this market, and another set showing what 7-acre lots trade
at. Each lot size might have a different PPA, and your total revenue calculation needs to reflect those differences.
How to do this simultaneously with the as-is comp process: when you zoom out on Land Insights and see the comp dots, you are
already seeing a range of acreages. Note the PPAs at your target lot sizes as you scan. If the MLS comp filter allows acreage range
adjustment, change the range to focus on your target lot size. Alternatively, use the comp data you already see on the map to
interpolate — if 5ac comps trade at $10K/ac and 15ac comps trade at $6K/ac, your 8ac lots should be around $8K/ac on the curve.
WHEN ROAD-BUILDING MAKES SENSE VS WHEN IT DOESN'T
Road-building makes sense when the SPREAD between total investment and total revenue is significant enough to justify the
additional cost, time, and complexity. The user's threshold: total investment (acquisition + road + utilities) should be approximately
50-60% of total revenue from lot sales. On bigger properties, up to 65-70% because the dollar profit is still large.
When Road-Building MAKES Sense
Large acreage with strong demand for smaller lots in the area AND road frontage alone can't support enough lots. If you have 80
acres with 500ft of road frontage, you might get 5 lots from the frontage alone. But building a 1,200ft gravel road through the middle
could give you 12+ lots (5 from the road frontage plus 7 interior lots). If the road costs $30K but the additional 7 lots generate $350K
in revenue, the math overwhelmingly works.
The key indicators: property has significantly more acreage than the road frontage can serve. Surrounding lot sizes are relatively
small (5-10ac) indicating strong subdivision demand. The PPA curve is steep (big difference between large-lot PPA and small-lot
PPA). County ordinances allow the number of lots you are proposing.
When Road-Building DOESN'T Make Sense
Small properties where the road cost eats too much of the margin. If you have 20 acres and a road costs $25K but only creates 2
additional lots worth $30K total, the math doesn't work — $25K cost for $30K additional revenue is razor-thin.
Areas where there is no demand for smaller lots. If surrounding properties are all 40+ acres and nobody is buying small lots, building
a road to create small lots is building a product nobody wants.
Properties with terrain that makes road construction prohibitively expensive — steep slopes, deep wetlands that need extensive fill,
rocky terrain that needs blasting. The road cost can balloon beyond what the lot revenue supports.
When the cost-to-revenue ratio exceeds 70% even on a big property. At that point, the spread is too thin to justify the complexity,
time, and risk of road construction. A simple flip is safer and faster.
