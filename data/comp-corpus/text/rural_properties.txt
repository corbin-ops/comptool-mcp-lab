DewClaw — Part 9: Rural Property Evaluation
 FULLY EXPANDED — How Extreme Rural Location Affects Value
 Identifying, Flagging, and Pricing Properties in the Middle of Nowhere
WHY RURAL EVALUATION MATTERS
DewClaw operates in the rural recreational land market — every property the business evaluates is rural to some degree. That is the
entire business model. But there is a massive difference between 'rural' and 'SUPER rural.' A property 15 minutes outside a small
town on a maintained gravel road with houses scattered around is rural. A property 2 hours from the nearest town on an
unmaintained dirt road with nothing but timber company land for miles in every direction is SUPER rural. That difference can impact
value by 20-50%.
The user identified this as an important area of evaluation because extreme rural isolation can KILL value on smaller properties.
When a property is so far from everything that Google Maps Street View doesn't even cover the area, when there are no houses
visible for miles on satellite, when the nearest town is 1-3 hours away — that level of isolation dramatically shrinks the buyer pool.
Fewer buyers means less demand, which means lower prices and longer selling times. This is not a minor adjustment — it can be a
20-50% discount depending on severity.
The frequency of encountering super rural properties is relatively low — the user estimated approximately 1 out of every 40-50
properties. But when you do encounter one, you need to recognize it immediately, flag it appropriately, and price it correctly. Missing
the rural discount on a super rural property means overpricing it, which means it sits on the market unsold. Recognizing it means
pricing it to sell within the 7-90 day target.
HOW TO IDENTIFY SUPER RURAL PROPERTIES
The Three Visual Indicators
When the user looks at a property and thinks 'this is super rural,' there are three things that immediately signal it:
1. Not many houses around it: When you zoom out on Land Insights or Google Maps and look at the surrounding area, there are
very few or NO houses visible on satellite. Instead, you see vast stretches of timber, empty land, or large agricultural operations. The
absence of residential development is the clearest signal that this area has very low demand for smaller residential lots. People are
not building here. That tells you something important about the market.
2. Nothing but big properties everywhere: The surrounding parcels are all LARGE — 50, 100, 200+ acres owned by timber
companies, farming operations, or long-term landowners. There are no small 5-10 acre lots with homes. No subdivisions, no
neighborhoods, no development. When every surrounding property is massive, it tells you two things: first, the area is deeply rural
with no development pressure. Second, buyers in this area want BIG properties, not small ones. If your subject is a small 10-acre lot
surrounded by 200-acre timber tracts, it is a small fish in a big pond — there is very little demand for small lots this far out.
3. It is just FAR from everything: When you zoom out on the map, the property is a long, long way from any town, city, or
population center. The user described this as properties that are 1-3 hours from the nearest town. States like Oklahoma, Kentucky,
Michigan, and other deeply rural states have areas where you can drive for hours and see nothing but trees and farmland. The
distance from services, convenience, and civilization itself reduces the property's appeal to most buyers.
Additional Indicators of Extreme Rural Isolation
Google Maps Street View doesn't exist there: This is a particularly telling indicator. Google Street View cars have covered most
roads in the United States. If Street View DOESN'T exist on the roads near the property, it means those roads are so remote, so
lightly traveled, and so far from anything that Google didn't bother sending a car there. When this happens, note it — it is a strong
signal that the area is extremely isolated. You will need to rely entirely on satellite imagery for visual assessment because
ground-level verification is not available.
Road quality in super rural areas: In extremely rural areas, roads are often gravel or dirt, and they may NOT be well-maintained.
The county or local government has limited resources and prioritizes roads closer to population centers. A dirt road 2 hours from

The 'zoom out' test: The user specifically said to ZOOM OUT on the map so you can see the area context. It doesn't take complex

HOW EXTREME RURAL LOCATION AFFECTS VALUE
The Rural Discount: 20-50% Depending on Severity
The user identified a discount range of 20-50% for super rural properties. The exact discount within that range depends on several
factors:
How far from the nearest town: A property that is 30-45 minutes from a small town is rural but manageable — maybe 10-15% rural
discount at most. A property that is 1-2 hours from the nearest town of any size is significantly isolated — 20-30% discount. A
property that is 2-3+ hours from any town, deep in timber country or mountain wilderness with nothing around — 35-50% discount.
The further from civilization, the smaller the buyer pool and the deeper the discount.
What the surrounding area looks like: If the property is rural but there are SOME houses scattered around, some signs of human
habitation and land use, the area shows SOME demand for residential use. Discount on the lower end (20-25%). If the property is
surrounded by nothing but large timber tracts and empty land with zero residential development visible for miles — nobody is living
here, nobody WANTS to live here — discount on the higher end (35-50%).
Road quality and access: Even within super rural areas, road quality varies. A property on a maintained gravel road that is
reasonably smooth and accessible year-round is better than a property on an unmaintained seasonal dirt road that turns to mud in
rain and might be impassable in winter. The user noted that road maintenance in super rural areas is often poor because local
governments don't prioritize remote roads. If the access road is particularly bad — deep ruts, no maintenance, barely a two-track —
that compounds the rural discount because every visit to the property is an unpleasant, car-damaging experience.
Small Acreage in Super Rural Areas — A Particular Problem
The user specifically highlighted a critical nuance: SMALL acreage properties in super rural areas are especially problematic. The
reasoning is logical: people who want to live or recreate in extremely rural areas typically want BIG properties. They want 50, 100,
200+ acres for hunting, farming, timber, or just having vast private space. That is the whole point of being out in the middle of
nowhere — you want SPACE.
A small 5-10 acre lot in the middle of super rural country surrounded by 200-acre tracts does not appeal to this buyer pool. The rural
buyer wants the big property next door, not the small lot. And the suburban buyer who might want 5-10 acres for a homesite doesn't
want to be 2 hours from the nearest town. The property falls into a gap between two buyer pools — too small for rural buyers, too
remote for suburban buyers. This gap shrinks the buyer pool dramatically and can push the discount toward the higher end (40-50%).
Conversely, LARGER acreage in super rural areas is less problematic because the property actually matches what rural buyers
want. A 100-acre hunting property deep in the woods is exactly what a certain buyer is looking for. They WANT the isolation, they
WANT the big acreage, and they are willing to drive 2 hours to get to their hunting camp. For these larger properties, the rural
discount is smaller (maybe 15-25%) because the property matches the buyer pool's preferences. The user said: 'If it's a little bit
bigger, you don't really need to discount it too much. Just know that it's rural.'
Signs That Indicate 'This Area Wants Big Properties'
When you look at a super rural area on the map and see that all surrounding properties are large (50-200+ acres), that is a market
signal. It tells you: 1) The area is deeply rural with no development pressure pushing toward smaller lots. 2) Buyers in this area prefer
and purchase large tracts, not small lots. 3) If your subject is significantly smaller than the surrounding properties, it is mismatched
with the local demand. 4) Subdivision of a large property into small lots would likely NOT work here because there is no demand for
small lots this far out.
This connects directly to the subdivision framework: one of the key rules is 'check what lot sizes are selling in the area.' In a super
rural area, the answer is 'big lots' — which means creating small lots through subdivision would likely fail. Flag this in the deliverable
so the user doesn't pursue a subdivision play that has no market.

COMPING SUPER RURAL PROPERTIES
When Comps Are Also Super Rural — Use Them
If you find comps in the same super rural area, and those comps are ALSO in equally remote, equally isolated conditions — those
are GOOD COMPS. They reflect the market reality of that specific area. The buyers who purchased those comps already factored in
the isolation, the poor road access, and the distance from town. Their purchase prices reflect the actual market value of super rural
land in that area.
In this situation, you do NOT need to apply an additional rural discount on top of these comps. The comps already ARE rural and
already reflect the rural market. Just comp normally using those rural comps as your data set. This is the same logic as comping
flood zone properties in an area where everything is flood zone — the condition is already baked into the market data.
When Your Subject Is MORE Rural Than the Comps
If you find comps in the broader county but your subject is significantly MORE rural than those comps — for example, the comps are
near a small town 20 minutes away but your subject is deep in timber country 2 hours from anything — then you DO need to discount
from those comps. The comps reflect a less-isolated market than where your subject sits. Apply the rural discount based on how
much more isolated your subject is compared to the comps.
Sparse Comps in Super Rural Areas
Super rural areas frequently have very sparse comp data. There just aren't many land transactions happening in the middle of
nowhere. When comps are sparse: widen your acreage range aggressively (AUTO, then 50% below / 200% above, then wider if
needed). Use active listings as ceiling references with appropriate discounts (20-30%). Build the best story you can from available
data. Note in the deliverable that comp data is limited due to the rural nature of the area.
The user's guidance on sparse comps applies especially here: 'Keep on looking until you actually have comps so that you can build
out a storyline.' Don't deliver a valuation with zero comp support even in super rural areas. Widen the range, use the PPA trendline,
use active listings — find SOMETHING to anchor your story to.
Road Access in Super Rural Areas — Flag for User to Verify
In super rural areas, road quality is a major concern that you often CANNOT verify from satellite or even from Street View (which may
not exist). The user specifically said to flag road access so they can triple-check and confirm it themselves. They will be talking to the
seller and can ask about road conditions directly. In your deliverable, note: '■■ Super rural area — road access and condition should
be verified with seller. Road maintenance may be limited due to remote location.' Also put road access verification in Section 9
(Questions to Ask Seller).
The user also clarified that you should COMP the property assuming normal/good access. The rural discount you apply is for the
ISOLATION (distance from everything, limited demand), not for bad road access. If the road turns out to be terrible, the user will
adjust further based on their conversation with the seller. Don't double-discount by applying both a rural discount AND a bad road
discount when you don't actually know the road condition.

SUPER RURAL PROPERTIES AS HUNTING LAND
This connects to the wetlands evaluation section, but the user expanded on it specifically in the context of rural properties. Super
rural properties — especially larger ones with significant wetlands, timber, or natural habitat — can be marketed as HUNTING
PROPERTIES. Hunters don't care about isolation. In fact, they PREFER it. Isolation means privacy, quiet, less human interference
with wildlife, and a better hunting experience.
When a super rural property butts up to STATE LAND, NATIONAL FOREST, or WILDLIFE MANAGEMENT AREAS, this is actually a
POSITIVE for hunting buyers. They can hunt on the adjacent public land as well as their own property, effectively expanding their
hunting area. The user said this adds maybe 2-3% to the value — not a huge premium, but a positive feature to note.
Wetlands on super rural properties are not always a negative for the hunting buyer pool. Wetlands attract DUCKS, WATERFOWL,
and other WILDLIFE because there is more water and habitat. Hunters actually PREFER wetlands for this reason — marshy swamp
land that is terrible for building is IDEAL for duck hunting. The user specifically said: 'Don't always throw out wetlands. Hunters don't
really care. They could obviously walk on that stuff. They actually prefer it because then that could attract ducks and a lot more
wildlife.'
However — and this is critical — while the hunting buyer pool values these features, it is the ONLY buyer pool. You cannot build a
house on 100% wetlands. You cannot install septic. The property has essentially ONE use case (hunting/recreation) and ONE buyer
pool (hunters/outdoor enthusiasts). To sell it within the 7-90 day target, you need to price it conservatively because the buyer pool is
limited. The property can sell — there IS a market — but it needs to be priced aggressively to attract the right buyer quickly.
The minimum lot size for a marketable hunting property remains approximately 20 acres. Below 20 acres of remote hunting land,
there is very little buyer interest — it's not enough area to hunt meaningfully. For super rural hunting properties, bigger is generally
better: 40, 80, 100+ acres of remote wilderness with wildlife habitat is genuinely attractive to serious hunters.
THE CONVENIENCE FACTOR — WHY RURAL DISCOUNTS EXIST
The user explained the fundamental reason why rural properties are discounted compared to properties closer to town. It boils down
to CONVENIENCE. Every time the property owner wants to visit their land, they have to DRIVE there. If it's 15 minutes from town,
that's easy — a quick trip. If it's 2 hours from town on rough dirt roads, that is a significant commitment of time and effort. Every single
visit.
The user described the experience vividly: driving deep into a rural area on a long dirt road that goes on and on, getting your car
dirty, bouncing through ruts and rough spots. That experience is NOT convenient and it is NOT pleasant for most people. Some
people love it — they enjoy the journey to their remote property as part of the experience. But the MAJORITY of buyers prefer easier
access to their property. And as the user said: 'The majority makes the rules, and that's how the market is made.'
This convenience factor is the same underlying principle that drives proximity premiums near cities and towns (covered in Part 5). It's
all on a spectrum: downtown city = maximum convenience, maximum price. City suburbs = high convenience, high price. Rural
recreational ring (10-15 min from city edge) = moderate convenience, moderate price. Standard rural (30-60 min from town) = lower
convenience, lower price. Super rural (1-3+ hours from anything) = minimal convenience, deeply discounted price. The rural discount
is just the far end of the convenience spectrum.
FLAGGING SUPER RURAL PROPERTIES IN YOUR DELIVERABLE
When you identify a property as super rural, flag it prominently in your deliverable. The user wants to know about this so they can
factor it into their offer strategy and their conversation with the seller. Here is how to flag it:
In Extra Notes (Section 6): Use the hazard emoji and a specific note: '■■ VERY RURAL — property is approximately [X] miles / [X]
minutes from the nearest town of [town name]. Surrounding area shows minimal residential development. Large timber/agricultural
tracts dominate the area. Limited buyer pool for smaller acreage at this level of isolation. Road access and condition should be
verified with seller.'
In Questions to Ask Seller (Section 9): Add: 'Verify road access and condition — is the access road maintained year-round? Is it
passable in all weather? How far is the nearest paved road?' This lets the user get ground-level intelligence about the access
situation that you cannot determine from satellite.

In the valuation itself: Note that the PPA reflects a rural discount. If your comps are from less rural areas, explain the adjustment:
'Comps from the [town name] area trade at ~$X,XXX/ac. Subject is significantly more isolated (~X miles/minutes further from town)
— applied X% rural isolation discount to reflect limited demand at this level of remoteness.'
Distance to nearest town: The user said you can note the approximate distance to the nearest town if it doesn't take too much time.
A quick check on the map — zoom out, identify the nearest town, estimate driving distance — takes 5-10 seconds and provides
useful context. Don't estimate distance to nearest house — the user noted that houses can be hidden under tree canopy and the
estimate wouldn't be accurate.
SUPER RURAL — DOES NOT MEAN SKIP THE PROPERTY
An important clarification: identifying a property as super rural does NOT mean the property is a bad deal or should be skipped. It
means the property needs to be PRICED CORRECTLY for its isolation level. The DewClaw business model works in any market —
including super rural markets — as long as the property is acquired at the right price.
If a super rural property has an as-is value of $50,000 (after the rural discount), and the user can acquire it at 50% ($25,000), that is
still a profitable flip. The property will sell — there are buyers for rural recreational land at every price point. It might take a bit longer
to find the right buyer (hunting enthusiast, cabin builder, recreational landowner), and the buyer pool is smaller, but with aggressive
pricing and elite drone marketing, it will sell within the target timeframe.
The user's business model is specifically designed for rural recreational land. Super rural properties are not outside the business
model — they are a subset of it. The key is accurate pricing that reflects the isolation, not avoiding these properties altogether.

QUICK REFERENCE — RURAL PROPERTY EVALUATION
Normal rural (15-30 min from small town): Standard DewClaw territory. No special rural discount needed beyond what comps
already reflect.
Moderately rural (30-60 min from nearest town): Slight discount, 10-15% if comps are from closer-to-town areas. Note the
distance.
Very rural (1-2 hours from nearest town): 20-30% discount. Flag with hazard emoji. Limited buyer pool. Road access verification
needed.
Super rural (2-3+ hours from anything): 30-50% discount. Heavy flag. Very limited buyer pool. Comp data likely sparse. Road
access likely poor and unmaintained.
Small acreage (<20ac) in super rural area: Especially problematic — mismatch between lot size and what rural buyers want (big
properties). Discount toward higher end (40-50%). Limited subdivision potential because no demand for small lots this far out.
Large acreage (50-100+ ac) in super rural area: Less discount (15-25%) because large acreage matches what rural/hunting
buyers actually want. Potential hunting property play.
Super rural + wetlands/timber + adjacent to public land: Hunting property opportunity. Hunters WANT isolation and wetlands
attract wildlife. Price as hunting property, not residential.
Super rural + no Street View available: Strong indicator of extreme isolation. Note in deliverable. Rely on satellite only for visual
assessment.
Comps that are also super rural: Use them normally — they already reflect the rural market. Don't double-discount.
Comps from less rural areas: Apply rural isolation discount based on how much more remote your subject is.
Always flag for road access verification: In super rural areas, road condition is uncertain. Note in Section 9 for seller to verify.
Comp assuming normal access — user adjusts further after talking to seller.
Note distance to nearest town: Quick check, useful context. Don't estimate distance to nearest house (hidden under canopy, not
accurate).
Frequency: Approximately 1 out of every 40-50 properties will be super rural. Uncommon but important to handle correctly when
encountered.
