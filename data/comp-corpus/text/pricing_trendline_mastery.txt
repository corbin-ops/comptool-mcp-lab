1. The PPA Trendline: Core Framework
The PPA (Price Per Acre) Trendline is the foundational analytical tool for every property valuation at
DewClaw. It is not optional. It is not something that gets built only on complex deals. It is built mentally on
EVERY property evaluation, every time, without exception. The trendline is what transforms comping from
guesswork into a defensible, math-backed process.
1.1 — The Shape of the Curve
The PPA trendline is NOT linear. It follows an exponential curve on the low-acreage end that gradually
flattens into a roughly linear shape on the high-acreage end. This is the universal shape of rural land
markets:
Small lots (1-5 acres): PPA is HIGH and rises STEEPLY as acreage decreases. A 2-acre lot might sell at
$15,000/ac while a 5-acre lot sells at $8,000/ac. The change is dramatic — small acreage differences
cause huge PPA swings. This is the volatile, exponential, hard-to-predict part of the curve.
Medium lots (5-20 acres): PPA is moderate and declining more gradually. The curve is still falling but the
rate of descent is slowing. This is the transition zone where the exponential shape bends toward linear.
Large parcels (20+ acres): PPA is LOW and relatively STABLE. A 20-acre parcel might be $3,500/ac
and a 40-acre might be $2,800/ac. The curve has flattened out. PPA is not jumping wildly anymore. This is
the predictable, trustworthy part of the curve.
1.2 — Why This Shape Matters for Pricing
Understanding the curve shape is not academic — it directly controls how you weight comps and where
you place confidence in your valuation:
The LOW END (small lots) is exponential and volatile. Small acreage differences cause huge PPA swings.
Comps in this zone are harder to trust and riskier to lean on. That is why the comp range is set at only
50% below the subject acreage — to limit exposure to the steep, unpredictable part of the curve.
The HIGH END (large parcels) is flat and stable. PPA does not change dramatically between a 30-acre
and a 50-acre parcel. Comps in this zone are trustworthy and predictable. That is why the comp range
extends to 100% above the subject acreage (or even 200-300% if needed) — the flat part of the curve can
be trusted at wider ranges.
Always weight toward the higher-acreage comps (lower PPA) for a conservative valuation. The
flat part of the trendline is where the data is reliable. The steep part is where mistakes get
expensive.
1.3 — The Trendline Is the Story
At its core, the trendline is the market itself, visualized. When you pull comps at 2 acres, 5 acres, 10 acres,
20 acres, and 50 acres, you are mapping out how the market values land across the acreage spectrum.
Each comp is a data point. Connected together, they tell a story about what buyers in this county are
willing to pay for different sized parcels.

[PAGE 4]
The subject property lives somewhere on this curve. The trendline tells you where. If the subject is at 10
acres and the curve shows $6,000/ac at 5 acres and $3,500/ac at 15 acres, the 10-acre point is
somewhere in between — shaped by whether the curve is steep or flat in that range.
You are not guessing. You are constructing a market. If the data says 5 acres goes for X and 15 acres
goes for Y, then 10 acres lives between those two points. A realtor or seller looking at the same comps
would reach a similar conclusion. The trendline makes the valuation defensible.
Pricing is always an estimate — it is never going to be exactly right. But if the math is sound and
the odds are stacked, the number will be fair and defensible. Then discount for the list price and
move on.
1.4 — Curve Shape Varies by Market
Not all counties have the same curve shape. The steepness and spread vary based on the market:
Metro-adjacent counties / lake counties: Tend to have STEEPER curves. Big spread between small-lot
PPA and large-parcel PPA. Strong demand for small lots drives up the low-acreage PPA.
Deep rural counties: Tend to have FLATTER curves. Less spread between small and large lot PPA.
Less demand for small lots means the curve does not spike as dramatically on the low end.
But these are generalizations. Some rural counties have steep curves. The comps themselves tell the
truth — let the data speak rather than assuming. The first time in any county, pull the widest possible
comp range to see the curve shape. That tells you what kind of market you are in.

2. Building the Trendline in Practice
2.1 — Pull Data Points Across the Spectrum
On every evaluation, pull comps at multiple acreage points to see the full curve. You want to know what
2-acre lots sell for, what 5-acre lots sell for, what 10 sells for, what 20 sells for. Each is a data point. The
more data points, the clearer the curve, the more trustworthy the valuation.
Start with AUTO range on Land Insights, then expand: 50% below / 100% above. If needed, go to
50%/200% or 100%/300%. More comps is always better. Evaluate ALL comps and build the full PPA
trendline across the acreage spectrum, then slot the subject into the curve.
2.2 — Filling Gaps in the Trendline
You will not always get clean comps at every acreage point. There will be gaps. When there is a gap —
say you have solid comps at 5 acres and 15 acres but nothing at 10 — use the curve shape itself to guide
where the subject falls:
If the curve between those two points looks LINEAR in this market, interpolation is straightforward. A
10-acre property falls proportionally between the 5-acre and 15-acre PPAs.
If the curve is STEEP between those two points (exponential zone), the 10-acre PPA will be closer to the
15-acre PPA than the 5-acre PPA — because the exponential curve drops fast on the small-lot side.
Weight toward the higher-acreage comp (15 acres) since that is the conservative, more stable end.
Even imperfect comps between the gap points help. A 7-acre comp that is not a great match for your
10-acre subject still gives you a ceiling or floor to narrow the range. Any data point between two anchor
points tightens the estimate.
Build SEPARATE mental curves per comp type when relevant: wooded vs. cleared, waterfront vs.
non-waterfront. Each land type has its own PPA behavior. Do not mix a waterfront 5-acre comp with a
landlocked wooded 5-acre comp.
2.3 — Handling Outliers
Outliers above and below the trendline are common. They are often off-market sales, friend deals, bidding
wars, or distressed sales. When you see a comp that does not fit the trendline:
Note it, but do not let it pull the valuation.
Check listing history — was there a bidding war? Price drops? Sold same day?
Check if it was off-market (Sold-Public Records) — if so, reject it per standing rules.
The trendline built from multiple comps is the truth. One outlier is an anecdote. Statistics are proven
over larger sample sizes — the more data points, the better the average, the more trustworthy the
number.
The trendline gets progressively tighter as you identify close ceilings and floors near the subject acreage.
You are whittling the range down from broad to narrow, not chasing individual outlier sales.

[PAGE 6]
2.4 — The Perspective Check: Think in Total Dollars
When working on the steep part of the curve (small lots), the PPA numbers look intimidating. A 2-acre lot
might show $12,000/ac versus $14,000/ac, which seems like a big range. But in total dollars, that is only
$24,000 vs $28,000 — a $4,000 difference. Not catastrophic.
Meanwhile, on the flat part of the curve (large parcels), the PPA looks calm. A 100-acre property at
$500/ac versus $750/ac seems like a small PPA difference. But in total dollars, that is $50,000 vs $75,000
— a $25,000 swing. Much more impactful.
Do not freak out about high PPA numbers on small lots. Do not get complacent about low PPA
numbers on large parcels. Always convert to total dollars to calibrate the actual financial impact
of your pricing decisions. Keep your head on straight.

3. The Comp Investigation Process
This is the exact order of operations for evaluating every single comp. This process is what makes the
difference between a 200% accurate valuation and a 500% accurate one. Do not skip steps. Do not get
lazy. Every step adds a piece of the puzzle that makes the final picture clearer.
Step 1 — Legitimacy Check
Is this a real, usable comp? Check immediately:
Closed (MLS) = gold standard. Use it.
Pending = usable but discount 5-10% from list price (hot market = 5%, slow = 10%). Always flag
which comps are pending and that you applied the discount.
Off-market / Sold-Public Records = REJECT. Do not use. Move on immediately.
Never use AI Comp, Zestimate, or any platform estimate.
Never cross county lines for comps.
If the comp fails this check, do not spend another second on it. Move to the next one.
Step 2 — Listing Photos (ALWAYS First)
Go through EVERY listing photo. Even when there are 20 photos of trees. Even when they all look the
same. Here is what you are looking for:
Cleared areas / building sites — where could someone build? How cleared is it? Maintained or
overgrown?
Road access — is there a visible driveway, entryway, or road? Paved, gravel, or dirt?
Culverts — are there culvert crossings visible? This indicates creek crossings and infrastructure.
Fencing — is the property fenced? What kind? This adds value.
Structures — any buildings, sheds, mobile homes? Metal or wood? Condition?
Water features — pond, creek, lake frontage? Size and quality of shoreline?
Wetland indicators — standing water, boggy areas, marsh grass?
Slope — can you see steep terrain, hillsides, ravines?
Timber — is the timber managed (uniform rows, plantation) or natural? Clear-cut stumps?
Rocky soil — visible rock outcroppings that could affect perc tests and building?
Aerial photos — these are gold. They show the parcel shape, surrounding area, how the property sits
relative to neighbors and roads. Always look carefully at aerials.
Community amenity photos — swimming pools, clubhouses, HOA amenities. These are NOT on the
actual parcel. Do not assign extra value for them. They just indicate the area is probably nicer than
average. It boils down to proximity, which you are already evaluating.
Even trees can be useful — you can sometimes identify timber type, density, and whether the land has
been managed or clear-cut. Every photo is a potential piece of the puzzle.

[PAGE 8]
Step 3 — Listing Description (Second)
Read the full listing description AFTER you have already seen the photos. Now you can put two and two
together — the description may mention something you did not catch visually, or confirm what you saw in
the photos. Together they give a much more complete picture than either one alone.
Look for specific details: acreage breakdown (wooded vs. cleared), road type, utilities available, perc test
results, survey information, zoning, restrictions, HOA fees, easements, and any mention of slope,
wetlands, or flood zones. Also look for red flags in the language (covered in detail in Section 5).
Do NOT take positive marketing claims at face value. 'Great building site,' 'cleared homesite,'
'perfect for your dream home' — all need verification through the photos, map view, or Land
Insights APN. Marketing language is designed to sell. Data tells the truth.
Step 4 — Map View on Redfin/Zillow (Third)
Check the map view to see the parcel boundaries and surrounding area. This gives you proximity context,
neighboring parcel sizes, road network, and distance to towns or features.
CRITICAL WARNING: The parcel lines on Redfin and Zillow are WRONG approximately 40-50% of
the time. Since you have already seen the listing photos, you can cross-reference and tell if the
map boundaries make sense. If the photos showed a cleared field but the map shows the parcel
sitting entirely in timber, something is off with the map. Do not trust the map blindly.
Step 5 — Land Insights APN Check (When Needed)
This is the bulletproof, foolproof verification step. Open a separate Land Insights tab and pull the comp's
APN. This gives you 100% accurate parcel boundaries, soil data, wetland/flood zone overlays, slope
contours, road classification, and proximity to the subject — all on the most reliable data source available.
When to use Step 5: When something does not add up between the photos, description, and map. When
the comp is a critical anchor for the valuation. When you see an outlier (high or low) and need to
understand why. When you want to verify road access, slope, or wetland status.
On Land Insights, you can also access the aerial view and sometimes Street View, which gives a much
better representation of the property than Redfin/Zillow maps. If the APN does not aggregate in Land
Insights, move on — it happens, and you cannot force it.
Combined with the listing data (photos + description + sale price), the Land Insights APN check
gives you the most complete, most accurate comp evaluation possible. This is what makes
comps 200-500% more accurate.
Timing Target
For a standard comp where photos and description align: Steps 1-3 take 30-45 seconds. Step 4 adds
15-20 seconds. Total: under 1 minute per comp for the standard process. Step 5 (Land Insights APN)
adds 30-60 seconds but is only used on critical comps or when something does not add up. For 5-8
comps with APN checks on the 2-3 most important ones, the full process fits within 3-4 minutes.

5. Red Flags in Listings
5.1 — Perc Test Language (Critical)
Perc test language in listings is one of the most important red flag indicators. A seller with a passing perc
test would ALWAYS include it because it removes uncertainty and adds value. When the perc results are
hidden or vague, assume the worst.
Language in Listing
What It Means
Action
"Perc test passed" or results included
POSITIVE — seller is marketing it because it adds value. No mystery.
Trust it. Factor as a positive for the comp.
"Talk to agent for perc results" or "See details for perc/soil test"
RED FLAG — if it passed, they would just say so. They want to get you on the phone to convert you into a lead.
Assume perc failed. Discount comp or note the risk.
"Perc test expired"
ACTUALLY POSITIVE — it means the test DID pass at some point, just needs renewal.
Leave hope on this one. Mild positive signal.
"Soil test failed" or negative soil language
RED FLAG — affects building, foundations, septic. Dealbreaker for residential buyers.
Significant discount. Note as inferior comp.
No mention of perc at all
MYSTERY — and mystery is bad. Good sellers showcase everything positive. Conspicuous absence = assume the worst.
Be cautious. Do not assume perc will pass.
5.2 — Listed / Delisted / Relisted Pattern
When a property has been listed, delisted, and relisted, the pattern usually means:
The seller was priced too high initially and got frustrated with low offers.
The seller may have had a disagreement with the realtor and temporarily pulled the listing.
The seller may have realized they were overpriced and relisted lower, OR they relisted at the same
unrealistic price.
The seller may be desperate and trying again.
Action: Check the price history. Did they relist lower? (Confirms overpricing.) Same price? (Still
unrealistic.) Note the pattern and factor it into how you weight this comp — it tells you something about the
market's willingness to pay at that price level.
5.3 — The Mystery Principle
Mystery is BAD for sellers. Good sellers showcase everything positive about their property — perc test
results, survey, utilities, road access, cleared building sites. When something is conspicuously absent
from a listing, there is usually a reason. The seller is hiding a negative.
If a listing is vague about road access — the road is probably bad. If it does not mention perc —
it probably failed. If there are no aerial photos — the shape or surroundings are probably
unflattering. Assume the worst until proven otherwise.

6. Handling Conflicting Comps
When two solid, legitimate comps — similar acreage, similar features, same county — show significantly
different PPAs, do not just pick one. Investigate both using the full comp investigation process (Section 3).
Usually, comps that appear identical on the surface are NOT truly identical. Something explains the gap —
better road access, a cleared building site, waterfront, slope, a feature visible in photo 14 of 20 that you
missed. The investigation almost always reveals the difference.
If after thorough investigation the comps really do appear identical and the gap is still significant:
Check listing history on both. Which one dropped price? (Desperate seller.) Which one went above
asking? (Motivated buyer.) Which one sold same day? (Someone really wanted it.)
Weight toward whichever comp looks like the most ORGANIC, NORMAL sale — a standard market
transaction where a normal buyer and seller agreed on a fair price without unusual pressure on either
side.
For larger acreage comps (10+ acres), a significant dollar gap (like $30k) between two supposedly
identical comps means something is definitely different. Keep digging.
The tiebreaker is always: which sale looks the most organic? Not recency, not proximity — but
which transaction looks like the most normal, arms-length market sale.
Non-Disclosure States
Some states do not disclose how much properties actually sold for — including parts of Texas, Mississippi,
and Oklahoma. In these states, comping is harder because you cannot see sold prices. Active and
pending listings become more important data points out of necessity. Be aware of which states are
non-disclosure when comping and adjust expectations for data availability.

7. Making Your Own Market
When the subject property is the best or worst thing in the area with nothing directly comparable, you are
not stuck. You work with what you have in the same market and build comparison layers.
7.1 — Subject Is the WORST Property in the Area
Find the bottom-grade comps in the area — the ones with slope, bad road access, issues. Build a mini
trendline from those. Then compare: is the subject worse than those comps, the same, or slightly better? If
the subject is still the worst, you have close comps to anchor from and you discount down from there. You
are not guessing blind — you are saying 'the worst comps sold at X, mine is a bit worse, so I price below
X.'
7.2 — Subject Is the BEST Property in the Area
Same process in reverse. Find the highest-quality comps — waterfront, cleared, great access. Build the
ceiling from those. Then ask: is the subject better? By how much? Apply a reasonable increase above the
best comps. Be conservative — do not go crazy. The premium should be defensible.
Waterfront properties are a common example of this. Waterfront parcels often set their own market
because they are rare. Price fairly and they sell quickly.
7.3 — The Middle Ground Always Helps
Even when pricing the best or worst property, still pull the mediocre/normal comps to establish the
baseline. This gives the full spectrum: bottom-grade comps set the floor, normal comps set the middle,
top-grade comps set the ceiling. The subject falls somewhere on this spectrum. The trendline, even an
imperfect one, is always better than blind guessing.
You are never truly without comps. Even when there is nothing identical, there are always
properties that are somewhat better and somewhat worse. Those create the range. Then whittle
it down based on how the subject compares feature by feature.

8. Special Comp Scenarios
8.1 — Active Listings
Active listings get weight in two specific scenarios:
Setting list price: Active listings are a ceiling for the list price. You do not want to compete with active
listings — you want to look priced well below them so buyers come to you first. If there are active listings
at $X/ac, your list price should be clearly below that.
Non-disclosure states: When sold prices are not publicly available, active and pending listings may be
all you have. In these states, they become more important data points by necessity.
In normal markets with plenty of closed comps, active listings are reference points, not primary comps.
The market has not validated their price yet.
8.2 — Pending Sales
Always discount pending sales by 5% from the listed price to be conservative. This is the standard — hot
market or slow market, always apply 5% for safety.
Multiple pending sales clustered at a similar price point carry real weight — they indicate the market is
accepting that price range. Three pendings at $6,000/ac is a stronger signal than one closed at $5,500/ac.
But still discount each by 5%.
Always flag which comps are pending and that the 5% discount was applied, so the owner knows exactly
what adjustments were made.
8.3 — Old Comps (1-2+ Years)
Rural land markets do not shift dramatically year to year. Comps from a couple years ago are still usable.
If adjusting for time, add approximately 2-5% annual appreciation — but this is not a precise science. Mix
older comps in with modern ones and see how much the market actually changed.
If you notice a meaningful shift between old and new comps, note it in extra notes for the owner. But do
not overthink the time adjustment — it is not a major factor in rural land.
8.4 — Farmland Comps
Farmland (crop rows, irrigation, tilled soil) is a yielding asset and is worth MORE than standard residential
rural land. It occupies a different tier from pasture, cleared, and wooded land.
If the subject is farmland in an area with both farmland and residential, comp it against other farmland first.
If no farmland comps exist, use residential as the trendline baseline and understand the subject is worth a
good amount more. Use gut feeling on the premium.
Since DewClaw sells as residential (not farmland), the pricing still needs to make sense for the buyer —
but do not undervalue farmland by only using residential comps.

If genuinely unsure about farmland valuation, flag with the red stop sign emoji and recommend getting a

9. The Pricing Philosophy
9.1 — Don't Overthink It
Pricing is all an estimate. It is never going to be exactly right. But if the math is sound, the comps are
investigated, and the trendline is built from stacked odds (multiple data points, multiple verification steps,
feature-by-feature comparison), the number will be fair and defensible.
At the end of the day, it really is just math. If you are comping the exact same type of property, using the
same data sources, building the same trendline — a realtor or appraiser looking at the same comps would
reach a similar conclusion. You are not doing rocket science. You are constructing a market from data and
placing your subject within it.
9.2 — Stack All the Odds
No single tool gives the answer alone. The trendline is one odd. The comp investigation is another. The
feature comparison is another. The area demand check is another. Each one narrows the range and
increases confidence. Together, they get close enough to make money.
The more comps you have, the better the average, the more trustworthy the number. One comp is an
anecdote. Ten comps is a trend. Statistics are proven over larger sample sizes — that is exactly what the
trendline represents. Every additional data point makes the story clearer.
9.3 — DOM Shaving: Keep It Simple
Days on market is a signal, not a formula. The overall approach is already conservative, and there is a
discount applied at the end. DOM is used as a directional lean:
If comps sold fast (under 30 days): feel confident pricing closer to market. Strong demand at fair price.
If something sat for a while (150+ days): weight toward the lower end to be conservative. The market was
not fully accepting that price.
Do not apply precise mathematical discounts per DOM bucket in practice. The final 5% (approximate)
discount on top of market value is the real safety net. DOM informs which direction you lean — it does not
dictate a specific percentage adjustment.
9.4 — The Gut Check Layer
After the math, after the trendline, after the comp investigation — there is a gut check. Based on the
market, what do you think people would actually pay for this property, knowing you want to sell quickly? Is
there a feature that makes this property stand out? Is there an active listing with similar features sitting on
the market at a certain price?
Active listings with nice features and okay sales ratios become ceilings. Your list price needs to be below
them to actually move. If their listing is struggling, you need to be WELL below.

[PAGE 16]
The 5% discount is not the holy grail. On average, that is the standard. But depending on how nice the
property is, how strong the demand is, and what the competitive landscape looks like, it could be slightly
more or less. The goal is: price it to create urgency without being desperate.

10. The Two-Number Deliverable
Every valuation delivers TWO specific numbers:
1. Actual Market Value: The best estimate of true market value based on comps, trendline position, and
property characteristics. This is the clean number — what the data says the property is worth.
2. List Price (~5% Discount): Market value discounted by approximately 5% to provide a margin of error
and ensure the property is priced to move. This is the price the property would be listed at for sale.
The owner uses market value to avoid missing deals by a few thousand dollars (sometimes you negotiate
close to market value). The list price sets the acquisition target at approximately 50% of list. Both numbers
are needed for every deal.
Always give SPECIFIC prices, not just ranges. A range is acceptable when uncertain, but always
include a specific recommended value. The owner needs concrete numbers to make offers.

11. The Final Sanity Check
Before every valuation goes out the door, run this mental frame:
Put yourself in the shoes of a knowledgeable realtor who has buyers actively looking for land. Would this
property, at this price, make that realtor pick up the phone and call their buyer saying: 'You need to grab
this before someone else does'?
That is the sweet spot. The price should create urgency without being desperate. A realtor should look at
the listing and think: 'This is priced right. My buyer would jump on this.' If the price would not generate that
reaction, it is either too high (will not sell fast enough) or could go a bit higher (leaving money on the table).
The balance: do not discount too much, do not discount too little. Just right. The 5% discount
from market value is the starting framework, but the gut check is always: 'Does this price make a
realtor excited to call their buyer?'
If the answer is yes — submit the valuation with confidence.

13. Common Mistakes & How to Avoid Them
Mistake 1: Lazy Comp Investigation
The Problem: Seeing a comp that looks similar on paper — similar acreage, same county — and
weighting it heavily without actually investigating what the property looks like. Skipping listing photos,
skipping map view, not checking Land Insights APN.
The Consequence: Mispricing. Treating a nicer comp as equivalent to the subject (overpricing), or missing
a feature that makes the subject better (underpricing). A comp might have a cleared building site
accessible by a dirt road visible in photo 14 of 20 that changes the entire valuation — but you skipped the
photos.
The Fix: Follow the full 5-step comp investigation process on every comp. No shortcuts. Photos
first, description second, map third, Land Insights APN on critical comps.
Mistake 2: Not Building the Trendline
The Problem: Comping only at the subject's acreage without pulling wider data points. This gives you a
cluster of comps but no context for where they sit on the market curve.
The Consequence: You cannot tell if the subject is priced above or below the trendline. You cannot
identify outliers. You cannot see the curve shape (steep vs. flat). You are flying blind.
The Fix: Pull comps at multiple acreage points on every eval: 2ac, 5ac, 10ac, 20ac+. Build the
mental trendline. See where the subject slots in. This takes 30 extra seconds and transforms the
valuation.
Mistake 3: Weighting Comps Too Heavily on One Side
The Problem: Finding one or two comps that seem like great matches and anchoring the entire valuation
to them without considering the broader trendline or investigating whether those comps are truly
comparable.
The Consequence: If the anchor comp was an outlier — overpay, friend deal, distressed sale — the entire
valuation is skewed.
The Fix: Never treat one comp as the Holy Grail. Use it as an anchor to build the story around,
but validate it against the broader trendline. More data points = more trustworthy average.
Mistake 4: Freaking Out About High PPA on Small Lots
The Problem: Seeing $12,000/ac versus $14,000/ac on a 2-acre lot and treating it as a crisis.
The Reality: That is only a $4,000 total price difference ($24k vs $28k). Meanwhile, being off by $250/ac
on a 100-acre parcel is a $25,000 swing. Think in total dollars, not PPA.

[PAGE 21]
The Fix: Always convert PPA to total dollars to calibrate the actual impact. Keep your head on
straight. The steep part of the curve looks scary but the dollar impact is smaller than the flat
part.
Mistake 5: Not Checking What the Subject Actually Looks Like
The Problem: Comping the subject based on acreage and location without verifying its actual features —
missing a dirt road that leads to a cleared spot, not realizing there are two access points, overlooking a
water feature visible on satellite.
The Fix: Street View is MANDATORY on every subject property. Check satellite imagery. Toggle
Land Insights layers. Look for entryways, driveways, cleared spots, mobile homes, sheds,
structures, wet areas, power lines, fencing. Note everything in Extra Notes.
