DEWCLAW LAND HOLDINGS
 LAND TYPE VISUAL IDENTIFICATION REFERENCE
 Detailed Visual Descriptions for Satellite & Ground-Level Land Type Assessment
 Version 1.0 — April 2026
This document provides granular visual descriptions of every land type encountered during property evaluation
— what each looks like from satellite view, from Google Maps aerial, from Street View, and across different
map filter layers. The goal is to eliminate guesswork and ensure consistent, accurate land type identification on
every comp.

[PAGE 2]
1. MANAGED TIMBERLAND (Pine Plantation / Timber
Investment)
[!] Flag with hazard symbol if suspected. Add ~$200–$500/ac premium.
What It Is
Land that has been intentionally planted and managed as a timber crop — a yielding asset, similar in concept to
farmland. Trees are planted in organized rows (most commonly pine species in the Southeast US), grown to
maturity over 15–30 years, then harvested. This is a farming operation for trees. Owners who manage timber
usually know their land's value.
Satellite View — What You Will See
The Signature Pattern: Uniform Rows
The single most distinctive feature of managed timberland from satellite is the ROW PATTERN. Trees are
planted in straight, parallel, evenly spaced rows — like a giant grid or corduroy fabric draped over the terrain.
This is immediately distinguishable from natural/wild forest, which has a random, irregular canopy pattern.
Specific visual cues from satellite:
 Parallel lines visible in the canopy — even at moderate zoom (Google Maps zoom 17–18), you can
often see the linear structure of the tree rows. The gaps between rows create faint darker lines
(shadow/ground visible between rows). At higher zoom, individual trees in rows become clearly visible.
 Uniform canopy color — because all trees are the same species and roughly the same age, the canopy
is a very consistent shade of green. No mixed species variation. Natural forest has a patchwork of different
greens, some lighter deciduous, some darker evergreen. Managed timber is monotone — one shade.
 Uniform canopy texture — all tree crowns are roughly the same size, creating an even, regular texture.
Natural forest has big canopies next to small ones, gaps, varied heights. Managed timber looks 'smooth'
and consistent from above.
 Sharp, straight boundaries — managed timber blocks typically have clean, straight edges where the
plantation meets other land types. Natural forest transitions gradually. Managed timber stops abruptly in a
straight line.
 Rectangular or geometric blocks — plantations are usually planted in rectangular blocks or sections,
following property lines or management boundaries. You'll often see a neat rectangle of uniform green next
to a different land type.
 Age variation between blocks — if the owner manages multiple planting cycles, you may see adjacent
blocks of different heights/densities. One block is mature (tall, full canopy), another is younger (shorter,
more ground visible between rows). This patchwork of different-age plantation blocks is a dead giveaway
of active timber management.
What It Does NOT Look Like
Random, scattered trees with varied species. Winding tree lines along creeks. Clumps and gaps. Mixed
deciduous and evergreen. Irregular canopy heights. These are all indicators of natural woodland, NOT
managed timber.
Street View / Ground Level — What You Will See

[PAGE 3]
 Trees in obvious rows — standing at ground level, you can look down the rows like looking down the
aisle of a grocery store. Trees recede in a straight line to the horizon.
 Clean understory — managed plantations are often kept relatively clear underneath. Not a lot of brush,
undergrowth, or debris between the rows (though some older or less-managed ones may have understory
growth).
 All the same species — every tree looks the same. Usually loblolly pine, slash pine, or another
commercial species. All roughly the same height and diameter.
 Firebreaks/access roads — cleared strips between blocks for fire management and harvesting access.
These show as light-colored paths/strips.
Map Layer Toggle Behavior
Managed timber looks the same across all map layers because it is a permanent land use. The rows will be
visible in older AND newer imagery (unless recently harvested — see Clear-Cut section). If one layer shows
rows and another shows bare ground, that is a CLEAR-CUT, not standing timber.
KEY DISTINCTION: Managed Timber vs. Just Wooded Land
Most wooded land is just 'wooded land' — trees grew naturally, no rows, no management, no timber premium.
Only flag as managed timber if you see the row pattern, uniform species, straight boundaries, and/or evidence
of active management. When in doubt, note it: 'Possible managed timber — verify with seller.' Do NOT default
to calling all wooded land 'timber.'

[PAGE 4]
2. CLEAR-CUT LAND (Recently Logged / Harvested Timber)
[!] Flag with hazard symbol. Discount 5–30% depending on severity.
What It Is
Land where trees have been recently removed (harvested or cleared). This is a TEMPORARY state — the
worst of both worlds. It is not wooded (no privacy, no recreation value, no timber value). It is not cleared
(stumps, debris, slash piles, logging ruts make it unusable). It is ugly, messy, and devalues the property until it
either regrows or is fully cleaned up.
Satellite View — What You Will See
The Signature: Stark Color/Texture Contrast Against Surrounding Forest
Clear-cut areas stand out dramatically from satellite. They appear as brown, beige, tan, or light-colored
PATCHES carved out of surrounding dark green forest. The contrast is unmistakable — like a scar or bald spot
in the landscape.
Specific visual cues from satellite:
 Brown/beige/tan patches — exposed soil, stumps, and dead vegetation create a light-colored area that
starkly contrasts with the dark green of intact forest. Fresh clear-cuts are the most obviously brown. Older
ones (1–3 years) start to green up with scrubby regrowth but are still clearly different from mature forest.
 Irregular or rectangular shapes — logging operations typically follow property lines or management
boundaries, creating rectangular or somewhat geometric cleared patches. Some are more irregular,
following terrain. Either way, the sharp boundary between 'trees' and 'no trees' is the key identifier.
 Visible logging roads — thin, light-colored lines running through or leading into the cleared area. These
are the roads the logging trucks used to haul timber out. They often branch in a herringbone or feather
pattern through the cut area.
 Texture differences — intact forest has a bumpy, irregular canopy texture. Clear-cut areas look flat,
rough, and messy — ground-level debris, slash piles, stumps. No vertical structure. At medium zoom it
looks like rough, bare earth.
 Slash/debris piles — sometimes visible as small darker clumps scattered across the cleared area.
These are piles of branches, treetops, and other logging waste left behind.
 Erosion/ruts — heavy equipment (skidders, log trucks) creates deep ruts and disturbed soil, especially
on slopes. This shows as irregular lighter/darker patterns on the bare ground.
THE CRITICAL MAP FILTER TEST
This is the #1 method to catch clear-cuts: TOGGLE ALL THREE MAP FILTERS QUICKLY. If one filter (older
satellite imagery) shows full tree canopy and another filter (newer imagery) shows bare/brown ground — that is
a clear-cut. The trees were there and now they are gone. This is definitive. The reverse can also happen:
newest imagery shows trees, but an intermediate layer shows the clear-cut (already regrowing). Always toggle
all three layers.
Stages of Clear-Cut (What Each Looks Like)
Fresh Clear-Cut (0–6 months)

[PAGE 5]
Bare brown/tan soil, stumps clearly visible at high zoom, logging roads and ruts, slash piles, zero green
regrowth. Looks like a bomb went off. Maximum ugliness. Maximum discount (20–30%).
Early Regrowth (6 months–2 years)
Light green patches starting to appear — grasses, weeds, and small seedlings beginning to cover the bare
ground. Still clearly not forest. Stumps may still be visible. From satellite, it looks like a patchy mix of brown and
light green. Still ugly, still a significant discount (15–25%).
Established Regrowth (2–5 years)
Green scrubby vegetation covering most of the ground. Young trees starting to establish but still very short
compared to surrounding mature forest. From satellite, the area is green but a noticeably DIFFERENT
shade/texture than mature forest — lighter, more uniform, lower. Still identifiable as a cut area. Moderate
discount (10–15%).
Maturing Regrowth (5–15 years)
Young forest establishing. Trees are growing but still shorter than surrounding mature trees. From satellite,
starting to blend in but still visible as a slightly different texture/height area. Minimal discount (5–10%).
Street View / Ground Level — What You Will See
 Stumps — cut tree stumps are the definitive ground-level indicator. Stumps everywhere across the
property.
 Debris/slash — branches, treetops, bark, and other waste scattered or piled.
 Disturbed soil — deep ruts from heavy equipment, compacted logging roads, erosion.
 No canopy — sky is open. No shade, no privacy. Very different feel from standing in a forest.
 Skid trails — paths worn by log-dragging equipment, running through the cut area.
Clear-Cut vs. Intentionally Cleared Land — KEY DISTINCTION
CLEARED land is CLEAN — someone spent money to remove stumps, grade the land, seed grass, and make
it usable. It looks maintained, intentional, and ready for use. CLEAR-CUT land is MESSY — stumps left behind,
debris everywhere, ruts, no cleanup. It was logged for the timber value and abandoned. One is an
improvement, the other is a scar.

3. MAINTAINED CLEARED LAND
What It Is
Open land that someone is ACTIVELY MAINTAINING. The grass is cut, the weeds are managed, random trees
and brush are removed, and the overall appearance is clean and presentable. Someone is going out there
regularly and taking care of this property. It is NOT farmed (no crop rows, no agricultural production) and it is
NOT just wild open land. It is intentionally kept in a maintained state.
Satellite View — What You Will See
 Uniform, consistent green — the grass is one consistent color because it is regularly mowed/cut. No tall
patches, no brown dead spots from neglect, no random variation. It looks like a large lawn or a well-kept
field.
 Clean edges — the boundary between the maintained area and any surrounding trees/brush is sharp
and clean. Someone is trimming the edges and keeping the boundary defined. No gradual transition from
grass to weeds to brush to trees — it goes from maintained green directly to tree line.
 No random trees or brush — the open area is truly open. No scattered saplings growing up, no brush
encroaching, no random trees in the middle of the field. If a tree is there, it was left intentionally (shade
tree, ornamental).
 Short, even texture — the grass appears short and uniform from satellite. Compared to pasture (see
below), which has a taller, more varied, rougher texture, maintained cleared land looks smooth and even.
 May have mowing patterns — at higher zoom on recent satellite imagery, you might see faint mowing
lines/stripes, similar to a baseball field. This is definitive proof of active maintenance.
 No crop rows — this is the critical distinction from farmland. Maintained cleared land is green and open
but has NO parallel crop rows, NO irrigation, NO tilled soil patterns. If you see crop rows, that is
FARMLAND, not maintained cleared.
Street View / Ground Level — What You Will See
 Short, cut grass (lawn-like or pasture-mowed height)
 No tall weeds, no overgrown sections
 Clean, presentable appearance — you could walk across it comfortably
 No fencing required (though some may have it)
 No livestock, no feed bins, no water troughs
 No crop rows, no farm equipment
THE SATELLITE TRAP
From satellite, maintained cleared land can look EXACTLY like farmland — both are open, green, flat fields.
The only way to distinguish them is to look for crop rows (farmland) vs. uniform grass (maintained cleared). If
satellite is ambiguous, ALWAYS check Street View or Google Earth to confirm. Getting this wrong can swing a
valuation by thousands of dollars because farmland commands a yielding-asset premium that maintained
cleared land does NOT.
Valuation Impact

[PAGE 7]
Maintained cleared land is desirable — it is ready-to-use, clean, buildable, and requires less site prep than
wooded or pasture land. It is worth MORE than raw timber or pasture because the clearing work is already
done. But it is worth LESS than farmland because it produces no income. Think of it as a premium version of
raw land — improved but not productive.

4. PASTURE LAND (Unmaintained Open Land / Meadow-Like)
What It Is
Open land that is NOT being actively maintained. Think of a meadow — tall grasses (2+ feet), weeds growing
freely, possibly some scattered trees or brush starting to encroach, and an overall unkempt appearance.
Nobody is going out there and cutting, mowing, or managing this land. It is just sitting there doing its thing.
KEY INSIGHT: The Difference is MAINTENANCE
Pasture vs. maintained cleared has NOTHING to do with fencing, livestock, or any other traditional 'pasture'
indicators. The ONLY distinction is: Is someone actively maintaining this land or not? Maintained = maintained
cleared land. Not maintained = pasture. That is it.
Satellite View — What You Will See
 Varied, uneven green — unlike maintained cleared land's uniform green, pasture has multiple shades
and tones. Some areas lighter, some darker, some yellowish-brown. This is because different grasses and
weeds are growing at different rates and heights.
 Taller, rougher texture — from satellite, the grass appears to have a taller, more three-dimensional
texture. Maintained cleared land looks flat and smooth; pasture looks bumpy and varied because the
grasses are 2+ feet tall and bending/swaying.
 Scattered trees/brush encroaching — since nobody is maintaining the land, young trees and brush
may be starting to grow in, especially at the edges. You might see scattered dots (tree crowns) randomly
placed in the open area. This is natural succession — the land is slowly reverting to woodland.
 Less defined edges — the boundary between pasture and surrounding woodland is softer and more
gradual. Brush and young trees blur the line. Compare to maintained cleared land where the edge is crisp.
 Seasonal variation — pasture shows more seasonal change from satellite. In summer it may be green
(tall grasses growing). In fall/winter it may be brown/golden (dead grasses standing). Maintained cleared
land stays more consistently green because it is mowed.
 Possible patches of different vegetation — weed species growing in clumps create visible patches of
different color/texture across the field. Maybe a patch of goldenrod (lighter), a section of fescue (darker),
some thistle (sparse). Not uniform.
Street View / Ground Level — What You Will See
 Tall grasses — 2 feet or more, often waist-high or higher
 Weeds mixed in freely — wildflowers, thistle, goldenrod, etc.
 Unkempt appearance — you would not want to walk through it in shorts
 Possible scattered saplings/small trees starting to grow
 No mowing lines, no evidence of recent maintenance
 If fencing exists, it may be old/broken/neglected
 Overall 'abandoned field' or 'meadow' feel
Valuation Impact
Pasture land is worth LESS than maintained cleared land because a buyer will need to do work to make it
usable — brush hogging, possible stump removal, grading, seeding. It is more desirable than dense timber

[PAGE 9]
(already open, can see the lay of the land) but less desirable than maintained cleared (needs cleanup). Price
accordingly — between timber and maintained cleared.

5. FARMLAND (Actively Farmed — Yielding Asset)
What It Is
Land that is actively being farmed — producing crops and generating income. This is a YIELDING ASSET. The
price of the land PLUS the value of agricultural production is baked into its market value. Farmland commands
a significant premium over all other non-waterfront land types. Farmers usually know what their land is worth
and are harder to get at deep discount.
Satellite View — What You Will See
 Visible crop rows — THE definitive indicator. Parallel lines running across the field where crops are
planted. These are visible from satellite at moderate zoom. The rows create a distinct lined/striped pattern
across the property.
 Tilled soil patterns — especially visible when land is freshly plowed or between growing seasons. Dark,
turned soil in organized patterns. Very different from natural ground color.
 Irrigation patterns — center-pivot irrigation creates distinctive circular crop patterns (green circles in
brown surroundings). Other irrigation shows as wet/dry patterns, water channels, or sprinkler patterns.
 Distinct field boundaries — farm fields have sharp, straight edges where the crop ends and field
margins/hedgerows begin. Multiple fields may be subdivided within a property.
 Seasonal crop stages — depending on time of year and satellite date, you may see bare tilled soil
(spring/fall), green growing crops (summer), or harvested stubble (fall). All are indicators of active farming.
 Farm infrastructure — barns, grain bins, silos, equipment sheds visible nearby. Farm roads/paths for
equipment access between fields.
THE CRITICAL DISTINCTION: Farmland vs. Maintained Cleared
Both can look like open, green, flat land from satellite. The KEY difference: farmland has CROP ROWS —
visible parallel lines in the vegetation. Maintained cleared land has UNIFORM GRASS — no rows, no pattern,
just a consistently mowed green field. If you cannot see crop rows, it is NOT farmland. Check Street View to
confirm if satellite is ambiguous.

[PAGE 11]
6. NATURAL WOODLAND (Just Wooded Land — No Timber
Premium)
What It Is
The default for most rural land. Trees grew naturally — mixed species, mixed ages, random spacing. No rows,
no management, no commercial timber value beyond firewood. This is NOT managed timber. Do NOT apply
timber premium. Comp as normal wooded property.
Satellite View — What You Will See
 Random, irregular canopy — tree crowns of varying sizes, randomly distributed. No rows, no pattern, no
uniformity. Gaps, clumps, varying density.
 Mixed colors — multiple shades of green from different tree species. Some lighter (deciduous), some
darker (evergreen). Seasonal imagery shows a mix of leaf-on and leaf-off.
 Irregular edges — the boundary of wooded areas is natural and gradual, not straight and geometric like
managed timber.
 Understory visible in gaps — lighter green or brown patches where the canopy is thin and
ground/understory shows through.
KEY: Natural woodland is the DEFAULT. Only upgrade to 'managed timber' if you see rows.

DEWCLAW LAND HOLDINGS
 COMP METHODOLOGY & RULES
 Complete Guide to Finding, Evaluating, and Weighting Comparable Sales
 Version 1.0 — April 2026
This document covers every aspect of how DewClaw evaluates comparable sales — from which sources to
trust and reject, to how wide to search, how to read the full story from all available comps, and how to whittle
down to a final price. The core philosophy: read the whole book, not just the middle chapters.

[PAGE 14]
1. COMP SOURCE HIERARCHY — WHAT TO TRUST AND WHAT
TO REJECT
Gold Standard: Closed MLS Sales
Closed MLS sales are the only fully reliable comp source. These are verified arms-length transactions where a
willing buyer and a willing seller agreed on a price through the open market. The price reflects actual market
value because both parties had access to information, representation, and alternatives. Every valuation should
be anchored primarily on closed MLS sales.
Valuable: Pending Sales
Pending sales are real buyers making real commitments — someone actually put money down and signed a
contract. This is very current market activity and highly valuable for understanding where the market IS right
now (not where it was 6 months ago). However, pending sales have not yet closed, so the final price may differ
from the list price. Apply a discount:
 Hot/normal market: Discount 5% from list price
 Slower market: Discount 10% from list price
Use common sense on the discount amount. A pending sale in a market where everything moves fast probably
went close to ask. A pending sale in a slow market probably had negotiation.
NEVER USE: Sold-Public Records
Sold-Public Records are terribly inaccurate representations of market value. These are sales recorded in
county records but NOT through the MLS — meaning they were not open-market, arms-length transactions.
The problem is you have absolutely no idea what happened behind the scenes:
 A neighbor sold to his friend of 30 years at 30% below market value as a favor
 A family member sold to another family member at 50% below market
 A tax sale where the property went for pennies on the dollar
 A partial interest sale (someone sold 50% ownership)
 An estate settlement where speed mattered more than price
None of these represent what the property would sell for on the open market. Using them as comps would
dramatically skew your valuation — usually downward — and kill deals that should have been made. There is
NO exception to this rule, even in sparse markets. If Sold-Public Records are your only data point, you are
better off using active listings, pending sales, and the PPA trendline than trusting a sale that may have been a
friend-to-friend deal at half price.
NEVER USE: Off-Market Sales
Same problem as Sold-Public Records. Off-market sales were never exposed to the open market. You have no
idea if the seller knew the true value, if the buyer got a sweetheart deal, or if there were personal relationships
influencing the price. A neighbor who finally sells his back 10 acres to the guy next door after 30 years of asking
— that price tells you nothing about what a stranger would pay on the open market. Reject these completely.
NEVER USE: AI Comps, Zestimates, or Platform Estimates
Zillow Zestimates, AI-generated comp values, and any platform's automated valuation are unreliable for vacant
rural land. These algorithms are designed for residential homes with standardized features (bedrooms,

[PAGE 15]
bathrooms, square footage). Vacant land has none of those standardized inputs. The algorithms cannot
account for road type, water features, slope, wetlands, land type, or any of the nuances that actually drive rural
land value. Ignore them completely.
NEVER Cross County Lines
Each county is its own market. Pink lines on Land Insights represent county boundaries — these are your comp
boundaries. Even if a property across the county line is physically closer than one within the county, do NOT
use it. The reason is how buyers actually search: they go to Zillow, Redfin, or Realtor.com and filter by
COUNTY. They never see listings in adjacent counties. Over time, this creates completely separate pricing
patterns. Adjacent counties can have DRASTICALLY different prices for identical land because they are
functionally different markets. No exceptions.

The Philosophy: MORE COMPS = ALWAYS BETTER
The wider you go, the more data points you have to build the full PPA trendline. Even comps that are not
directly comparable to your subject are NOT noise — they are data points that shape the curve in your head. A
2-acre comp and an 80-acre comp may not be 'like-kind' to your 15-acre subject, but they tell you what the
market looks like at the extremes, which helps you understand where your subject sits on the curve.
The only reason to stop widening is if the volume of comps starts CONFUSING you — if you can't keep the
story straight because there are too many variables. But if you can handle the volume and still identify which
comps matter most, go wider. Always.

[PAGE 17]
3. THE BOOK ANALOGY — READ THE WHOLE BOOK
This is the single most important mental model for comping at DewClaw. It governs how you think about every
comp you pull.
The Analogy
Imagine you are trying to understand a chapter in the middle of a book. You COULD just read the two chapters
before and the two chapters after — and you would have a pretty good idea of what is happening. But imagine if
you read the WHOLE book. You would have the absolute best, most detailed, most nuanced understanding
possible.
Comping is the exact same thing. The 'middle chapter' is the 2–3 comps that are most similar to your subject —
same acreage range, same area, same land type. Those are your primary anchors. But the 'whole book' is
EVERY comp across the full acreage spectrum in the county. The small lots at high PPA, the medium lots at
mid PPA, the large lots at low PPA — they ALL contribute to your understanding of the market.
How This Works In Practice
 Step 1: Pull comps across the widest acreage range you can handle
 Step 2: Build a mental PPA trendline across the entire spectrum — from the smallest lots (highest PPA)
to the largest lots (lowest PPA). This is the exponential curve.
 Step 3: Identify where your subject property falls on that curve based on its acreage
 Step 4: Find the 2–5 comps most similar to your subject — these are your primary anchors that get the
most weight
 Step 5: Use ALL the other comps to VALIDATE and SOLIDIFY the story those anchor comps are telling
you. Do the anchors make sense in the context of the full curve? Are they consistent with what smaller lots
and larger lots are doing?
 Step 6: Price the subject based on where it falls on the validated trendline, with appropriate adjustments
for its specific features (road type, water, slope, etc.)
Why This Matters
Without the full trendline, you are guessing. You might have 3 comps that all say $5,000/ac and feel confident
— but those 3 comps might all be slightly overpriced outliers. If you had pulled the full spectrum and seen that
2-acre lots sell at $12,000/ac, 10-acre lots at $6,000/ac, and 40-acre lots at $3,500/ac, you would know that
$5,000/ac for your 15-acre subject is actually on the higher end of the trendline, not the middle. That context
changes everything.
The anchor comps tell you the price. The full trendline tells you whether to BELIEVE the price. Both are
essential.

[PAGE 18]
4. EVALUATING COMPS — LOOK AT ALL OF THEM
Never Pre-Filter
If you have 8, 10, 12, or more comps in range — evaluate ALL of them. Do not pre-filter down to the 'best' 4–5
before looking at the rest. Every comp contributes to the story. The ones that are not directly comparable still
establish the price floor and price ceiling for the market, which helps you understand where the like-kind comps
sit within that range.
NEVER Use One Comp As Your Holy Grail
No matter how perfect a single comp looks — same acreage, same road, same area, same land type —
NEVER anchor your entire valuation to it. There is always a chance it is an outlier. There could be something on
the property that was not in the description. There could be something the photos did not show. There could
have been unusual circumstances (desperate seller, unusually motivated buyer, family connection). You will
never know for certain. This is why you always need MULTIPLE comps to build a RANGE, and then whittle that
range down to your price.
What To Check On Every Comp
 Description: Read the full listing description. Look for features mentioned (water, cleared, structures,
views, utilities) that affect value.
 DOM (Days on Market): Apply the DOM shaving schedule (covered in DOM section). Check if DOM is
total days or days since last price drop.
 Full listing history: Was it listed, delisted, and relisted? Multiple price drops? This is a red flag —
something was wrong (overpriced, had issues, etc.).
 Listing photos (Redfin/Zillow): Capture AS MANY photos as possible. Look for amenities, slope,
cleared areas, plot maps, aerials, structures, layout. Photos may show subdivision/HOA amenities
(clubhouse, pool, tennis courts) that are NOT on the actual parcel — always verify what is on the ACTUAL
property via satellite/APN.
 Road type: What road is the comp on? Paved, gravel, dirt? This affects how you weight it relative to your
subject.
 Land type: What kind of land? Farmland, maintained cleared, pasture, wooded? Compare to your
subject's land type.
 Proximity: How close is the comp to your subject? Closer = more weight.
Comp Quality Awareness
Not all comps with the same label are equal. A 'waterfront' comp that is marshy, wetland-heavy swampland that
happened to touch a lake is NOT the same as a clean, walkable-shoreline waterfront comp. A 'riverfront' comp
in a flood zone with unusable banks is NOT the same as a clean riverfront with gentle slope to the water.
Always assess the QUALITY of a comp's features, not just the labels. If you need to, pull the comp's APN on
Zillow/Redfin and check it in Land Insights for soil survey, wetlands, and flood zone data.

5. WHITTLING DOWN TO A PRICE
The Process
After evaluating all comps, you are not looking for one magic number. You are looking for a RANGE — and
then you narrow that range down until you have a tight window.
 Step 1 — Establish the full range: From all comps evaluated, identify the absolute ceiling (highest PPA
among relevant comps) and the absolute floor (lowest PPA among relevant comps). This is your starting
range.
 Step 2 — Identify the like-kind comps: Which 2–5 comps are most similar to your subject in acreage,
proximity, land type, road type, and features? These get the heaviest weight.
 Step 3 — Refine the range: Based on the like-kind comps, narrow the range. You should be able to get
within approximately $5,000 of where the property should list.
 Step 4 — Go conservative: Within that $5,000 range, price on the LOWER end. Then apply a slight
additional discount for list price (5–10% below market value). This ensures fast sales with elite drone
marketing.
No Property Is Exactly The Same
Accept this reality: no comp will ever be a perfect match to your subject. Every property is different — different
shape, different road, different trees, different access, different topography. That is normal and expected. Your
job is not to find the perfect comp. Your job is to build enough context from enough comps that you can
confidently identify a tight range and price conservatively within it.
You will never land on an exact number — it is impossible. But you can get within a range of ~$5,000, and
within that range you go conservative. That is how you comp, and that is how you ensure every deal has margin
and every listing sells fast.

7. DUPLICATE SUBDIVISION SALES — COUNT AS ONE
When a seller subdivided a larger parcel into multiple lots and all of them sold, only count ONE as a comp. The
reason: they are essentially the same type of property from one parent parcel. Using all 4 of them would
over-weight that single subdivision in your analysis and make it look like you have more independent data
points than you actually do.
Pick the most representative lot — the one closest in acreage and features to your subject. If one lot is clearly
different from the others (bigger, better access, has a pond), it can be used as a separate comp because it
genuinely represents a different value proposition.

[PAGE 22]
8. SPARSE MARKETS — WHEN COMPS ARE LIMITED
In some counties, sold comps are scarce. You might have 2–3 closed sales in the relevant acreage range. In
these situations, you still weight SOLDS more heavily, but actives and pendings become more important for
context.
Views-to-Saves Ratio (Zillow Only)
On Zillow, underneath the property description that the realtor wrote, you can find the views-to-saves ratio. This
tells you whether an active listing is priced competitively:
 ~10 views to 1 save = Good ratio — listing is priced well, generating real interest
 High views, low saves = Overpriced — people are looking but nobody wants to save it at that price
 Low views overall = Listing has visibility issues or is in a very slow market
Compare save ratios across active listings at different price points. The ones with good ratios = realistic market
pricing. The ones with bad ratios = probably overpriced. This helps you calibrate which active listings to weight
more heavily in your analysis.
This data is ONLY available on Zillow. Redfin does not have views-to-saves ratios.
Setting Price When Comps Are Thin
When there are no clear direct comps, YOU set the price. This is where the 'read the whole book' approach is
most critical. Widen the acreage range to the maximum. Pull every comp in the county. Build the full PPA
trendline in your head — see how price per acre changes from the smallest lots to the largest. Then interpolate
where your subject falls on that curve.
Price slightly below where the trendline says the property should be. Couple that with elite drone photos and the
property will sell quickly because it is priced competitively in a market where everything else is either overpriced
or poorly marketed.
Sparse Market Note Format
When comps are limited, note it in the deliverable so the investor understands the context:
NOTE: Limited direct comps in this market. Pricing based on county-wide PPA trendline: 5ac lots selling ~$7,000/ac /
20ac lots selling ~$4,500/ac. Estimated 10-acre value: ~$5,500/ac. Recommend conservative list price to ensure quick
sale.

 WATERFRONT & WATER FEATURES
 Complete Guide to Valuing Water Features — From Seasonal Creeks to Lakefront
 Version 1.0 — April 2026
Water features are one of the most significant value drivers in rural land. But not all water is created equal. This
guide covers the full spectrum — from dry seasonal creeks (zero premium) to prime lakefront (massive
premium) — and everything in between. The core principle: every market is different, every body of water is
different, and you must always verify with your own eyes.

[PAGE 25]
1. ALWAYS VERIFY WATER FEATURES YOURSELF
[!] MANDATORY — NEVER trust filters alone. NEVER assume water exists because a map layer
shows it.
Land Insights may show a surface water overlay, wetlands overlay, or blue shading that LOOKS like a pond,
lake, or creek. But it could be swamp, seasonal ponding, seasonal creek, or wetlands that look like water on a
filter but are actually just soggy ground. Conversely, Land Insights sometimes MISSES real ponds — small
ponds may not show up on any filter.
Mandatory Verification Process:
 Turn OFF all filters — look at raw satellite imagery
 Look at actual satellite imagery with your own eyes — on both Google Maps AND Land Insights
 Check Google Earth — often comes out cleaner than Google Maps for seeing water features
 Use Street View — if the property fronts a road near water, Street View will show you the actual body of
water (or lack thereof). Is the creek dry? Is the river actually flowing? How big is it really?
 If it even SLIGHTLY looks like there might be water — give BOTH a waterfront valuation AND a
non-waterfront valuation. Let the investor verify with the seller.
Trust your own eyes. ALWAYS.

[PAGE 26]

Not all water is 'waterfront.' A seasonal creek is not waterfront. A small stream is not waterfront. Even a normal
river is not always considered waterfront. The term 'waterfront' is reserved for properties with significant bodies
of water — lakes, large ponds, and big rivers that offer real recreational value (boating, fishing, swimming,
scenic views).

3. NO UNIVERSAL WATERFRONT MULTIPLIER
There is no fixed '2x' or '3x' multiplier for waterfront. Every market is different. In Michigan, waterfront might
trade at 100% premium over non-waterfront. In a different area, the same quality waterfront might command
300%+ premium. You cannot apply a formula. You MUST look at the actual comps in the actual market and let
the data tell you.
How To Comp Waterfront
 Step 1 — Establish the floor: What are NON-waterfront properties going for in this area? Pull comps for
similar acreage without water features. This is your baseline PPA.
 Step 2 — Find the ceiling: What are WATERFRONT properties going for? Pull comps with water
features. This is your ceiling PPA.
 Step 3 — Compare features: How does your subject's waterfront compare to the waterfront comps?
More footage? Less? Better shore? Worse? Bigger body of water? Smaller?
 Step 4 — Price between floor and ceiling: Based on your subject's water feature quality relative to the
comps, slot it into the range. If your waterfront is nicer than the comps, price higher. If it is marginal or less
impressive, price lower but still above the non-waterfront floor.
 Step 5 — Discount for fast sale: Apply the standard conservative discount so that the property moves
quickly with elite drone marketing.

[PAGE 28]
4. WATERFRONT FOOTAGE & SHORE QUALITY
How Much Waterfront Footage Matters — A LOT
The amount of waterfront a property has directly impacts its premium. A property with the entire back boundary
along a lake is VASTLY more valuable than one that barely touches the water at one corner. When comparing
your subject to waterfront comps, always note how much frontage each has.
 Entire back/side on water: Maximum premium — extensive access, views, recreation
 Significant frontage (hundreds of feet): Strong premium — room for dock, beach, access
 Moderate frontage (50–100 feet): Good premium — usable waterfront but limited
 Tiny corner touching water: Reduced premium — technically waterfront but barely usable
Shore Quality — Can You Walk Into The Water?
The quality of the shoreline makes an enormous difference in how much premium the water feature commands.
Two properties can both be 'lakefront' but have completely different shoreline value:
 Clean, gentle slope, sandy/firm bottom: Maximum shore quality. You can walk right into the water.
Perfect for swimming, dock, boat launch. This is what buyers dream about.
 Grassy slope to water: Good quality. Usable, scenic, can install dock/steps. Not as premium as sandy
but still very desirable.
 Steep banks: Reduced quality. Can see the water but accessing it requires stairs, retaining walls, or
significant work. Views are still valuable but recreation is limited.
 Marshy/swampy: Lowest quality waterfront. You cannot walk to the water — you will sink in mud. Limited
to visual appeal only. Do NOT comp this the same as clean waterfront. A marshy 'waterfront' comp that
sold low is NOT comparable to a clean waterfront property.
Always compare the shore quality of your subject to the shore quality of your waterfront comps. If your subject
has a clean walkable shore and the best waterfront comp has marshy banks, your subject is SUPERIOR and
should be priced above that comp.

Deeded Water Access (Not Direct Frontage)
Some properties have deeded easements to nearby water — a 50-foot wide path to the lake, for example. A
real, usable deeded lake access (50ft wide) is essentially waterfront for the buyer — they can walk to the water,
put in a kayak, fish. Worth approximately 80% of the direct waterfront premium. Narrower or less usable
easements are worth closer to 50%. The amount of water frontage at the easement point (linear feet) also
matters — more frontage at the access point = more value.

[PAGE 30]
6. MULTIPLE WATER FEATURES — DO THEY STACK?
Yes, multiple water features stack premiums when they are ON the property. A property with a pond AND a
creek running through it is more valuable than a property with just one of those features. More water = more
recreation, more appeal, more buyer interest.
For water features NEAR the property (not on it), the premium stacking is smaller — similar to being near a
single water feature. Being near a lake and a river is nice, but if you don't own any shoreline, the stacking is
modest.
The hierarchy still applies: bigger bodies of water dominate the premium. If a property has both a creek and
lake frontage, the lake frontage drives the premium and the creek is a bonus. If it has a pond and a seasonal
creek, the pond drives the premium and the seasonal creek adds nothing (seasonal = zero value).

[PAGE 31]
7. WATER FEATURE SIZE — THE USABLE ACREAGE PROBLEM
Bigger water = better. That is the general rule. BUT there is a counterpoint: if the pond or lake on the property
takes up a significant portion of the total acreage, you are losing usable land. A 10-acre property with a 4-acre
pond has only 6 acres of usable land. The waterfront premium applies, but you are comping based on usable
acreage plus the water premium, not total acreage at full waterfront rates.
Same logic as flagpole easements: you comp the usable portion of the property and then add the premium for
the water feature. The water takes up space that cannot be built on or used for anything other than recreation.

4. FLAGPOLE LOT CALCULATION — TWO-PART DISCOUNT
Flagpole lots have a narrow access strip (30–50 feet wide) leading from the road back to a larger buildable
area. The access strip adds very little usable value — you cannot build on it, it is just a driveway corridor. The
discount is calculated in two parts:
Part 1: Subtract the Access Strip Acreage
Do NOT comp the property at its total acreage. Identify how much acreage the access strip takes up and
subtract it. Comp only the BUILDABLE portion of the property. Example: a 10-acre flagpole with a 2-acre
access strip = comp as an 8-acre lot.
This is a significant discount by itself. A property that shows as 10 acres on paper is really only 8 usable acres,
and PPA increases as acreage decreases (exponential curve). So by comping 8 acres instead of 10, you are
already pricing it lower per acre AND on fewer acres.
Part 2: Apply Inconvenience Discount (2–5%)
ON TOP of subtracting the strip acreage, apply a small additional discount for the inconvenience of being a
flagpole lot:
 Not that deep (short access strip): 2–3% inconvenience discount
 Deeper access strip: 5% inconvenience discount
Why Both Discounts Are Needed
Subtracting the strip acreage accounts for the lost usable land. The inconvenience discount accounts for the
ADDITIONAL negatives: long driveway cost, farther from road, harder to access, less convenient for
emergency services, less road frontage. These are real costs and inconveniences that a buyer will factor into
their willingness to pay.
Example Calculation
Subject: 10-acre flagpole lot with 1.5-acre access strip, moderate depth.
 Step 1: Comp as 8.5 acres (10 - 1.5 strip)
 Step 2: Market PPA for 8.5 acres in this area = $6,000/ac = $51,000
 Step 3: Apply 3% inconvenience discount (moderate depth) = $51,000 x 0.97 = $49,470
 Market Value: ~$49,500
 Compare to: 10 acres with full road frontage at $5,500/ac = $55,000
 Net discount vs. full-frontage lot: ~$5,500 or ~10%

likely perc too.
 Dry spots within the property: If the property has identifiable dry, elevated areas (visible when you
toggle hazards OFF and look at satellite), those areas may perc even if surrounding areas are wet.
 100% wetlands with no nearby development: Very unlikely to perc. Price as hunting property /
recreation only.

without special measures. Above 20%, you start needing retaining walls, special foundations, grading, and
more expensive site prep. Above 35%, building becomes very expensive and sometimes impractical.
Ravines, Valleys, and Low Spots
Always flag ravines or valleys on the property. These are important for several reasons:
 Creeks typically run through ravines — water flows to the lowest point
 Cannot build in a ravine — too steep on the sides, water issues at the bottom
 Limits buildable area — a 10-acre property with a ravine cutting through the middle may only have 6–7
buildable acres
 Excellent negotiation point — the investor can point out that a significant portion of the property is
unusable due to the ravine, strengthening a lower offer
Always Compare Subject Slope to Comp Slopes
Slope discounts are RELATIVE. If your subject has 25% slope but all the sold comps in the area also have
20–30% slope, the market has already priced in steep terrain. Do not double-discount. Only apply a slope
discount when the subject is significantly steeper than the comps.

[PAGE 42]
3. FLOOD ZONE — DON'T PANIC, LOOK AROUND FIRST
[!] Do NOT panic-discount for flood zone. LOOK AROUND FIRST.
The instinct when you see '100% flood zone' is to slash the value by 50%. This is wrong in many situations.
Before applying any flood zone discount, LOOK AROUND the property:
 Are people building houses in the area? If yes, flood zone is just a cost factor (flood insurance, raised
foundation, engineered plans), NOT a value killer. Many coastal and riverfront communities are 100% flood
zone and everyone builds there anyway.
 Are there homes ON or near the subject property? If neighbors have homes in the same flood zone,
the market has accepted the flood risk and priced it in.
 What type of flood zone? Different designations carry different risk levels. Note the specific zone in your
deliverable if visible.
The Edisto Island Lesson
Mary Seabrook Rd, Charleston Co, SC: 5.64 acres, 79% wetlands, 100% flood zone. Initial valuation was
$75,000 — a 60–70% discount from clean comps. This was WRONG. The entire island is in a flood zone.
Everyone builds there. Houses were visible on and near the property. The flood zone designation was not a
deal-killer — it was just how that market works. Correct approach: recognize flood zone as a cost factor, look at
what comparable properties actually sell for, and price accordingly.
Process
 Toggle hazards OFF to see actual property (structures, land type, water features)
 Toggle ON to check flood/wetland zones
 Always check for structures on or near the property
 Note flood zone designation in deliverable
 Let comps in the SAME flood zone guide pricing — if they sold at normal PPA, yours should too

4. STRUCTURES — COMP AS VACANT, FLAG FOR INVESTOR
The Rule: Always Comp As Vacant Land
A structure on a property could add $2,000 or $200,000 — it is impossible to know from satellite imagery. A
dilapidated house could be a liability (demolition costs). A nice barn could be worth $60,000+. You cannot
assess condition, foundation, utilities, or habitability from a bird's eye view.
Therefore: ALWAYS comp the land as if the structure does not exist. Price it as vacant land. Then flag the
structure with [!] so the investor knows to ask the seller about it.
What To Note About Structures
 That a structure exists — this is the most important thing
 What it appears to be from satellite — house, barn, shed, mobile home, warehouse
 Material if visible — metal (warehouse/barn) vs. wood (house/cabin)
 Condition if visible from Street View — maintained vs. dilapidated
 Note: 'Has structure — needs seller verification for specs and condition'
Structures On Comps — Same Rule
When a comp has a structure on it, apply the exact same logic: comp the land as vacant. Do NOT try to
mentally subtract the structure's value from the comp's sold price. Just comp the vacant land portion. The
structure on the comp is irrelevant to your vacant land valuation.
[!] NEVER trust Land Insights structure data.
Land Insights has shown '0 sqft structures' and 'Mobile Home: No' on properties where Street View clearly
showed a singlewide mobile home. Always verify with Street View (mandatory), Zillow lookup, and Google
Maps satellite. Trust your own eyes.

5. SELLER MOTIVATION SIGNALS — REPORT ALL OF THEM
Every signal of seller motivation strengthens the investor's negotiation position. Report ALL of the following
whenever found:
 Ownership 5+ years: Land has likely appreciated since purchase. Owner may not know current value.
10+ years = even stronger signal.
 Out of state/county: Owner is not using the land. Less emotional attachment. Maintaining from a
distance is a hassle.
 Family transfer (inherited): No emotional attachment — they did not buy it. Did not pay for it. Any sale
feels like free money.
 Tax delinquent: HUGE motivation signal. Always note the AMOUNT owed. Tax delinquency directly
factors into offer strategy — the investor can point out the growing tax burden and use it as leverage for a
lower offer. The more back taxes owed, the more motivated the seller.
 Low cost basis: If the owner bought for $10,000 twenty years ago, any offer feels like significant profit.
More room to negotiate.
Already Listed Properties
[!] Flag with hazard symbol if the property is already listed.
If the seller already has the property listed with a realtor, they are more knowledgeable about pricing, have
talked to a professional, and are psychologically anchored to their list price. This makes deep discounts much
harder to achieve. Flag it prominently, include the asking price in the deliverable, and still provide the full
valuation with offer ranges. Let the investor decide how to approach.
This is one of the MOST important flags in the deliverable because it directly affects whether the deal is likely to
convert. The investor needs to know this immediately.
Zoning Flags
Note the zoning code in every deliverable. Flag the following with [!] if found:
 Conservation zoning — may restrict development entirely
 Agricultural-only zoning — may restrict residential use
 Industrial zoning — different buyer pool, different market
 Commercial zoning — different buyer pool, different market
Properties Under 2 Acres
Still in DewClaw's wheelhouse. Mark as a smaller lot and note it may be more of an infill or residential lot. The
investor prefers 2+ acres but money can still be made on smaller lots. Not the primary focus, but do not skip
them.
Views / Elevation Premium
 Mediocre view: ~5% premium
 Good view: 10–15% premium
 Superman/panoramic view: 20–30% premium

[PAGE 45]
True great views are rare — and even when the property has elevation, you still need to build a road up, clear
trees, and make a building pad. Flag potential views based on contour lines and elevation in Land Insights, but
do not over-weight this factor.
New Construction Nearby
Active new construction within 1–2 miles of the subject is NOT a value-add. But flag it so the investor has
awareness of development activity in the area. It provides context about market health and demand, even if it
does not directly change the property's value.

2. LIST PRICE STRATEGY
Price 5–10% below calculated market value. This is not about hot vs. slow markets — the investor already
prices conservatively enough that market conditions do not significantly change the discount. The 5–10% range
ensures fast sales in all conditions.
Elite drone marketing + competitive pricing = dominate the market. Realtors notice well-priced, well-marketed
properties and bring their buyers. The combination of great photos and aggressive pricing creates a perception
of a mega deal for buyers, which drives 1–2 week sales.
Low DOM comps are your calibration points for list pricing. If a comp sold in 14 days at $45K, that is a realistic
fast-sale price. Work backwards from there to set your list price and offer.

3. SUBDIVISION & VALUE-ADD ANALYSIS
The Strategic Purpose
Subdivision is the primary value-add play. It is the BACKUP strategy when the seller rejects the 50% offer on
as-is value. If the property can be subdivided, the improved value may be 60–100%+ higher than as-is value.
Offering 50% of the IMPROVED value might equal the seller's asking price — the seller gets what they want,
and DewClaw still gets 50% margin on the value-add play.
Example: As-is value $100K, offer $50K, seller says no and wants $80K. Subdivision potential identified: $160K
after split. New offer: $80K (50% of $160K) = exactly what seller wanted. DewClaw still gets 50% margin.
No Minimum Acreage Floor
There is NO hard minimum for subdivision analysis. The old guideline of '15–20+ acres' does not apply as a
rigid floor. Subdivision can be as simple as a lot split — dividing one parcel into two. Here is what is worth
analyzing:

The PPA trendline tells you whether subdivision makes sense. If the curve is steep (exponential), splitting large
lots into smaller ones creates significant value. If the curve is flat (linear), splitting does not create enough value
to justify the hassle.
Check Before You Recommend
 PPA curve shape: Steep = subdivide opportunity. Flat = skip.
 Road frontage: Enough to create separate legal access for each lot? Check county LDR.
 Shape: Will splitting create bowling alley lots (ratio worse than 1:5 or 1:6)? If yes, no subdivision value.
 Wetlands: Can each resulting lot perc? If not, don't create it.
 County LDR: Check minimum lot size, minimum road frontage per lot, plat-exempt thresholds, and any
other requirements.
Bowling Alley Lots — The Shape Rule
Width-to-length ratio: Golden = 1:2 to 1:4. Maximum acceptable = 1:5 or 1:6. Anything skinnier is a 'bowling
alley lot' and loses significant value. Buyers cannot build near the road because the lot is too narrow. Forced to
go deep — long driveway, utilities run farther, building site far from road.
Before proposing ANY subdivision, check if splitting creates bowling alley lots. If yes, there is NO value-add.
Also consider whether the local market even supports long/skinny lot sales — too rural + too skinny = no buyer
pool.

4. OPERATIONAL STANDARDS & WORKFLOW
Target: 2.5 Minutes Per Property
Every property evaluation — from first glance to final deliverable — should be completed in approximately 2.5
minutes. Speed comes from discipline, not shortcuts. Follow the workflow without deviation, narration, or

MCP Tab Management
Close ALL MCP tabs after EVERY property evaluation automatically. The investor should NEVER have to close

Comp Listing Photos — Maximize Capture
For listing photos on Redfin and Zillow, capture AS MANY as possible. These are not screenshots of Land

 Slope/terrain indicators
 Cleared areas visible in photos
 Plot maps / plat maps showing layout
 Aerial photos showing property boundaries
 Structures (barns, sheds, houses, mobile homes)

More photos = more intel. Max out capture as long as it does not cause space/performance issues.
